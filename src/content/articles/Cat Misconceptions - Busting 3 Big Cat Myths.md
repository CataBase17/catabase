## Cats Always Land on Their Feet

Your cat does have a remarkable ability to right themselves in midair, but it's not foolproof. This happens through two key mechanisms: a vestibular righting reflex that acts like a compass in their head to detect which way is up, and an incredibly flexible skeleton that lets them twist their body while falling. The absence of a collarbone gives them extra rotational freedom. As they fall, cats can extend their limbs and use the flaps of skin between their legs (similar to flying squirrels) to create drag and achieve a softer landing.

However, this reflex has a catch. Kittens don't perfect their righting mechanism until around seven weeks old, and even adult cats need enough height for the reflex to kick in effectively. Falls from very low heights—like six feet or less—may not give your cat enough time to activate this mechanism. Interestingly, cats falling from greater heights sometimes sustain less severe injuries than those from lower heights, a phenomenon called high-rise syndrome, because they have more time to fully orient themselves and reduce their speed.

Several factors can affect whether your cat lands safely. The surface they're falling onto matters—hardwood floors offer less cushioning than carpet. Obstacles in their path, like bookshelves or coffee tables, can cause collisions that injure them even after a successful landing. When setting up your home, consider the height of shelving and remove hazards in potential fall paths. Remember that even if your cat can land on their feet, they can still get hurt from slipping or hitting objects after impact.

## Cats Are Nocturnal

Cats aren't actually nocturnal—they're crepuscular, meaning they're most active during dusk and dawn when light is low. This behavior developed because their natural prey, like rodents and smaller animals, are also active during these twilight hours. While cats do have excellent low-light vision, they cannot see in complete darkness. Their best hunting opportunities come during dusk and dawn, not the middle of the night.

The misconception that cats are nocturnal often develops because domesticated cats seem more active at night. This usually happens because your cat sleeps during the day while you're away, then tries to match your energy when you come home in the evening. To help your cat develop a proper circadian rhythm that aligns with your schedule, establish a routine that includes regular feeding times, play sessions, and consistent attention. Think of these as the "three R's": routine, ritual, and rhythm. Feral cats, which rely on natural instincts to survive, tend to stay crepuscular rather than shifting to purely nocturnal habits.

## Cats Don't Show Emotions

Your cat doesn't openly display their feelings in obvious ways, which makes it genuinely challenging to read their emotional state. Throughout history, humans have misunderstood cats because of this reserved nature. Ancient Egyptians worshiped them, while later cultures blamed them for disasters—cats were even scapegoated for the black plague, which was actually spread by rats and mice. This extreme swinging between worship and revulsion shows how little humans have historically understood about cats. Building a deeper understanding of their behavior and communication helps bridge this gap.

## Cats Are Not Solitary Creatures

Cats get labeled as solitary and aloof, but this is a misunderstanding of how they actually live. While it's true that cats are solitary hunters, they're not solitary animals overall. Feral cats, for instance, live in colonies, which clearly contradicts the idea that they're anti-social. Many cat behaviors that seem unfriendly—not coming when called or avoiding cuddling—get interpreted as rejection when really, your cat is just showing affection in their own way.

Kittens are born and raised with their mothers and siblings until at least 16 weeks of age, showing they have natural social bonds. Your cat's behavior isn't a sign they don't care about you; it's often that humans project their own expectations onto cats instead of understanding feline nature. Dogs show affection through enthusiasm and responsiveness in ways humans easily recognize, and it's easy to mistake a cat's different style of interaction for indifference. When you learn to understand your cat's natural behavior and unique personality rather than expecting them to act like a dog, you'll discover they have a genuine social nature. The more you understand cats, the less likely you are to see them as solitary or non-social, and the stronger your relationship with them becomes.
