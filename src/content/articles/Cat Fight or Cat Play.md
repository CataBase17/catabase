## How to Tell If Your Cats Are Fighting or Playing

If you have two cats and you're worried they're fighting, you're not alone in your concern. Many cat owners find it hard to know whether their cats are playing or actually battling each other. The good news is that most of the time when owners think their cats are fighting, something else is actually going on—about 80% of the time, it turns out to be a different issue entirely.

## Reading Your Cats' Body Language

Pay close attention to what your cats' bodies are telling you. When one cat has flattened ears plastered to their head and is making themselves small—pressing their face down low—they're signaling self-preservation. This isn't playfulness; your cat is preparing for a real conflict. When you see this kind of posturing, especially combined with one cat returning to provoke another after you've already broken up an encounter, you're likely looking at genuine aggression rather than play.

## Use "Cat Detective" Observation

To understand what's really happening between your cats, become a careful observer. Track where and when conflicts occur by keeping notes or even marking the spots with tape on the floor. This detective work helps you spot patterns. Are the fights happening in the same location? At certain times of day? Around particular furniture or resources? These details matter, because your cats may be arguing over something specific—like access to a favorite cat tree or other furniture.

## Address Resource Competition

Once you've identified patterns, the next step is often to provide more of what your cats are competing over. If they're fighting near cat furniture, add more pieces so both cats have access without feeling threatened. This ties into the concept of "catification"—creating an environment with multiple traffic lanes, escape routes, and levels that allow each cat to move around freely without getting cornered or feeling trapped.

Think about vertical space especially. Adding shelves or repositioning furniture to create multiple routes at different heights gives a stressed cat like Olivia a way to escape without having to jump down and face confrontation. The more options your cats have to avoid each other or move through your home, the less likely tension will build.

## Make Sure Your Cat Gets Enough Play and Exercise

Sometimes what looks like fighting is actually a cat with pent-up energy trying to engage another cat as a playmate. If one of your cats is very active and the other isn't interested in that kind of interaction, you need to redirect that energy. Spend dedicated time playing with the more energetic cat using toys, wand toys, or other interactive play sessions. A well-exercised cat is far less likely to pounce on their housemate out of boredom or frustration.

## Take Visible Stress Seriously

If one of your cats appears very stressed by these encounters—hiding, trembling, or showing other signs of anxiety—don't wait to address it. The conflicts are clearly affecting their wellbeing, and you need to make changes to your environment and routine to help them feel safe again.
