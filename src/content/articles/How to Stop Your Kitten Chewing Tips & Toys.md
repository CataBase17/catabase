## Understanding Kitten Teething

Your young kitten's chewing behavior is completely normal—it's part of the teething process. Between two and nine weeks old, kittens' baby teeth come in, and by around 12 weeks, those baby teeth start falling out to make room for adult teeth. If you have a larger breed like a Maine Coon, the teething process may take longer than it does for other cats.

## Redirecting Chewing to Appropriate Toys

The key to managing chewing is teaching your kitten the difference between what's acceptable to chew and what isn't. When your kitten chews on hands, feet, plastic, or other inappropriate items, let them know this behavior isn't acceptable—a high-pitched sound can work well for this. Immediately redirect them to a positive alternative, like a toy that's safe for them to chew on.

If your kitten has been focusing on stick toys, try switching to toys with fuzzy or feather attachments instead. When your kitten starts chewing on an inappropriate part of any toy, take it away and replace it with an acceptable alternative like a feather toy. The goal is to consistently redirect them toward good chewing behavior so they learn what gives them satisfaction and what doesn't.

## Safe Toys for Teething Kittens

Several types of toys work well for teething kittens. Look for toys made from sisal fabric, hard rubber toys that are indestructible, or dedicated teething toys. Kickers are another excellent option—they help your kitten release play frustration and are safe for them to bite and wrestle with.

Raw meaty bones can also help. Chicken necks or chicken wings are good options that help clean teeth and provide natural teething relief. However, never give your kitten cooked bones, as they can splinter and cause injury. Always supervise your kitten when they're working on raw bones or any other toys, since young kittens may make decisions that could harm them, like trying to swallow something they shouldn't.

## Supervision and Storage

Put toys away when they're not in use, and supervise your kitten during playtime to prevent them from ingesting undigestible objects. When you get out the toy cabinet, your kitten will start to associate that sound with playtime, making the time you spend together more rewarding and enjoyable.

## Building Good Habits

By providing your kitten with suitable alternatives and consistently teaching them what's appropriate, you're helping them develop good habits. During the first six months of teething, every time you say "no" to unwanted chewing, you should offer a "yes" alternative. By the time your kitten finishes teething, they should understand what's off-limits—like arms, hands, fingers, and toes—and what gives them the satisfaction they're looking for.
