## Knocking Things Off Shelves

When your cat knocks items off shelves, it's often a sign of boredom rather than spite. This behavior is especially common in kittens, who have lots of natural energy and curiosity. The good news is that playing with your cat can be a very effective solution. Interactive play with a toy like a wand or board helps satisfy their need for stimulation and activity, which reduces the urge to create their own entertainment by knocking things around.

## Zoomies

The sudden bursts of frantic energy your cat displays—often called "zoomies"—are a normal part of feline behavior. Playing with your cat is essential to help drain this energy. If zoomies happen at night when you're trying to sleep, establishing a consistent routine can help. Creating a structured rhythm with regular meal times and dedicated playtime sessions helps your cat settle into a routine that aligns with your schedule. A consistent routine invested over time can help reduce or eliminate nighttime zoomies and allow your cat to adapt to your sleep patterns.

## Ankle Biting and Play Aggression

When your cat pounces on and bites your ankles, it's a form of play aggression. Your moving feet and ankles trigger their hunting instincts—they see them as prey, similar to how they'd chase a squirrel. This behavior is most common when cats aren't getting enough constructive playtime. Without adequate interactive play, cats redirect their energy toward your feet and legs. Providing regular, dedicated playtime with interactive toys helps your cat expend their energy and satisfy their natural hunting instincts, which significantly reduces this behavior.

## Eating Plants

If your cat is eating, destroying, or digging in your houseplants, your first priority should be ensuring none of them are toxic to cats—there's a long list of plants that can harm your pet. To prevent plant damage, hang plants out of reach when possible. For plants that must stay on shelves or tables, you can make them less appealing by covering the soil with lava rocks (lightweight pebbles with sharp sides that cats dislike digging in) or using sticky deterrent products like Sticky Paws, which have an unpleasant texture that discourages cats from accessing the plants.

You can also redirect this natural behavior by providing your cat with an acceptable alternative: a flat of wheatgrass, oat grass, or barley grass. This gives them an outlet for their natural instinct to root around and chew on plants.

## Urinating or Defecating Outside the Litter Box

When a cat starts eliminating outside the box, your first step should always be a veterinary visit. Even if the behavior doesn't seem like a big deal, a complete checkup is essential. If your cat is urinating outside the box, ask your vet to do a urinalysis. If your cat is pooping outside the box, bring a fecal sample to rule out any underlying health problems.

Once medical issues are ruled out, think about what changes have happened in your household. Has your work schedule changed, leaving your cat alone for longer periods? Have new cats been introduced, reducing your cat's territory? Are there feral cats in your neighborhood, which might trigger your cat to mark its territory on curtains, doors, or other perimeter areas? Your cat might also be experiencing separation anxiety.

It's important to approach this with empathy. The behavior isn't normal, but it's also not demonic—your cat is communicating that something is wrong, whether it's physical pain, anxiety, or a combination of both.

## Destructive Scratching

Scratching is a completely normal behavior. Your cat needs to remove dead sheaths from their nails and mark their territory with both visual scratches and scent glands in their paws. To manage destructive scratching, apply a two-part approach: "no" to unwanted areas and "yes" to acceptable ones.

For the "no" areas, use double-sided sticky tape on couches, chairs, and other furniture your cat is scratching. For the "yes" areas, provide alternative scratching surfaces positioned near where your cat is currently scratching. The location and sturdiness of the alternative are crucial—your cat needs to feel the scratching post or pad is sturdy enough to lean into. Some cats prefer vertical surfaces like scratching posts, while others prefer horizontal surfaces like floor-based scratchers. Observe your cat's preference and provide an appropriate alternative.

Never consider declawing as a solution. It causes significant distress and often leads to other behavioral problems like biting. Instead, focus on redirecting the behavior to appropriate scratching surfaces and making unwanted areas less appealing.

## General Approach to Behavior Issues

Two key principles address most behavior problems: play with your cat regularly to combat boredom, and establish a consistent routine and rhythm for meals and playtime. These fundamentals contribute to your cat's overall wellness and significantly reduce unwanted behaviors.
