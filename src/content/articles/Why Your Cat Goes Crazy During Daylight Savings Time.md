Your cat's internal clock is deeply tied to the position of the sun. Cats are naturally programmed to hunt during dusk and dawn, and their circadian rhythm—the internal system that regulates sleep, activity, and behavior—is strongly influenced by daylight. When daylight savings time arrives and the clocks shift, your cat's sense of timing gets disrupted because the sun is now rising and setting at different times than before.

## Understanding Your Cat's Natural Schedule

Cats are routine-oriented animals. They follow the sun throughout the day and adjust their activities based on its position in the sky. In addition to responding to natural light, your cat also learns to expect feeding times, playtime, and other interactions based on your daily schedule. This means their circadian rhythm isn't controlled entirely by nature—you influence it through the consistent routines you establish with meals, play, and attention.

Your cat sleeps around 10 to 12 hours a day and spends much of the rest observing their surroundings. They often look out windows and follow the sun as it moves across your home, creating what's essentially a personal sundial based on light and shadow.

## What Happens During the Time Change

When the clocks change, your cat can become confused about when things should happen. You might notice them demanding food or attention at times that seem unusual to you—but to your cat, they're right on schedule according to the sun's position. Don't give in to these demands for off-schedule meals or extra attention, even though it can be tempting.

## Helping Your Cat Adjust

The best way to help your cat transition is to stick to your regular routine. Keep feeding times consistent, maintain your usual playtime schedule, and continue any other daily interactions you have with your cat. Consistency is what helps your cat feel secure and eventually re-align their internal clock.

Exposure to natural sunlight is also important. Make sure your cat has access to windows where they can observe the outdoors and experience the natural light cycle. Consider providing a comfortable spot with good sun exposure—a windowsill or cat bed near a window works well. You can also use scent soakers or items that carry familiar scents near these sunny spots to make them more appealing resting areas.

By maintaining your established routine and providing your cat with plenty of natural light and environmental enrichment, you'll help them adjust to daylight savings time with minimal disruption to their behavior and yours.
