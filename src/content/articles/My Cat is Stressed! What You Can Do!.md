## Understanding Stress in Cats

Cats are naturally inclined to hide their pain—whether physical, emotional, or mental. They often show stress only through subtle changes in behavior that you might notice if you're paying close attention. If your cat exhibits unusual behavior, the first step is to rule out any underlying medical issues by taking them to the vet, since pain can cause changes in behavior. This is the golden rule: if your cat is doing something they have never done before, a vet check-up is essential to ensure they're not acting out due to pain.

## Physical Signs of Stress

Stress can manifest physically in several ways. Your cat might over-groom, especially in areas like the belly or between the legs, which can indicate stress or allergies. Excessive vomiting or diarrhea can be exacerbated by stress in the home, and stress can also play a role in feline lower urinary tract disorders and irritable bowel syndrome—digestive issues that can't be easily explained by other causes.

Some cats eat too much or too fast when stressed and then throw up, or they may avoid food altogether. Another sign is displacement behavior, which is like nail-biting in humans. You might notice your cat engaging in sudden and repeated grooming that's more intense and determined than their normal languid grooming.

## Territorial Stress

Territorial stress occurs when something threatens your cat's sense of ownership and control over their environment. External threats like feral cats feeding near your home, wildlife such as raccoons or skunks, or even intact cats during kitten season can trigger this type of stress.

When your cat feels threatened from outside, they may mark their territory by peeing around doors and windows to warn other animals to stay away. Some cats are more prone to territorial behavior than others—certain personalities tend to over-own things. If your cat has never shown this behavior before, check what's happening outside your windows and doors first.

Your cat might also start hiding to avoid the perceived threat, staying away from windows and retreating under the bed or into closets. They may become aggressive or fearful when you approach them, even if they're just sitting in a window. This behavior isn't personal—it's a reflection of their stress and anxiety.

Moving to a new home is a significant stressor because cats define their safety and security through ownership of their territory. When the territory changes, they may exhibit stress behaviors like over-ownership of space, scratching on furniture, or peeing on cushions. You might notice your cat scratching, rubbing, or headbutting areas like the arm of a couch to claim ownership. During times of stress, this behavior can escalate to destructive levels.

To reduce territorial stress, keep the animals that trigger it away from your home. For example, move feral cat feeding stations far away from your house.

## Relational Stress

Relational stress comes from changes in your cat's relationships with you and other household members.

**Separation anxiety** occurs when you spend more time away from home after a period of being together, such as when you return to work after an extended time at home. This can lead to stress-related behaviors like peeing or pooping on your bed.

**New animal or human family members** can cause significant relational stress. The introduction of a new pet or person—a new partner, spouse, or even a baby—can trigger aggression, litter box avoidance, vomiting, or diarrhea. It's crucial to introduce new members slowly and carefully rather than suddenly. Your cat may feel insecure about a new person taking up territory, leading to stress-related behaviors.

**Multi-cat households** require careful resource management. Not providing enough territorial resources like cat trees, vertical stations, and separate areas for each cat can lead to stress and fighting. Providing adequate resources helps create a more harmonious environment.

**Dogs invading a cat's space** can cause significant stress, especially if a dog has access to the cat's litter box. Train your dog to respect your cat's boundaries and provide a safe, stress-free environment for both.

## Managing and Reducing Stress

When you notice unusual behavior in your cat, step back from the situation and view it objectively rather than reacting emotionally. Remember that the cat is suffering and needs your guidance. The territorial and relational manifestations of stress are interconnected and can lead to various behaviors such as hiding, aggression, or health issues. Understanding this connection is crucial for addressing the problem.

Playing with your cat is an effective way to release energy and reduce stress. Establishing a structured play routine can help bring your cat's energy levels back down.

Following the "three R's"—routine, ritual, and rhythm—can help alleviate stress significantly. Cats are highly attuned to rhythm and routine, and a structured schedule provides a sense of stability and familiarity. By adopting an observer's perspective and implementing these strategies, you can help reduce your cat's stress levels and create a more harmonious environment for your entire family.
