## Start with a Veterinary Checkup

If your cat is peeing or pooping outside the litter box, your first step should always be a veterinary visit. Ask your vet to run a urinalysis and full blood panel to rule out any underlying medical issues. This is critical because some health problems that cause litter box avoidance are easily treatable once identified. A cat with an abscessed tooth, for example, might avoid the litter box as a sign of pain, and without proper diagnosis, behavioral problems could escalate dangerously. Medical issues can masquerade as pure behavior problems, so a thorough checkup protects your cat's health and gives you a clear starting point for solving the problem.

## Approach the Problem Calmly and Objectively

Once medical issues have been ruled out, the most important thing you can do is step back emotionally. This behavior is solvable, but it requires detective work rather than frustration. Don't dwell on the mess or take the behavior personally. Instead, approach this like a journalist investigating a story: stay calm, gather facts, and remain emotionally detached so you can think clearly. How you respond to the problem matters tremendously. Emotional reactions can lead to serious consequences, so commit to investigating the cause with a rational mind.

## Start Journaling to Identify Patterns

Begin by writing down when accidents happen and what was different about that day or the time period leading up to it. Rather than reacting emotionally, journaling helps you step back and objectively gather information. Record the whats, wheres, and hows of each incident—this will help you identify patterns you might otherwise miss. Most importantly, pinpoint when this behavior started. Was it recent, or has it been happening for a while? Then think carefully about what changed in your home around that time.

Major life events often trigger litter box avoidance—a new roommate, separation or divorce, the birth of a baby, or the addition of a new pet. But smaller changes count too: a new job, a job loss, or a shift in how much time you spend at home. Your cat may also react to less obvious stressors in much the same way you might need to cool down after a frustrating day. If you can pinpoint the approximate time of an incident, back up an hour from that moment and think about what might have happened to stress your cat.

## Map the Accidents to Reveal What's Really Happening

Create what you might call an "anti-treasure map" to visualize patterns. Use painter's tape to mark an X on the floor every time your cat has an accident outside the box. Do this for about a week. Nothing about this behavior is random—patterns will emerge. After a week of X's on your floor, you'll start to see where your cat is choosing to go. A black light can also help you discover the full extent of the problem if accidents aren't always visible. These tools give you a clear picture of what's happening and help pinpoint whether your cat's issue stems from something making them feel insecure or threatened.

## Understand What the Location Tells You

The location of accidents reveals different underlying problems, so pay close attention to where the X's cluster on your map.

If your cat is peeing in a circle around the litter box itself, they're likely trying to protect their territory or create a boundary because something is making them uncomfortable. This could be pain, stress, or the presence of another animal like a dog near the box. When your cat marks right outside the litter box, they may be signaling that something is preventing them from using it—an environmental issue you need to address.

If your cat is peeing around the perimeter of your home—creating what you might think of as a moat around their castle—they're likely responding to external threats. Neighborhood cats or feral cats outside may be triggering territorial marking behavior. Cats typically engage in perimeter marking near front doors, back doors, or under windows to protect their territory from perceived threats. In this case, investigate your yard and identify what outside stressors might be causing your cat to feel the need to defend their territory.

## Reduce Stress to Solve the Problem

Once you've identified the pattern and potential cause, the key to resolving the problem is reducing stress and tension in your home. Bringing down the overall "temperature" of the environment helps both your cat and you feel calmer and safer. Address the actual stressor your cat has identified rather than making assumptions about what's bothering them. Don't blame your cat's behavior on personal relationships—thinking your cat hates a particular family member will only increase your stress and won't help solve the problem. Stay objective, stay calm, and focus on the environmental or behavioral change that will actually help your cat feel secure enough to use the litter box again.
