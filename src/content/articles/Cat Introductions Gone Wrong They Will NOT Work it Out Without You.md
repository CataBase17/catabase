## How to Introduce Cats to Each Other Properly

If you have two cats that aren't getting along, the problem likely lies in how you introduced them in the first place. When one cat is trying to hunt or bite the other, and the newcomer is hiding, it's a sign that the introduction process needs to restart. Let's walk through what's going wrong and how to fix it.

## Why Your Current Approach Isn't Working

The most common mistake is opening the door and expecting cats to figure it out on their own. When you do this, the more assertive cat ends up guarding the door, blocking the other cat's exit route. If your cat runs under the bed or hides, she has nowhere to escape and feels trapped. This creates fear and tension, not a successful introduction. Expecting them to play together or be distracted by toys after a failed introduction won't happen either—you need to nail the introduction process first.

## Start with Separate Play Sessions

Begin by letting each cat have the entire house to themselves while the other stays in a separate room. Let Tosha run around all the rooms and burn energy while Marusa is closed off. Then switch: give Marusa access to the whole house while Tosha is separated. This gives each cat a chance to explore, build confidence, and get comfortable without feeling like she has to compete or defend herself.

During these separate sessions, play with each cat individually to learn what motivates her—whether that's specific toys or treats. This knowledge will become crucial later when you bring them together.

## Use Sight Swapping to Build Familiarity

Sight swapping means regularly switching the cats' locations so they experience each other's scents and sounds without direct contact. If your flat has multiple rooms, you have enough space to do this effectively. This gradual process helps each cat recognize that they share the home without the stress of face-to-face meetings.

Keep things consistent day to day. Maintain regular meal times, regular door meetings, and regular play sessions so your cats begin to expect and anticipate what comes next. This consistency helps them feel more secure.

## The Right Time to Let Them Meet

Your cats are ready for closer interaction when they stop showing extreme interest in the door—no more diving at it or trying to break through. Instead, they should be calm enough to eat their meals normally near the closed door. At this point, they're showing less frustration and more acceptance that the other cat exists.

When you do start controlled meetings, use high-value items that matter more to each cat than the presence of the other. Jackpot treats or their favorite toys can help redirect their focus. The goal is to get both cats interested in something other than each other, so they can be in the same space without tension.

## What to Expect

The situation may not be as dire as it feels right now. It's possible for cats to recognize they live together without being terrified of each other or fighting constantly. With two young cats—one fearful and one assertive—you can guide them toward the middle through patience and proper technique.

Remember: introducing a new cat (or any major change like a new person, baby, or piece of furniture) disrupts your cat's sense of territorial security, which is essential to her well-being. Gradual, unthreatening introductions protect that feeling of safety. Don't rush the process by simply hoping they'll work it out. With time and the right approach, your cats can learn to coexist peacefully.
