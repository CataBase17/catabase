## Understanding Suckling Behavior in Cats

Your senior cat may have a suckling habit that seems odd or annoying—chewing, licking, or sucking on blankets, clothing, furniture, or other household items whenever she sits with you. This behavior is surprisingly common, especially in older cats, and there are practical ways to manage it.

## Is Your Cat Just Suckling or Does She Have Pica?

The first thing to determine is whether your cat is simply suckling on items or actually ingesting them. This distinction matters because it affects how concerned you should be.

Pica is the technical term for eating non-organic materials—things that aren't food. Cats with pica may chew and swallow sweaters, plastic, knobs off dressers, or even crown molding off walls. They continuously ingest these objects, which can be dangerous. Ingested materials like yarn are particularly risky because they can wrap around the intestinal tract and cause serious health problems.

If your cat is only suckling and not swallowing, the risk is much lower, though wet spots on your belongings may still be an issue.

## Why Cats Suckle

Suckling behavior often stems from early weaning. If a kitten is separated from her mother too soon, she may continue this self-soothing behavior into adulthood. It's similar to thumb-sucking in humans—a comforting habit that can persist for life. In an older cat, this behavior is deeply ingrained and has likely been part of her personality for many years.

Changing an established behavior in a senior cat is difficult and may not be worth the stress and effort. At 21 years old, your cat has had this habit for a long time, and attempting to train it away could cause more frustration than it prevents.

## Managing the Behavior Through Your Environment

Rather than trying to change your cat's behavior, the practical approach is to change her environment. Make your home less appealing for suckling by removing or securing the items she targets most.

If your cat loves cable knit sweaters, store them away or keep them in sealed containers. Put throw rugs where she can't easily access them. Secure blankets or cover them with materials she doesn't enjoy. The goal is to make it harder for her to find her preferred suckling items, so she has fewer opportunities to engage in the behavior.

If your cat is only suckling and not eating these items, managing the problem becomes simpler—even if she does leave wet spots on things. Focus on protecting the items that matter most to you while accepting that this is part of who your cat is.
