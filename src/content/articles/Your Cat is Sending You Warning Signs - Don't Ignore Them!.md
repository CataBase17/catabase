## Understanding What Your Cat's Behavior Is Telling You

Cats often exhibit behaviors that concern their owners, and understanding what these behaviors mean is crucial. Many of these actions are your cat's way of communicating that something is wrong—whether that's pain, discomfort, or distress. The key is recognizing that behavioral and physical issues in cats are deeply intertwined. Some signs require immediate veterinary attention: squatting without peeing, bloody urine, constant diarrhea, hiding all the time, or not eating for over 24 hours. If you notice any of these alarming signs, stop and contact your vet right away, as they can indicate serious health issues.

## Litter Box Behavior

Changes in your cat's litter box habits can be a window into their physical health. If your cat is missing the litter box, avoiding it, peeing all over the place, or sitting in the box but producing very little, these may signal a physical problem. Before assuming it's a behavioral issue related to territorial insecurity or household changes, it's essential to get a clean bill of health from your vet first. Rule out urinary tract problems and gastrointestinal issues.

Your cat might also avoid the litter box due to pain or discomfort while squatting—this could stem from joint pain. Pay close attention to whether your cat seems to be in discomfort during or after using the box.

If you use an automatic litter box, be aware that it makes monitoring your cat's health much harder. You won't be able to spot blood in urine or feces, and you can't collect a fecal sample to bring to your vet. Switching to a traditional litter box with a scoop allows you to stay on top of your cat's day-to-day physical health and catch potential issues early.

## Joint Pain and Arthritis

Joint pain, including arthritis, can affect any of your cat's joints—their spine, tail, legs, and more. If your cat hasn't jumped up on the couch or other high surfaces for a while, this might signal joint pain. Consider whether jumping down is also causing them pain; sometimes cats avoid these activities for both reasons.

Middle-aged and senior cats especially may show less interest in playing or toys—this can be a sign of joint pain rather than simple loss of interest. Additionally, if your cat is having difficulty cleaning themselves, you may notice dander buildup or greasy fur, particularly on their back. This happens because it hurts them to turn and move their body. This can easily be mistaken for a skin issue, so it's important to consider arthritis and other joint-related problems when assessing your cat.

## Skin Issues

Skin problems in cats can stem from multiple causes. Barbering—when your cat pulls their hair out—is often a stress response, similar to how humans bite their nails when anxious. However, barbering can also be caused by food sensitivity, food allergies, or environmental allergies, all of which lead to itching or pain that prompts hair pulling.

You might also notice extra dander on your cat. Skin issues can be due to vitamin deficiency, stress, or itchiness. It's essential to consult your vet or an animal dermatologist to determine the underlying cause so you can provide appropriate treatment.

## Dental Problems and Bad Breath

Unusually bad breath in your cat can be a sign of serious underlying issues. Rotting teeth, abscesses, or gastrointestinal problems can all cause foul odor. If the problem is gut-related, it might be connected to food sensitivity or your cat's need for probiotics.

Physical pain—including dental pain—can dramatically change your cat's behavior and temperament. One shelter cat that was hyper-aggressive and nearly euthanized turned out to have severe dental disease with rotting and abscess-laden teeth. After tooth extractions, the cat's behavior improved significantly and became calm. This demonstrates how profoundly physical pain affects an animal's character and behavior. By addressing physical issues like dental problems or gut health, you can improve both your cat's emotional state and their behavioral well-being.

## Blood in Urine or Feces

If you notice blood in your cat's urine or feces, several things could be responsible. Stress can lead to urinary tract infections and bloody urine. Parasitic infections like worms or coccidia can also cause bloody feces. Additionally, if your cat's food is too dry, it may cause straining during bowel movements, which can burst blood vessels and cause bleeding.

To address this issue, you'll want to reduce your cat's stress levels, but it's crucial to consult your vet for a proper diagnosis and treatment plan.

## Hiding and Social Avoidance

If your cat is hiding in closets, under beds, or behind couches more than usual, or if their sleep patterns have changed, they're signaling that something is wrong. Cats hide when they don't feel well, just as they would in the wild to avoid predators when vulnerable. They're avoiding people or animals and aren't as present as they used to be.

When you notice this behavior, think about what changes may have occurred in your household that could be making your cat feel unsafe or uncomfortable in their territory. Any form of discomfort or pain will cause significant anxiety and hiding. If your cat doesn't feel secure or "on top of their game," they'll retreat to safety.

## Not Eating

If your cat stops eating for a few days, this is a medical emergency. Cats that don't eat for several days are in danger of developing hepatic lipidosis, also known as fatty liver disease, which can be fatal if left untreated. Don't wait to identify potential behavioral reasons—visit your vet immediately. Time is critical when a cat stops eating.

## Eating or Drinking Excessively

If your cat is eating or drinking much more than usual, this is unusual behavior that warrants a vet visit. Excessive drinking can indicate the onset of renal failure or an endocrine problem. Excessive eating may signal diabetes or malnutrition. In some cases, cats may eat non-food items like dirt due to a deficiency, such as an iron deficiency, as they attempt to fulfill their body's needs.

Your cat may also overgraze on grass and then vomit, which could be their way of trying to clear their gut of an issue. These behaviors come with a big red flag attached—they're your cat's way of telling you something needs attention.

## Excessive Vocalization (Yowling)

If your cat is yowling or howling more than usual, especially if this is new behavior, something may be amiss. In older cats, hyper-vocalizing can result from deafness, causing them to vocalize more loudly. Cognitive decline and dementia can also lead to increased vocalization as your cat experiences feelings of vulnerability.

Joint problems can also cause yowling. Try gently touching your cat's back and observing their reaction—screaming or yowling in response indicates a potential issue worth discussing with your vet.

## Being Mindful of Changes

The key to catching problems early is mindfulness. Pay attention when something changes about your cat—like when they haven't jumped on the couch, or when they're hiding more than usual. These small observations prompt you to figure out why.

A holistic approach to understanding your cat involves considering both medical and behavioral factors. Don't rely solely on your vet or a behavior specialist—use your own knowledge of your cat combined with mindfulness to recognize potential issues. When you notice something different, assume there may be a problem and work together with your vet and other resources to resolve it.

Above all, show love and care for your cat and prioritize their well-being. Small acts of attention and affection, paired with prompt veterinary care when needed, make a real difference in your cat's health and happiness.
