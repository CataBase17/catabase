## Veterinary Care

As your senior cat ages, changes can happen quickly. Watch for signs like stiffness, decreased appetite, or increased thirst, and contact your vet to rule out physical problems. Regular yearly exams are important for senior cats, though twice-yearly visits may be necessary if your cat has advanced issues like kidney disease, thyroid problems, diabetes, or arthritis.

Beyond traditional veterinary care, consider integrative approaches or alternative therapies. Acupuncture, Tellington Touch, and cold laser therapy can help alleviate symptoms and improve your senior cat's overall well-being. These options can work alongside regular vet care to help your cat manage age-related issues and maintain quality of life.

## Nutrition and Hydration

Your senior cat is an obligate carnivore and still needs a diet rich in meat, regardless of age. The best options are a raw diet, followed by grain-free wet food, then wet food, with dry food being the least ideal. The key is keeping water flowing through your cat's system to maintain kidney function and hydration.

Cats naturally get 70 to 75 percent of their hydration through their food, so add warm water to wet food to make it more palatable and ensure adequate water intake. Place multiple water sources around your home—fountains and bowls work well—to encourage your senior cat to stay hydrated and reduce the risk of urinary tract infections.

## Making Your Home Senior-Friendly

Your senior cat's bones and joints become creaky with age, making jumping difficult. Replace tall cat trees with ramps to help them reach their favorite vertical spots. A simple solution is a plank of wood with carpeting attached. This same thinking applies to getting onto furniture like the back of a couch.

Getting in and out of the litter box becomes challenging for senior cats, especially boxes with high fronts or lids. Switch to a low-sided litter box—those designed for puppies work well. If your cat has accidents outside the box, try a different type of litter and add a new box rather than replacing the old one, so your cat still has familiar options.

Senior cats often become heat seekers as they age. Provide warm, comfortable beds with mylar layers or low-voltage heated beds. These small touches can greatly improve their comfort.

## Helping Your Senior Cat's Changing Senses

As your cat ages, their eyesight and hearing may decline, sometimes quickly. Losing any sense can cause stress, particularly in a multi-cat home. To check if your cat's hearing is declining, try the "snap test"—walk behind your cat and snap your fingers to see if their ears turn in response.

If your cat's eyesight is fading, keep lights on at night to help them navigate. Watch how your cat interacts with its territory and notice any small changes, adapting as needed.

Dementia is common in senior cats and may show up as disorientation or howling at night when lights turn off. Provide low ambient light using LED strip lights or night lights to help your cat feel more secure. If your cat seems distressed in darkness, you might bring them into your bedroom or create a quiet, softly lit space. Running lights across the floor can also help ease their anxiety.

## Play and Mental Stimulation

Your senior cat may not play like a kitten, but they still need outlets for physical and mental activity. This doesn't mean intense play—even simple movements or gentle swiping at a toy help keep them young and their joints mobile.

Food puzzle toys are excellent for senior cats that don't move around much. They challenge your cat mentally and physically as they work to extract treats, keeping both mind and body engaged. Some puzzle toys are static, requiring minimal movement while still providing mental stimulation.

Another low-key option is "cat TV"—setting up a window where your cat can watch birds or other animals outside. This keeps their hunting instincts sharp and provides engaging entertainment regardless of age.

## Caring for Your Senior Cat Long-Term

Keep your senior cat happy by challenging them and not treating them as fragile just because they're older. Instead, practice observational and adaptive love by keeping a close eye on them. Changes can happen quickly—watch for shifts in drinking, eating, or activity levels like spending an entire day in bed.

If your cat misses the litter box or suddenly stops using it, contact your vet right away and adapt their environment to support their needs. With proper care, cats can live well into their late teens and even into their twenties. The average cat now lives around 16 years, and some reach 26 years or more.

Consider adopting a senior cat. They bring tremendous love and companionship and often spend longer in shelters than younger cats. Senior cats adapt well to new homes and can be incredibly grateful for the care they receive. Giving a senior cat a home greatly improves their life and creates a unique, rewarding bond between you and your companion.
