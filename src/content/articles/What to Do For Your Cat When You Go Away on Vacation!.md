## Understanding Your Cat's Needs

When you go on vacation or travel, your cat relies on you to maintain the things that keep them happy and confident. Cats thrive on routine, ritual, and a strong sense of rhythm in their daily lives. This circadian rhythm is crucial for their confidence and well-being. Beyond schedule, your cat needs to feel confident ownership of their territory—their home and the familiar smells that make them feel secure. They also rely on their relationship with you. Together, these three elements make up what gives your cat their "mojo." Without them, cats can suffer and lose confidence.

When you're planning time away, think about how to preserve these three key elements for your cat: routine, territorial confidence, and the relationship you've built with them.

## What Not to Do

Leaving your cat alone for 24 or 48 hours with a trough of food and a water fountain is not suitable, even if you assume they can manage without litter box scooping. Your cat needs active care and attention during your absence.

Having someone stop in for just 20 minutes a day isn't ideal either. A brief visit interrupts your cat's sense of territorial confidence and doesn't provide the continuity they need. It's not a soft landing for them.

Taking your cat to a boarding facility or someone else's house is often destructive for them. Removing them from their home turf and familiar smells can lead to anxious behavior. Even facilities that try to create a "home away from home" experience aren't the same as staying in their actual home. However, in genuine emergencies—like a gas leak—boarding may be necessary.

## Better Options: Pet Sitters

Hiring a pet sitter is a solid choice. A good pet sitter provides care in the comfort of your cat's own home. Look for pet sitters through websites that require their members to be licensed, bonded, and insured. You can also ask your veterinarian, groomer, or trusted friends and family for referrals. Once you've found candidates, interview them and have them meet your cats to ensure a good fit.

A pet sitter can visit your cat twice a day—morning and evening—to maintain their routine and provide food, play, and attention. This helps keep your cat's natural rhythm and energy levels stable. Pet sitters typically charge based on visit length, and you can customize their schedule and services to fit your cat's needs and your budget if you plan in advance.

If hiring a professional pet sitter isn't feasible due to cost, ask a trusted friend or family member to care for your cat. The key is to clearly communicate your cat's needs and the responsibilities involved in caring for them. Whatever option you choose, the goal is maintaining the three R's—relationship, rhythm, and territorial confidence—which provides stability and familiarity and helps reduce stress.

## The Ideal Approach

The best arrangement combines two strategies: have a pet sitter visit twice a day, and also have them stay overnight. An overnight stay can be arranged in 12-hour blocks, such as 7 p.m. to 7 a.m., which covers dinner, bedtime, and breakfast. This approach inserts all three essential elements—territorial confidence, relationship, and routine—into one comprehensive 12-hour period. The cost is often built into a pet sitter's price structure, so it may not be as expensive as you'd expect.

## Setting Up Your Pet Sitter for Success

Create a detailed guide or manual for your pet sitter that includes emergency phone numbers, contact persons for specific situations, and your cats' specific care instructions. This helps your sitter know exactly what to expect and how to care for your cats—including the rituals and routines that make them feel exercised and relaxed.

Place signs and reminders around your house to help your sitter remember important tasks, like locking certain doors or preventing your cats from escaping. This reduces your stress while you're away.

Do a "handoff" exercise before you leave. Have your pet sitter come over one to three times for a meal and hangout session with your cats. This allows your cats to become familiar with the sitter's presence and build trust. During these visits, demonstrate how to care for your cats—like how you feed them. Your cats will start to associate the sitter with positive experiences, which reduces their stress when you're actually gone. The sitter can gradually take on more responsibilities, like feeding and playing with the cats, to build their comfort and trust.

## Supporting Your Pet Sitter

Set up your home to keep your cat engaged while your sitter is there. Provide self-activated toys, puzzle toys, and "Cat TV"—like birdhouses outside the window that your cat can watch. Place cat trees and beds near windows, as windows are a great source of entertainment.

Create a "Santa Claus stash" of extra treats and toys for your sitter to use—things like churu tube treats and freeze-dried treats. This makes your sitter's job more enjoyable and gives your cat a special experience.

The goal is to come home to happy and relaxed cats, not cats that have exhibited stress-related behavior like peeing in suitcases.

## Taking Your Cat With You

Taking your cat on vacation is another option. Start by harness training your cat for safety, regardless of destination. A harness is especially crucial if you're flying, since you'll need to remove your cat from their carrier at the airport. Harness training also helps when transporting them between locations.

When traveling with your cat, bring a mobile version of your "base camp"—items that retain your cat's scent, like their carrier or a familiar blanket, to provide comfort. Bring their favorite food, dishes, and treats, and maintain their regular feeding schedule as much as possible. This minimizes disruption to their routine while you're away.
