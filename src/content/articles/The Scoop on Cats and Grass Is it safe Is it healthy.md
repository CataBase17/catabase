## Why Cats Eat Grass

Your cat's grass-eating habit is an instinctual behavior passed down from wild ancestors. Historically, cats would eat grass to cleanse their insides of parasites and impurities. The grass acts like a natural cleanser, helping to remove harmful substances from their system through stool or vomit.

## Nutritional Benefits

Grass is packed with helpful nutrients for your cat. It contains chlorophyll, protein, and folic acid—all beneficial for overall health. The chlorophyll in particular can have positive effects on your cat's wellness, much like the benefits humans experience from wheatgrass.

While nibbling on grass is beneficial for cats, not all types are safe. Outdoor grass may have been treated with fertilizers and pesticides, so choosing the right grass matters.

## Growing Grass Indoors

The safest option is to grow cat grass indoors. The main types recommended for cats are wheatgrass, oat, barley, and rye. These grasses have smaller blades than regular lawn grass, making them easier for cats to process.

You can grow these easily in pots using all-in-one seed packets, which sprout quickly. Most kits take around three days to sprout, though you should wait until the grass has fully grown before letting your cat nibble to avoid waste.

Each type of grass offers different benefits. Oat grass is sweeter and makes a great beginner option for cats who have never eaten grass before. Wheatgrass is packed with chlorophyll—about 70% of it—making it nutrient-dense. Rye grass is durable and short, ideal for cats to roll around in. Barley grass grows up to 14 inches long, allowing your cat to munch on it continuously as it regrows. A combination of all four types is ideal.

You can also plant fresh catnip indoors, which is better for your cat than dried catnip since they can eat it fresh.

## When to Check with Your Vet

If your cat has never eaten grass before but suddenly starts eating it frequently, this may signal indigestion or gastrointestinal issues. A vet visit is recommended to determine the cause.

Cats sometimes eat grass to ease their stomach or alleviate nausea, reverting to their natural instincts for relief. However, grass-eating can also indicate a missing nutrient, such as iron. Your cat might try to get iron from other sources like dirt. A full blood panel from your vet may be necessary to identify any deficiencies.

If your cat starts eating grass or other non-food items suddenly, consult your vet to rule out underlying health issues and ensure overall well-being.

## Choosing Cat Grass

When buying cat grass for the first time, stick to the basic types: oat, wheat, barley, and rye, or any combination of these. Always choose organic cat grass. Non-organic varieties may be treated with chemicals, pesticides, and fertilizers similar to lawn grass, which can be harmful to your cat.

Providing your cat with grass allows them to connect with their natural instincts and can help stimulate their behavior and overall well-being—making it a positive addition to their environment.
