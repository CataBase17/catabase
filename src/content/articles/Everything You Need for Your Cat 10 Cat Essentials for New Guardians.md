Bringing a new cat home is a wonderful decision that saves a life and enriches yours. Whether you're adopting an adult cat or fostering a kitten, having the right supplies and setup from the start will help your new companion feel safe, secure, and happy in their new environment.

## Basecamp: Creating a Safe Starting Point

The most important thing you can do when bringing a cat home is to establish a "basecamp"—a dedicated space where your cat can claim territory by soaking it with their scent. This safe space helps your cat develop confidence, often called "cat mojo," which allows them to explore and eventually claim the rest of your home as they gradually get introduced to new areas.

A basecamp works as a series of scent-based signposts that help your cat recognize and feel comfortable in their surroundings. This sense of belonging and security is crucial for your cat's adjustment to their new home. Within this basecamp, you'll need to set up the essentials: food, water, a litter box, and comfortable places to rest.

## Choosing Food and Water Bowls

Avoid plastic bowls for your cat's food and water. Plastic is porous and allows bacteria and germs to accumulate, which can lead to chin acne in cats. Instead, choose non-porous materials like ceramic, stainless steel, or glass (such as Pyrex).

Change your cat's water daily to keep it fresh. Many cats enjoy drinking from and playing in water fountains, which can also help keep water fresh and flowing. If you use a cat fountain, opt for one made primarily of ceramic, glass, or stainless steel rather than plastic.

## Setting Up Litter Boxes

Follow the general rule of having one litter box per cat, plus one extra. This ensures your cat always has a clean option available.

Choose a large litter box that gives your cat plenty of room to turn around and dig comfortably. The size matters—a bigger box is better.

Use natural, unscented litter. Some cats are sensitive to strong perfumes in scented litters. Avoid plastic liners in the box, as they can catch your cat's claws and cause discomfort. Also skip the litter box hood—the idea that cats prefer privacy is a myth, and hoods can trap odors and make the box feel cramped.

Scoop the litter box at least once or twice a day. Cats prefer a clean environment and may avoid a dirty litter box. A litter scoop and disposal system like a Litter Genie can make this task easier.

## Feeding Your Cat

Dry food alone is not the best nutritional choice for cats. If your cat is currently eating dry food (as many rescue cats are, since it's often donated and easier to manage), consider transitioning to a wet or raw diet over about a week.

Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning they require a diet primarily made of meat. A grain-free wet diet or raw diet will support a longer, healthier, and happier life for your cat.

## Providing Scent Soakers

Scent soakers are items your cat can rub against, paw at, or scratch to leave their scent and claim territory in a positive way. These are essential for your cat's sense of security and confidence.

Beds of various shapes and sizes work well as scent soakers. Simple flat pads, sometimes called "bed frisbees," can be placed on couches, beds, or other furniture and moved easily to different locations. These give your cat familiar, scent-soaked spots throughout your home.

Scratching posts are equally important scent soakers. They come in different textures—sisal rope, carpet, or bare wood—and choosing the right ones depends on understanding your cat's scratching preferences. Some cats prefer vertical scratching, some prefer horizontal surfaces, and others like inclined angles. Observe your cat's natural scratching behavior to choose posts that match their preference.

When selecting scratching posts, choose ones with a wide, sturdy base that is tall enough for your cat to stretch fully. A wobbly or unstable post won't work well.

Cat trees, especially those placed near windows, provide both scent-soaking and enrichment. They give your cat a view of the outdoors—what many call "cat tv"—where they can watch birds, traffic, and other activity while enjoying the sun.

Having multiple scent soakers throughout your home, including beds, scratching posts, and cat trees, helps boost your cat's confidence and creates a sense of security. Don't skimp on these items—they're essential to a comfortable home.

## Selecting Toys for Play and Enrichment

Interactive play is crucial for bonding with your cat and keeping them entertained. Have a variety of toys on hand, with interactive toys like fishing poles with strings and feathers being particularly engaging.

Keep interactive toys in a safe place like a cabinet or drawer, and only bring them out during playtime. This makes the experience special and prevents potential dangers, since some cats can get into trouble if left unsupervised with certain toys.

In addition to interactive toys, have self-play toys available, like small stuffed mice, that your cat can bat around independently. Remember that every cat has a different personality—some cats are eager to play while others need to be coaxed into activity. Get to know your cat's individual preferences and build up a variety of toys to match their play style.

## Cat-Proofing Your Home

Think of cat-proofing like childproofing for a child who can reach the ceiling. Cats can access high places and get into unexpected spaces, so you need to consider potential hazards throughout your entire home.

Remove or secure toxic plants and foods that can harm cats. Secure medicine cabinets and the area under sinks with child locks. Check your garage and other spaces for hazards. Create a checklist of areas to review and make safe for your curious new companion.

## Choosing a Cat Carrier

A carrier should feel like your cat's den, escape hatch, or safe panic room—a place they can retreat when they need to feel secure. Creating a positive association with the carrier from the beginning is essential.

Make the carrier comfortable by removing the door or top, placing a nice blanket inside, and adding something that smells like you. Most importantly, only give your cat their favorite treats inside the carrier. This builds a positive association so that when you need to take them to the vet or travel, they'll settle into the carrier rather than panicking.

Choose a carrier large enough for your cat to turn around inside, but not so open that it feels exposed. If you travel by airplane, check that your carrier is airline-approved and fits under the seat in front of you.

## Identification Supplies

Many rescue cats come with microchips already, but if yours doesn't have one, get your cat microchipped immediately. This is the most reliable way to ensure your cat can be returned to you if they escape or get lost.

In addition to a microchip, fit your cat with a breakaway collar and tag containing your phone number. A breakaway collar is a crucial safety feature—if the collar gets caught on something while your cat is running, it will snap apart and prevent choking.

Take a clear, full-face picture of your cat and keep it handy. This photo will help identify and recover your cat if they ever go missing.

## Grooming Essentials

Start getting comfortable with grooming tasks like nail trimming from the beginning. Begin by lightly touching your cat's paws during relaxing times and offering reassurance.

To trim nails, gently press on the knuckle to make the claw extend, then carefully trim only the tip with nail trimmers, avoiding the blood supply inside the claw. Keep the experience positive to avoid creating negative associations with grooming.

Have a brush on hand as well. Different cats prefer different brush types, so try out a few options to see what your cat responds to best. Brushing stimulates oil production, reduces dander, and improves your cat's coat. Start slowly and gently, beginning with sensitive areas like the cheeks or top of the head, and always respect your cat's boundaries to keep grooming enjoyable.

## Getting to Know Your Individual Cat

Every cat has unique preferences and needs. What works for one cat may not work for another. The key to successful cat ownership is patience, flexibility, and a willingness to learn your specific cat's personality, play style, and preferences.

The process of bonding with your cat and establishing trust is ongoing. Prioritize your cat's health, safety, and well-being, and be open to adapting your approach based on what you learn about their individual needs.
