## Collar and Tag

Your cat needs to wear a collar with an ID tag containing your current phone number. The most important information on the tag is how someone can reach you—while it's nice to include your cat's name, the priority is making sure anyone who finds your cat can call you right away.

Make sure your cat's collar is a breakaway collar. This type of collar is designed to snap open if your cat gets caught on a fence, chain-link, or a branch. This safety feature prevents serious injury or worse if your cat gets snagged while exploring. Some people worry that a breakaway collar might come off too easily, but it's your first line of defense in keeping your cat safe.

## Microchipping

Microchipping is one of the most important steps you can take. Microchipped cats are up to 50% more likely to be returned to their owners than cats without a microchip. When your cat is found and brought to a vet or shelter, staff can scan the microchip and use the information to contact you immediately.

The key is keeping your microchip information current. If you move or change your phone number, update your microchip registration right away. Without updated information, even a microchipped cat may not make it back home.

## Spaying and Neutering

Having your cat spayed or neutered reduces the hormonal drives that make cats want to escape and roam. Intact male cats will try to break out if they detect females in heat in the neighborhood. Intact female cats in heat attract males and can trigger dangerous situations. Spaying or neutering eliminates these urges and also prevents the disruptive sounds of caterwauling and fighting between males. Beyond preventing escapes, it's an important part of responsible pet ownership given the number of cats already in need of homes.

## Pet-Proofing Your Home

Installing heavy-gauge pet screening on windows prevents your cat from clawing through regular screens. Check that screen doors are secure and can't be pushed open. Many cats can open traditional doorknobs, so consider replacing them with round door handles that your cat can't operate.

If your cat has access to a backyard, add fence extensions designed to prevent escape, and make sure all gates are securely latched. Loud noises like car backfires or fireworks can startle your cat into bolting, so secure latches are essential.

Never leave your cat unattended indoors or outdoors. Close windows when you leave the house, and test your pet-proofing measures while you're home to make sure they actually work. Pay attention to separation anxiety, which can drive cats to try escaping when their owners leave.

## Training and Enrichment

Training your cat gives you control in the home—teach your cat to sit near the door so they don't bolt when you open it. You can also train your cat using a harness and leash for safe outdoor time, or use clicker training to reinforce good behavior.

Bring the outdoors inside by setting up "cat TV"—a comfortable window with sun exposure where your cat can watch birds, bugs, and bees. This kind of enrichment reduces your cat's desire to go outside and explore on their own.

## If Your Cat Does Escape

Have a current, high-quality photograph of your cat ready. If your cat goes missing, create a bright-colored flyer with a clear, full-frontal photo, your cat's description, and your phone number. Include a reward if you can offer one.

Plaster the area with flyers at intersections and give them to neighbors. Repetition helps people remember and report sightings. When you search, check nearby hiding spots like under houses, cars, and neighbors' garages. Cats often stay within their territory when they escape, hiding in quiet places. Check with neighbors and ask them to look in their garages and enclosed spaces—your cat might be trapped somewhere close by.

Stay calm and prepared. Being proactive and having these tools in place helps ensure you'll be reunited with your cat if the worst happens.
