Your cat does plenty of things that might seem strange at first, but most behaviors have logical explanations rooted in feline instincts and needs.

## Why Your Cat Knocks Things Over

Your cat knocks items off tables and shelves because they're bored and need an outlet for their energy. Once a cat hears the crash of something falling, they're likely to repeat the behavior—the noise and action are genuinely entertaining to them.

Pay attention to when your cat does this most. If they tend to knock things over at certain times of day, that's when their energy is highest. Playing with your cat during those peak times can redirect that energy and reduce the behavior.

For valuable or fragile items you want to protect, museum putty is an effective solution. It secures items in place without damaging shelves or surfaces, making it ideal for keeping breakables safe.

## The Belly-Up "Cat Hug"

When your cat flops on the floor with their belly exposed in what looks like a hug position, they're showing you serious trust and affection. This behavior means your cat feels safe and loved in your home.

Exposing the belly is a big deal for cats because it goes against their natural survival instincts. The underside is vulnerable—in the wild, this would be an invitation to attack. When your cat does this with you, they're making themselves defenseless because they genuinely feel secure. It's one of the highest compliments your cat can give you.

## "If It Fits, I Sits"

Your cat's obsession with squeezing into tiny boxes and containers comes from deep instincts. Even when a space looks impossibly cramped, your cat is seeking the security of an enclosed area where they feel hidden and protected, much like their wild ancestors did to stay safe and conserve energy.

Squeezing into small spaces also helps your cat retain body heat—the enclosed area traps warmth around their body. Beyond the practical benefits, many cats simply find it fun to hop from box to box.

This behavior is completely normal, and it's satisfying for your cat even if it looks uncomfortable to you. Don't be surprised if your cat manages to fit into spaces you didn't think were physically possible.

## Kneading (Making Biscuits)

Your cat's rhythmic pushing motion with their paws—sometimes called kneading, making biscuits, smurgling, or making bread—is a leftover behavior from kittenhood. Kittens knead their mother's belly to stimulate milk production, and even as adults, cats continue this motion because it reminds them of safety and comfort.

When your cat kneads on blankets, beds, or directly on you, they're associating you with that maternal comfort. If your cat kneads on items that smell like you or while sitting on your lap, they're treating you like their mother. This is a genuine sign of affection and trust—you've earned the honorary title of "cat mom" or "cat dad."

## The Zoomies

Those sudden bursts of frenzied running, climbing, and vertical wall-running are called the zoomies, and they're connected to your cat's natural hunting cycle. Cats sleep a lot because they're conserving energy for the hunt. When they wake up, all that pent-up energy needs release, and without actual prey to pursue, they race around your home instead.

If your cat gets the zoomies at night when you're trying to sleep, their circadian rhythm is out of sync with yours. You can help fix this by establishing a consistent schedule for feeding and playtime.

Free feeding, especially dry food, can make zoomies worse because dry kibble converts to sugar quickly in your cat's system, causing energy spikes. Instead, feed meals at set times—ideally at least three times a day. A structured routine helps synchronize your cat's body clock with yours, reducing late-night "freight train" episodes in the living room.

## Why Your Cat Sits on Your Keyboard

Your cat sits on your keyboard, monitor, or clothes while you're working because they want to be near you and receive attention. But there's also a simpler explanation: cats are heat-seekers. Electronics generate warmth, and your cat is naturally drawn to that heat source, much like they love sunny windowsills.

Instead of fighting this, provide an alternative. A heated cat bed near your workspace—on your desk or a side table nearby—gives your cat the warmth they crave while keeping them close to you. Plug-in cat beds work well, or you can use a regular bed with a layer of mylar underneath for insulation. This way, your cat stays warm and comfortable while you can actually work.

## Chewing on Plastic Bags

Cats chew plastic bags for a few reasons. The crinkling sound is appealing to them—that's why crinkly toys are popular cat toys. The smell also matters: many plastic bags are treated with stearates derived from animal fat, and some are treated with fish oil or fish scales to prevent sticking. Your cat's powerful sense of smell detects these animal-derived scents and finds them irresistible.

However, if your cat is intensely focused on chewing plastic, it might indicate a condition called pica—the compulsive eating of non-edible objects. This can be hard to stop once it develops.

The safest approach is to "pica-proof" your house by removing plastic bags and replacing them with paper alternatives. Plastic bags pose a suffocation risk, so keeping them away from your cat is important regardless of whether they chew them.

## Radar Ears

You might notice your cat looking directly at you, then suddenly their ears swivel in a completely different direction. This is called "radar ears," and it happens because cats are constantly scanning for potential prey or threats.

Your cat's ears are remarkably complex. The outer part of each ear, called the pinna, is controlled by 32 tiny muscles that allow each ear to rotate independently of the other. Your cat can rotate their ears up to 180 degrees, which boosts their already excellent hearing by up to 30 percent. This ability helps them detect sounds from multiple directions simultaneously—like a mouse rustling behind them while they're looking at you. It's an essential survival skill.

## The Stinkface (Flehmen Response)

When your cat encounters a strong smell and makes an odd grimacing expression with their mouth slightly open, they're using something called the Jacobson's organ (or vomeronasal organ) to gather detailed scent information. This secondary scenting system is located in the roof of their mouth and helps them analyze smells more thoroughly.

Your cat is most likely to make this face when they smell something important to territorial security—like cat urine, feces, or other animals' scent markers. These smells contain information about other animals' identity, sex, and reproductive status. Your cat needs this information to defend their territory and stay safe, which is why they take time to really investigate these scents.

## The "Back-Ended Compliment"

When your cat walks up to you, puts their butt in your face, and presents their rear end, they're giving you a "back-ended compliment." This comes from kittenhood behavior: mother cats stimulate their kittens' rear ends to encourage them to eliminate after eating. As kittens grow, they become used to walking up to their mother and having her attend to this area.

Your cat is treating you like their mother, seeking the comfort and care they associate with her. It's a genuine sign of trust and affection, even though it feels odd from a human perspective.

If you prefer not to have your cat do this, you can gently pick them up and reposition them while still acknowledging their affectionate intention. The behavior itself is not meant to offend—it's purely instinctual and comes from a place of trust.
