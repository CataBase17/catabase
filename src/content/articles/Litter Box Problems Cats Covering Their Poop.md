## Why Cats Bury Their Waste

Your cat's instinct to bury poop comes from deep ancestral roots. Cats are both supreme hunters and prey animals—they hunt but also know what it's like to be hunted. This behavior shows up across all cats, from big wild cats to your house pet.

Burying waste serves two purposes. First, it can be a way to hide from predators by throwing off the scent trail. But not burying poop is actually a dominant move—it's how cats mark territory and tell other cats, "This is mine." Which behavior your cat chooses depends on their confidence level.

Some cats, like those with less confidence, bury their poop because they don't want to announce their presence or challenge other cats' territory. It's a submissive, "wallflower" behavior. This can stem from experiences during kittenhood (being picked on by littermates, for example), genetic predisposition to nervousness, or even learned behavior from their mother. The good news is that this isn't a reflection on your relationship with your cat or how much you care for them—it's about your cat's own personality and history.

## Building Your Cat's Confidence

To address excessive burying behavior, you need to work on building your cat's confidence and sense of ownership in their space. Here are several concrete steps you can take.

**Add more litter boxes.** Start by adding at least one or two additional litter boxes to your home. Cats find confidence in scent—even after you scoop, the smell in the box gives your cat a sense of ownership and security. By spreading litter boxes throughout your home, you're giving your cat more areas to claim as their own, which naturally boosts confidence.

**Reconsider litter box placement.** Where you put the box matters. Placing it in a closet or right against a wall can actually encourage obsessive scratching behavior. Try moving the box to the middle of a room or an open area instead, away from walls. Breaking a long-established habit takes patience, but this change can help reduce the scratching.

**Choose the right litter.** The litter itself might be part of the problem. Many cats dislike scented litter, so switch to an unscented option. Texture matters too—aim for the smoothest litter you can find, something like sand. Avoid clay, crystals, or heavily textured materials. When you add new boxes, try a completely different type of litter to see if your cat responds better.

**Encourage play and hunting.** Interactive play with toys can increase your cat's sense of ownership and confidence in their space. Hunting behavior reinforces that sense of strength and territory.

## Addressing Food Burying

If your cat is also burying food, the solution is to switch from free-feeding to scheduled mealtimes. Leaving food out all day actually increases your cat's anxiety—they feel the need to protect and bury it. Instead, put food out at set times and pick it up when your cat finishes eating. This eliminates leftovers and removes the trigger for the burying behavior.

If your cat is already on a meal schedule but still tries to bury food, use it as a cue to pick up the bowl right away. Your cat is telling you they're done.

The root causes of these behaviors—both waste burying and food burying—can be natural instinct, hereditary, or learned. There's rarely just one solution. By understanding your cat's personality and trying different approaches (adding boxes, changing litter, adjusting feeding schedules, and playing more), you can find what works best for your individual cat and create a more comfortable environment for everyone in your home.
