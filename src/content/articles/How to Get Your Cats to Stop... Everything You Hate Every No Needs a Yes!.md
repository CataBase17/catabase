## Why punishment doesn't work with cats

You want your cat to sit in your lap but stay off the countertops. When she jumps where you don't want her to, it's tempting to say "no" over and over—but cats can seem stubborn and indifferent to scolding. What you're seeing isn't defiance; it's your cat exhibiting normal behavior that you're trying to redirect.

When you get angry or frustrated, that's the moment to step back and calm down. Your usual reactions—yelling, spraying with a bottle, swatting, or throwing pillows—won't work the way you hope.

Using a spray bottle might seem to stop the behavior in the moment, but here's what's actually happening: your cat becomes afraid of *you*, not afraid of the unwanted behavior itself. That fear can extend to other objects or actions too. And the moment you leave the room, she'll return to scratching, jumping, or whatever she was doing. You've taught her to fear you, not to change her behavior.

## The "No-Yes" method

The solution is the "No-Yes" method: give your cat a clear "no" to the unwanted behavior *and* a clear "yes"—a desirable alternative. Think of it like a child who draws on the walls with markers and crayons. Punishing her every day creates a cycle of anger and repetition that never breaks. But if you provide an easel instead, you've said "no" to the wall and "yes" to the easel. The unwanted behavior can be replaced with something acceptable.

With cats, you apply the same principle. Identify what you don't want her doing, then provide an alternative she can do instead.

## Getting your cat off the counter

Counters are appealing partly because of food. Start by removing any food left on the counter—leaving it out is an open invitation.

Next, make the counter surface unpleasant without causing pain. Turn a feeding mat upside down and apply double-sided sticky tape to it. Cats dislike the sticky feeling on their paws and will avoid landing there.

An Air Canister with an electric eye is another effective tool. It releases a puff of air and makes a sound when triggered, so your cat associates the counter itself with an unpleasant experience—not you.

But giving a "no" isn't enough. Provide a "yes": place a cat tree in the kitchen where your cat can still be near you and observe the action. Use high-value treats to lead her to this spot. Reward her heavily when she goes there. Positive reinforcement helps her associate the cat tree with good things.

Avoid screaming at her or throwing her off the counter. Stay consistent and patient; these techniques work better than any amount of punishment.

## Preventing door dashing

Cats run out the door because they're curious about what's happening outside. Create a shelf or perch near your entryway where your cat can watch people come and go. This gives her a bird's-eye view of the world while you keep her safely inside.

You can also set up an Air Canister about 10 feet from the door, always turned on. This makes the space leading to the door a "no" zone, while the nearby perch becomes a "yes" zone. Your cat learns that the approach to the door is uncomfortable but has a rewarding alternative close by.

If she does dash out, don't yell at her or kick her away. That creates a negative association and makes her less likely to come back inside willingly. Instead, use the environmental tools consistently and gently to train her.

## Stopping furniture scratching

Sticky Paws—a gentle, sticky deterrent—applied to furniture works well because most cats will avoid scratching where it's placed. But again, you need the "yes" to go with the "no."

Place a scratching post or pad near the furniture your cat is scratching. Cats are drawn to these spots because of the scent already there; the scratching post complements that and gives her an acceptable place to do what she naturally needs to do.

Sticky Paws is a training tool, not a permanent fixture. Once your cat learns to use the post instead of the couch, you can remove it. This method works because it provides a clear boundary without punishment, and it lets your cat satisfy her scratching instinct in a way that works for both of you.

## Getting your cat to sleep through the night

If your cat wakes you at 3 a.m., walking on your face or making noise, the "no" is simple: ignore her completely. Don't throw a pillow, don't get up to feed or play with her, don't react at all. Bury your head and stay silent.

The "yes" happens around bedtime: give her a meal before bed and playtime earlier in the evening. This routine helps wind her body down. Be consistent with this schedule every single day, and don't give in to her nighttime demands. Most cats show a change in behavior within about 10 days when you maintain this routine without exception.

## Managing excess energy

When your cat has too much energy, she might knock things off shelves, climb drapes, or race around the house. These behaviors often happen at the same time each day.

Identify both the "no" and the "yes." The "no" could be removing tempting items from shelves, applying sticky paws to drapes, or moving the couch away from the curtains so she can't use it as a launch pad. Environmental changes like these say "no" without any interaction from you.

For the "yes," provide cat trees in appealing designs and engage in daily play. Use lasers, independent toys with sound makers, or little fuzzy mice to catch her attention and redirect her energy before she gets into trouble. A shaker or laser toy can gently redirect her if you catch her mid-pounce.

By combining the right environment, cat trees, regular playtime, and interactive toys, you give your cat constructive outlets for her energy and reduce the need to say "no" at all.

## Tools to avoid

Be clear about the line between uncomfortable and painful. Avoid devices with spikes, electric charges, or shock collars. These can cause pain, even if it's not visible, and they have no place in cat training.

Sticky paws, scat mats, air canisters, and interactive toys are all humane tools you can find in most stores.

## Moving forward

If you've used harsher methods in the past, don't judge yourself harshly. The important thing is learning and being willing to change. Think of training your cat as building a relationship and finding compromise, not imposing rules and always getting your way. When you approach your cat with patience, love, and a willingness to learn, you build something much stronger than obedience—you build trust.
