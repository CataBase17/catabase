## Understanding Non-Recognition Aggression

If you have multiple cats at home, you may encounter a puzzling and distressing situation: when one cat returns from a vet visit, the others suddenly treat them as a stranger. This is called non-recognition aggression, and it can turn what's already a stressful day for your cat into an even worse one, complete with fighting, growling, and hostility from their familiar companions.

The reason this happens is simple but powerful. Cats rely heavily on scent to identify each other and recognize members of their community. When your cat goes to the vet—especially if they've been anesthetized—they return home smelling completely different. The anesthesia, medical procedures, and unfamiliar smells from the clinic all mask their original scent. To your other cats, this returning cat might as well be a stranger who has invaded their home.

## How Cats Recognize Each Other

Cats greet each other by bumping noses, which allows them to smell and identify one another. This scent recognition is so fundamental to how cats relate to each other that it matters even between cats who have lived together for years, like littermates. Without that familiar scent, your cats can't properly recognize each other, and the situation can escalate quickly.

When a cat comes out of the carrier after a vet visit, the moment of freeze and intense staring begins. Your other cats investigate with focused smelling, but because the returning cat smells unfamiliar, that investigation can trigger mistrust and uncertainty. The returned cat, still groggy from anesthesia and stressed, may not tolerate this intense scrutiny and can become defensive. What starts as confusion snowballs into aggression in moments, and a fight can break out—even between littermates.

## A Simple Solution Using Scent

There's an easy way to prevent this problem. Before you leave for the vet, take a clean t-shirt and rub it thoroughly on your cat to transfer their scent onto the fabric. Keep this scent-soaked t-shirt in a sealed ziploc bag to preserve the scent during your absence. When you return home from the vet, rub your cat down with the same t-shirt again before letting them out of the carrier. This may not be your cat's favorite experience, but it's worth doing.

By rubbing your cat with the t-shirt, they'll smell like themselves when they step out of the carrier—not like the vet's office. Your other cats will recognize their familiar companion's scent, even though the cat has been away. The t-shirt carries not only your returning cat's original scent but also the scent of you, their owner, and any shared bed in the house. This combination helps bridge the gap and prevents the moment of non-recognition that leads to aggression.

This technique essentially short-circuits the problem before it starts. Your cats will recognize each other, the stress of homecoming is reduced, and you avoid potential conflicts in your multi-cat household. It's a small effort that makes a real difference in helping your cats readjust smoothly.
