## Why Your Neutered Cat Is Still Humping

If your cat is neutered or spayed but continues to hump stuffed animals, blankets, or other objects, you're not alone—and the behavior isn't necessarily sexual. Neutered and spayed cats can still display humping behavior for non-sexual reasons, but it's important to rule out a few physical possibilities first.

## Check for Cryptorchidism

In rare cases, a neutered male cat may still exhibit humping because one of his testicles never descended into the scrotum. This condition, called cryptorchidism, means the veterinarian may have missed the undescended testicle during the neutering procedure. A vet can check for this by looking for barbs around the penis (which indicate testosterone production) or by running a blood test to measure testosterone levels. If cryptorchidism is confirmed, your vet can address it.

## Understanding the Real Cause

Once you've ruled out any physical issues, the humping is likely behavioral. The behavior often serves as a self-soothing action—similar to nail biting or chain smoking in humans. Your cat may be using it to calm himself down or manage stress.

Consider your cat's history. When did the behavior start? Does it happen at certain times of day or in specific situations? If your cat was neutered late in life (like at three years old), he may have developed this behavior before the surgery and it became a learned, ingrained habit. Some cats who were weaned early from their mothers continue to perform comfort behaviors into adulthood; humping can work the same way.

Pay attention to your cat's environment and stress levels. Is there added stress in his life that might be contributing? Does the behavior happen when your family is particularly active, at certain meal times, or during other predictable patterns?

## Redirect Through Structured Playtime

The most effective solution is to redirect your cat's energy through structured playtime multiple times a day. Unlike free play, structured playtime means you control the pace, intensity, and outcome. Use toys that encourage chasing and pouncing—this helps your cat burn energy and de-stress in a healthy, appropriate way.

Start by journaling your cat's behavior. Note when humping occurs, what triggers it, and what's happening in your household at those times. This will help you identify patterns. Once you spot them, schedule intensive playtime during the periods when your cat is most likely to hump. If he tends to hump in the afternoon, for example, have a vigorous play session then to help him release energy.

When you notice your cat about to hump an inappropriate object, gently redirect him to a suitable toy or into a play session. Over time, as your cat releases more energy through play and gains confidence, the humping behavior should decrease. Eventually, you may be able to reintroduce previously hidden objects like stuffed animals, though this depends on your individual cat's progress.

## When to Consider Additional Help

If you've tried structured playtime and environmental adjustments and the behavior persists, talk to your veterinarian. In cases of truly compulsive behavior that doesn't respond to behavioral management, pharmaceutical options exist. Some owners also explore gentler approaches like flower essences designed to help with obsessive behaviors, though these should be discussed with your vet.

The key is patience and consistency. Even older cats can benefit from structured playtime and a thoughtful management plan. With time and the right approach, you can help your cat develop healthier, more confident behaviors.
