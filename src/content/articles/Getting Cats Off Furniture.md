## Setting Limits with Your Cat

If your cat is doing something you don't want it to do, the key isn't just to say no—it's to say "no yes." This means blocking the unwanted behavior while offering an appealing alternative at the same time.

Think of it like this: if you take away a child's markers, you replace them with an easel. You're not just removing something; you're redirecting to something better. The same principle works perfectly with cats.

Let's say your cat loves sitting on a particular chair. You can tell your cat no to that specific chair, but you need to provide a yes—another chair nearby that has what your cat loves about the original one. If your cat is drawn to the blanket on the forbidden chair, put a blanket on an alternative chair instead.

To make the unwanted chair less appealing, use temporary deterrents like double-sided sticky tape or a compressed air canister. These tools encourage your cat to jump off without any punishment—they're just unpleasant enough that your cat will naturally avoid the spot. The key is that your cat learns to avoid it through its own choice, not because you forced it.

Now comes the important part: make the alternative so good that your cat actually wants to use it. Place the new chair right next to the original one if possible. Make sure it has the blanket your cat loves. Add treats to sweeten the deal. Give your cat time to claim this new space, mark it with its scent, and make it its own.

The goal is to create a positive association with the alternative. When your cat gets to scratch, hang out, and experience something similar to what it was originally seeking—but on the approved chair instead—it will naturally choose the better option. Both you and your cat can be happy, and everyone's boundaries are respected.
