## What's Really Happening When Your Cat Attacks Your Feet

Your cat's evening attacks and pouncing on your feet aren't signs of aggression—they're rooted in your cat's natural hunting instincts. When you notice your cat fixating on your feet, especially when you're moving around the house, your cat sees prey, not a family member.

It helps to reconsider how you describe your cat's behavior. Many people use words like "attack" when talking about these moments, but that language can create unnecessary fear. Instead, recognize what your cat is actually doing: engaging in play aggression, which is a normal expression of the hunting drive that all cats carry from their ancestors.

## Why Cats Play Like Hunters

Cats descend from hunters with a simple life cycle: hunt, catch, kill, eat, groom, sleep, and repeat. That cycle is deeply wired into your cat's nature. For kittens, play is practice for hunting. They learn this from siblings and littermates as they grow, and the behavior continues into adulthood. Your cat's play is how they release the built-up energy that accumulates during their long sleep hours.

When your cat pursues your feet or attacks when you run across the house, they're not trying to hurt you—they're expelling stored energy through play that mimics stalking ground prey like squirrels or bugs. This behavior often becomes most noticeable in the evenings, when your cat's energy peaks.

## Recognizing the Signs

Watch your cat's tail. When your cat's tail starts to flick as they watch you move, that's a signal they're engaged and ready to play. As their agitation builds, the tail will move from flicking to wagging. This is your cat's "escape valve" for pent-up energy—a sign that playtime is coming.

Your cat may also try to lead you into a chasing game by positioning themselves in your path or waiting for you to move again. When you don't comply, the play escalates because your cat isn't getting the outlet they need.

## How to Stop the Biting

The solution isn't to discourage your cat's hunting behavior—it's to give them an appropriate target for it.

**Use interactive toys.** Wand toys with feathers or string-attached prey toys let your cat engage their hunting instinct while keeping your body safely out of reach. Drag the toy in unpredictable, prey-like movements to trigger your cat's natural response. By trailing the toy and encouraging your cat to chase and catch it, you satisfy their hunting drive and actually deepen your bond with them.

**Create predictable playtime routines.** Once you notice when your cat becomes most energetic (typically evenings), use that knowledge to your advantage. Schedule interactive play sessions at those times using the three R's: routine, ritual, and rhythm. Doing the same things every day at the same time syncs your cat's natural energy cycles with your own.

**Complete the hunt-to-sleep cycle.** This is crucial. A cat needs to experience the full cycle: hunt, catch, kill, eat, groom, and sleep. After an energetic play session with a wand toy, offer your cat a meal. This natural progression satisfies your cat completely and helps them transition from playful to calm and sleepy.

**Use remote toys for random moments.** Keep stuffed mice or other toys on hand to redirect your cat's attention when they suddenly bite or attack during the day. These toys give your cat an alternative target when the hunting urge strikes unexpectedly.

## Putting It All Together

Establish a daily routine that incorporates interactive play until your cat is tired, followed by mealtime. After your cat eats, pick up the toys and don't leave food down for grazing throughout the day. This clear structure—play, food, rest—helps your cat feel satisfied and reduces the urge to bite.

If you notice random moments of aggression, have remote toys available to redirect your cat's energy. The goal is to help your cat transition from an activated, playful state into a calm, sleepy state, so everyone in your home can have a peaceful night without unexpected bites or scratches.
