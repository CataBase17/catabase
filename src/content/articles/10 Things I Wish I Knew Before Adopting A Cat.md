## Introductions take time

When introducing a cat to another animal like a dog, don't assume they'll simply "work it out" on their own. This approach can turn ugly quickly, depending on the dog's size, prey drive, and other factors. A chase can damage your cat's confidence in ways that are hard to undo.

Before bringing a cat into a home with a dog, train the dog to do a reliable down stay—meaning your dog should be able to remain in a down position even when the cat is present. If your dog breaks out of this position, go back to training. If your dog isn't trustworthy during introductions, keep them on a leash to prevent any potential chase.

Introduce the animals step by step rather than leaving them unsupervised to work things out. Make sure your dog has no access to the cat's litter box, food, and water. A baby gate can block access to the litter box, and feeding them separately helps prevent resource guarding. Place your cat's food dish off the ground to keep the dog away, and protect your dog's food dish from the cat as well. It's also important to ensure your cat's nails are trimmed before close interactions to prevent accidental harm to your dog.

## Nail trimming

Trim your cat's nails every 3 to 4 weeks. This is crucial because allowing nails to grow too long can cause health issues—the nails can curve under and dig into the pads of the paws, which is painful for your cat. Cats don't naturally blunt their nails through scratching, so they need your help to maintain healthy nails.

Getting into the habit of trimming nails early makes the process less stressful for both you and your cat. Be cautious when handling a new cat, and avoid petting or picking them up too quickly, as sharp nails can be used in self-defense.

## Proper nutrition

Your cat is an obligate carnivore and requires a diet rich in meat. Do your due diligence on what a cat needs to eat when you bring one into your home.

Cats get the majority of their hydration through food, not water, so this matters when choosing their diet. Feeding a cat a dry diet like kibble can be detrimental to their health and can lead to serious issues like diabetes. Being your cat's best advocate when it comes to nutrition helps prevent health problems and improves their overall well-being.

## Cats need enrichment

Cats are often seen as low-maintenance pets because they don't protest or demand attention the way dogs do, but they absolutely require enrichment. Play is the first essential aspect—it's crucial for their well-being and should happen regularly.

Environmental enrichment, sometimes called "catification," is also vital. Cats explore and interact with their surroundings in unique ways. Your cat needs to be able to explore every square inch of their space to feel confident and secure. Optimizing their environment greatly improves their confidence and quality of life. Without proper enrichment, cats suffer—and so does your home.

## Cat-proofing

Think of cat-proofing like child-proofing: you need to block off hazards from floor to ceiling. Cats can get into anything they want, so everything needs to be secured.

Block off the unders of beds, coffee tables, and other hiding spots where cats could get stuck and be unreachable in an emergency. Cats will get into whatever is not secured, including medicines and toxic substances. It's vital to remember that they'll explore and investigate any accessible space. Researching and planning ahead when you bring a new cat home allows you to create a safer and more secure environment.

## Finding your team

Before bringing a cat home, establish a team of professionals: a trusted veterinarian and a reliable pet sitter. When selecting a vet, focus on the quality of care they provide rather than just convenience. Ask friends and family for recommendations.

Find a trustworthy pet sitter you can count on for emergencies or unexpected trips. A good sitter is bonded and assured, with references from people you know. Having a pet sitter gives you peace of mind when you're away and can be a better alternative to boarding.

Also consider pet insurance. It can provide financial protection when emergencies happen and unexpected veterinary expenses arise. Different plans offer varying premiums, deductibles, and coverage, so shop around to find what works for you.

## Emergency preparedness

Create an emergency plan that includes a "go bag" with essential items. Prepare a game plan in advance so you don't have to rely on adrenaline during a crisis.

Acclimate your cat to their carrier by making it a safe and familiar space. This helps prevent panic during emergency situations and allows for more efficient, stress-free evacuation if needed.

## The cat community

Being part of the cat community provides amazing advice and support. You can form well-rounded opinions by considering multiple perspectives from different people, which is especially helpful when dealing with cat health issues.

Numerous support groups and online communities exist, and people in your local area can offer valuable advice and guidance. Online resources like videos and websites can help you identify potential health issues, and connecting with a community can provide security and comfort when dealing with a diagnosis. The time and effort you invest in learning about cat care and health is valuable and rewarding—it allows you to feel confident in providing the best possible life for your cat and to help others do the same.

## Understanding cat history and nature

Understanding the history and nature of cats is essential to understanding their behavior, nutrition, and overall needs. Every aspect of your cat's life is informed by their ancestral background. Your cat's ancestor is still present in every domestic cat today.

This ancestral knowledge helps explain natural behaviors like scratching and informs optimal nutrition for your cat. Knowing cat history increases your appreciation for cats and helps you understand why certain behaviors are necessary. You can then find solutions—like providing proper scratching surfaces—that work for both you and your cat.
