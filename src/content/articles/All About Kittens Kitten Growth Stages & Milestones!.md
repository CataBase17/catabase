## Newborn Kittens

Newborn kittens are completely dependent on their mother. They're born without the ability to hear, see, walk, or regulate their own body temperature, and they have no teeth. They rely entirely on their mother for warmth—huddling against her to stay alive—and they can't eliminate waste on their own. Their mother stimulates them to do so by cleaning them.

Their eyes typically start to open between 9 to 13 days after birth, with day 10 being a common milestone.

## Around Day 10

By day 10, ear canals begin to open, and kittens start to hear, though it's unclear what they can perceive at this stage. Around three weeks of age, their ears are fully open and functional. Their eyes also open at this point, allowing them to see and begin moving around on their feet to explore and get away from their mother—though they remain completely dependent on her for survival.

## Five Weeks Old

At five weeks old, kittens enter a critical phase of social development. Their ears and eyes are functioning well, and they can walk around, avoid obstacles, and right themselves if they fall. By around four weeks, their teeth start to develop, and you can introduce solid food like cat food at this stage.

Kittens also begin moving away from their mother and becoming more independent. Human interaction—such as petting—helps them get used to people. The experiences your kitten has with humans during the first nine to ten weeks are crucial in determining the quality of their future interactions with people.

During this period, kittens start to mimic their mother's behavior and practice hunting skills with their littermates. They learn how to inhibit their bites and retract their claws, which is essential to their development. Through play with littermates and their mother, they learn valuable lessons about boundaries and social behavior—like not hitting too hard with their claws. By four to five weeks old, kittens are finding a measure of independence, watching and learning from their mother and siblings to understand what it means to be a cat.

## Six Weeks Old

By six weeks old, kittens are pretty much fully weaned and seeking independence. They play more with their littermates and establish a sleeping pattern, so they're not sleeping all the time anymore. At this age, kittens need to hunt—and that's where you come in as their caregiver.

Present them with hunting toys such as squeaky mice, plastic balls, crinkles, or interactive toys like fishing pole toys. It's important not to leave these toys out all the time, but rather use them during play sessions. Allowing your kitten to hunt a target—to stalk, pounce, tackle, and experience the full hunting cycle—is critical during this phase of development. Providing a suitable alternative to hunt rather than their littermates, mother, or random household objects helps them develop their natural instincts in a safe, controlled way.

## 10 to 14 Weeks Old

Kittens between 10 to 14 weeks old are undergoing significant development, transforming from blind and deaf newborns into little explorative hunting machines. They're starting to differentiate from their mother and explore beyond their siblings.

At this stage, kittens are learning to play and communicate. Provide them with toys to engage in object play, as they'll try to play with anything—including your furniture. You may notice them scratching couches and beds. To redirect this behavior, introduce kittens to scratching surfaces, including vertical, horizontal, or angled scratching posts.

By around 14 weeks old, wild kittens need to be self-sufficient, able to hunt, catch, and kill their own prey. Although they're solitary hunters, they'll still return to their siblings or playmates. The more secure kittens will start exploring on their own.

## 12 to 16 Weeks Old

At 12 to 16 weeks old, kittens reach sexual maturity—meaning a three-month-old kitten can reproduce. This is an important reason to spay or neuter your kitten to prevent unwanted litters and help address cat overpopulation. By approximately four months old, your kitten has become fully explorative, and their development has fully differentiated into an active, capable little cat.

## Six Months Old

At six months old, kittens enter a phase where they start to push limits and test boundaries, similar to human teenagers. This stage can last until they're around 16 months old. During this period, they're curious and try to see how far they can go before something happens.

It's essential to provide them with plenty of resources—beds, condos, scratching posts, and toys—to keep them occupied and stimulated. Having enough enrichment prevents them from getting into mischief. If kittens don't receive enough stimulation and attention, they may become withdrawn and lose their confidence and sense of purpose.

Adopting two kittens at the same time can be especially beneficial, as they can entertain and challenge each other, reducing the need for constant human involvement while keeping them engaged.

The concept of "cat mojo" refers to a kitten's confidence and understanding of their purpose. Help them develop this by providing opportunities to hunt, play, and interact with their environment. Finding out their purpose and developing their cat mojo is critical for their confidence and long-term adjustment as adult cats. Continue providing attention, stimulation, and opportunities for growth from six months to a year old and beyond to help them become confident and well-adjusted.

## The First Year

The progression from blind, deaf newborns who can't walk or regulate their own body temperature to skilled hunters is remarkable and happens within the first year of life. Kittens are considered kittens until around one year old, at which point they become grown cats. This first year is crucial for their development into one of the most perfect hunters on the planet.
