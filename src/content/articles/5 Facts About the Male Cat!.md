## Paw Preference

Male cats tend to be left-pawed, while female cats are more likely to be right-pawed. You can test your male cat's paw preference by holding up one of his favorite treats and watching which paw he uses to reach for it. Most male cats will instinctively use their left paw.

## Orange Tabbies Are Usually Male

About eighty percent of orange tabby cats are male. This happens because the orange gene is located on the X chromosome. The genetics of this trait means that male cats are far more likely to be born with orange coloring than females.

## The Origin of "Tomcat"

The term "tomcat" for male cats likely comes from an 18th-century book called "The Life and Adventures of a Cat," written in 1760. The protagonist of this adventure story was a cat named Tom, and over time, his name became a general term for male cats.

## Lifespan Differences

Male cats typically live one to two years shorter than female cats. However, neutering makes a significant difference in how long your male cat will live. Neutered male cats live about two to six years longer than unneutered ones.

This is because testosterone drives unneutered males to fight with other cats and claim territory, which exposes them to diseases like FIV. Unneutered males are also at higher risk for testicular cancer and face greater dangers from predators and cars when they roam outdoors.

Testosterone also causes unneutered male cats to develop pronounced jowls—thickened, toughened skin on their face that serves as protection during fights and acts as a signal to other cats. Once you neuter your cat, testosterone levels drop and his face will become noticeably smaller.

## Territory Marking and Urine Spraying

Male cats, especially unneutered ones, spray urine to mark their territory—a behavior driven by testosterone. While both male and female cats can spray on vertical surfaces, unneutered males do this much more frequently and intensely. They concentrate their urine and spray small amounts on key marking spots to establish boundaries and let other cats know the area belongs to them.

Over time, if a male cat isn't neutered, this spraying behavior can become deeply ingrained—almost like muscle memory—and harder to stop. Not all male cats will spray, but the behavior is far more common in unneutered males.

If you're dealing with urine spraying, enzymatic cleaners designed to break down pet urine are effective for removing both stains and odors from affected areas.

## When to Neuter Your Male Cat

The recommended age for neutering is eight weeks old. Neutering your male cat prevents a host of problems: he's far less likely to try escaping outside, he won't be driven to mate, he'll avoid fights with other cats, and he'll live a longer, healthier life with significantly reduced risk of testicular cancer. Given the millions of unwanted cats struggling on the streets, neutering is also an important part of controlling cat overpopulation.
