## Play

Your cat really does love to play, and the way you do it matters. Lazy play isn't very effective—instead, engage in interactive play where your cat is attached to one end of a wand toy or similar toy and you're holding the other end. This creates a dynamic, engaging experience that allows your cat to use their natural hunting instincts, run around, and get real exercise.

Interactive play makes cats happier and more well-adjusted. It can even help them learn how to play together and share with other cats. Playing with your cat also strengthens your bond, creating quality time together and opportunities for praise and positive reinforcement. Cats love to hear praise and being talked to, so use that to encourage good behavior and reward them for playing with you.

You can play with your cat multiple times a day with no downside—it just makes them happier. Make interactive play a regular part of your routine to provide your cat with the exercise, mental stimulation, and strong bond they need.

## Plastic Bags

Your cat's attraction to plastic bags has a specific cause: the presence of stearic acid (also called tallow), which is used as a slip agent during manufacturing to prevent bags from sticking together. Stearic acid is derived from animal products, and when your cat licks, paws, or rubs against plastic bags, they're responding to the scent of an animal already present on the bag. In some cases, they may even pee on the bag to mark it with their own scent.

Stearic acid isn't the only animal-derived substance in plastic bags—fish oil and fish scales are sometimes used as well, which may also contribute to your cat's attraction. These same non-slip agents appear in other products too, like bath mats, which may explain why your cat is drawn to those items or even pees on them. The combination of these animal-scent factors is why cats tend to go crazy for plastic bags, enjoying activities like licking, eating, rolling around in, and even destroying them.

## Scratching Furniture

Your cat tends to scratch furniture they love and use frequently—like your couch or bed—because these items have your scent on them, and your scent is strongest where you spend the most time. When your cat scratches these pieces, they're marking territory and complementing their own scent with yours. This is a form of communal scent sharing, similar to when cats touch noses with each other, and it gives them a sense of confidence and security called "Mojo."

Cats also scratch furniture because it provides a stable surface that doesn't move when scratched, giving them the traction they need. Many scratching posts tip over, which defeats the purpose.

To redirect your cat's scratching, provide a heavy, stable scratching post that won't tip over when pulled on, and place it right next to the furniture they tend to scratch—like next to your couch. You can use temporary deterrents like sticky tape on the furniture to discourage scratching while encouraging use of the post. It's also important to consider your cat's scratching preference—whether they like horizontal surfaces, vertical ones, or a combination—and provide a surface that matches their needs. By offering an appropriate scratching alternative, you can redirect their behavior away from your furniture and preserve your belongings.

## Dirty Laundry

Your cat often winds up in the hamper, buried in dirty laundry, and may even pee in it. This happens because dirty laundry smells like you, and the more things smell like you, the more your cat wants to add their own scent to create communal scent. By rolling in or peeing in your dirty laundry, they're marking territory and ensuring their scent is strong and dominant.

Your cat may pee in dirty laundry if they're feeling insecure about a smell—they want to make sure their scent is strong. This behavior is related to their instinct to mark territory and establish that communal scent with you, which equals security and confidence for them.

To keep your cat out of dirty laundry, use a hamper with a lock. Since your cat is drawn to the scent of you in the laundry, they'll try to mark it or roll in it if they can access it.

## Grass

Your cat has a strong affinity for grass, particularly cat grass. Cat grass is typically a combination of oat, wheat, barley, and rye, and it's beneficial for your cat's digestion and overall health. The grass in your lawn isn't suitable for cats because of fertilizers and chemicals, so keep them away from it.

Your cat often seeks out grass when they have digestive trouble or feel nauseous, as it soothes their stomach. The chlorophyll in the plants can also freshen their breath if they throw up afterward. You can grow your own cat grass at home, which can regenerate itself when cut down and looks nice in a windowsill with water. You can also grow catnip, which is another plant cats enjoy eating and benefit from.

Growing cat grass provides enrichment for your cat, gives them an activity to engage in, and supports their digestion. It's a fun and interactive way to engage with your cat and can supplement other forms of enrichment like playtime.

## Knocking Things Over

Your cat loves knocking things over—like items on shelves—because they're curious and want to see what happens when they push something off, which is why they often repeat the action. This behavior can also indicate boredom. Instead of getting upset, play with your cat to satisfy their curiosity and give them an outlet for their energy.

To prevent valuable items from breaking, museum putty is a helpful solution. It keeps objects in place without damaging them. Understand that your cat isn't being a jerk when they knock things over—they're acting out of curiosity or boredom. By providing adequate play and stimulation, you can reduce this behavior and redirect their energy toward toys and activities that satisfy their natural hunting instincts.

## Boxes

Cats are born with the knowledge that they need to spend time in an enclosed space to stay safe and alive, and boxes fit this requirement perfectly. Boxes provide a sense of security and a place to decompress—a safe zone where they can feel protected and have exit routes if needed. This is why even a cat with a fancy condo may still prefer to jump into a cardboard box.

You can make boxes even more enjoyable by adding toys, catnip, or crumpled paper inside. Use a box cutter to create tunnels, forts, and holes, which allows multiple cats to play together and poke at each other through the openings. Let your cat have fun in a box before throwing it away to get the most use out of it. The joy of watching your cat discover a box and have the time of their life is a significant part of the cat-owner experience.
