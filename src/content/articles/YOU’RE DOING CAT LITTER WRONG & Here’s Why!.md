## How Cat Litter Came to Be

Before the 1940s, most cats lived outdoors or had regular access to the outside world. Many were working cats on farms, partly because there was no good indoor solution for where they could eliminate. Sand was available as an option, but it had a terrible smell and wasn't practical.

In 1947, a neighbor asked a man named Edlo for sand for her cat's litter box. She had previously tried ashes from her fireplace, but her cat tracked them all over the house. Edlo gave her a composite of clay instead—a material that could soak up urine and hold its weight in water. This simple solution marked the beginning of the cat litter market. By 2018, cat litter alone accounted for $3.9 billion in sales in the US, not counting the additional products like boxes, scoops, liners, and automated systems that came with it.

## Prioritize Your Cat's Needs Over Convenience

The concept of cat litter has evolved to prioritize human convenience and aesthetic preferences over what cats actually need. Consider a homeowner with several cats who refused to place litter boxes in front of a beautiful picture window because she didn't want to compromise the view. Instead, she kept the boxes hidden away in a bathroom or garage. The result? A yellow stripe of cat urine on the curtain—the very thing she was trying to avoid.

Cats are naturally inclined to mark territory over large areas. When you use a litter box, your cat is actually doing you a favor by concentrating their elimination in one spot. It's not fair to suppress their demands for a suitable place to eliminate just to keep your home looking perfect.

Your cat needs a place where they can bury their waste and walk away from it. Every relationship requires compromise, and that includes the one you have with your cat. You may need to accept some litter on the floor or place boxes in locations you'd prefer to hide them. The trade-off is a happier, healthier cat and fewer behavioral problems.

## Avoid Crystal Litter

Crystal litter is uncomfortable for cats. In nature, cats would choose to urinate in topsoil, not on a surface like lava rock. When you use crystal litter, your cat has to step on something hard and unnatural every time they use the box. Crystal litter is chosen mainly for its absorbency and odor control benefits for you—not because it's better for your cat. The texture may actually discourage them from using the litter box at all.

## Avoid Heavily Scented Litter

This is even more important than avoiding crystals. When you smell a deodorizer or flowery scent in cat litter, it might be appealing to you, but remember: your cat's sense of smell is far more sensitive than yours. When your cat puts their nose into the litter box to bury their waste, that scent is overwhelming to them. Scented litter is designed to make the litter more pleasant for humans, not for cats. Stick with unscented litter that doesn't assault your cat's sensitive nose.

## Skip These Litter Box Accessories

**Liners** are problematic because when cats try to bury and scratch, their claws get stuck in the plastic. You'll notice small cuts at the bottom of liners if they've been used with a cat.

**Cabinets and decorative boxes** that hide the litter aren't recommended. Some people even cut litter boxes into the bottoms of planters with palm trees on top to conceal them. These don't benefit your cat.

**Lids on litter boxes** create problems too. Many people think cats need privacy when eliminating, but this isn't true. Feral cats don't have private areas to use the bathroom, and they manage fine. The idea that cats need privacy is something people tell themselves because they want it to be true—not because it's based on cat behavior.

A lid or a litter box in a closet with a cutaway door can actually create a stressful situation for your cat. When they exit the litter box, they may feel vulnerable to other animals or children. The real reason people use these designs is to hide the litter box out of sight so they don't have to look at it or smell it. That's a human preference, not a cat need.

## Don't Use Self-Cleaning Litter Boxes

Self-cleaning boxes seem convenient, but they prevent you from monitoring your cat's health. When waste is automatically flushed away or cleaned, you miss important warning signs like diarrhea or blood in your cat's urine or stool. Dog owners, by contrast, have to pick up after their dogs and immediately notice any health issues, which prompts them to take their dog to the vet right away.

Cats are skilled at hiding their pain and illness. It's essential that you stay aware of changes in your cat's elimination habits, but a self-cleaning box makes this nearly impossible. The fancy litters and unique box designs you see are often more about making life easier for the owner than helping the cat.

## Don't Toilet Train Your Cat

Cats are not meant to poop in water. Naturally, they go in dirt and sand and cover their waste—this behavior is almost hardwired into their DNA. They learn it from their mothers at a young age, and it's essential for their mental well-being.

Toilet training involves a special device that fits over the toilet bowl and gradually reduces the amount of litter over months. Even if you succeed, it's uncertain and can take a very long time. Plus, you'd have to remove the training device every time you need to use the toilet. A litter box with litter is far more practical and allows your cat to exhibit their natural behavior without complicated, potentially unsuccessful training methods.

## The Ideal Setup

The best litter box setup is simple: use an unscented, smooth substrate that's easy for your cat to step on. The box should have no cover, no liners—just a plain box with litter in it. Place multiple boxes around your house so your cat always has easy access to one.

This setup prevents your cat from peeing on things or acting out in other ways. Your cat will have a sense of security and well-being that's essential for their happiness. The goal isn't to turn your entire home into a litter box area, but to find a middle ground that works for both you and your cats. This might mean compromising on placement or accepting that litter boxes need to be visible and accessible. When you show this kind of care and consideration for your cat's basic needs, you build a healthier and stronger relationship.
