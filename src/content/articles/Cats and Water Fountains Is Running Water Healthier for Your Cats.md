## Understanding Your Cat's Hydration Needs

Your cat needs water to stay hydrated, just like all other living beings. This is especially important because cats descended from desert animals. Even though their ancestors evolved to conserve water, your modern cat still needs reliable access to fresh water every day.

Your cat's wild ancestors got most of their hydration from the food they ate. Mice, crickets, and voles are about 80 percent water, so cats would naturally consume a lot of water through their prey. Today, if your cat eats a raw or wet diet, they'll get about 75 to 80 percent of their daily hydration from their food. If you feed your cat a dry diet, they need to make up that missing water by drinking from a bowl or fountain.

## How Much Water Should Your Cat Drink?

A good guideline is to provide at least three and a half ounces of water per five pounds of body weight. This means a 10-pound cat needs around eight ounces of water per day. Keep in mind that your cat will hopefully get some additional hydration from their food, so this formula is meant to work alongside their diet, not replace it.

## Why Cats Love Running Water

Your cat is likely drawn to running water, which is why they may try to drink from the tap when you leave it on. This instinct comes from their survival ancestors, who learned that running water contains less bacteria than stagnant water. By seeking out flowing water, your cat is following an ancient survival strategy.

## The Benefits of Water Fountains

A water fountain can be one of the most effective ways to encourage your cat to drink more. Fountains appeal to your cat's natural preferences in several ways. They allow your cat to engage multiple senses: hearing the sound of running water, smelling the clean source, and seeing the visually appealing effect of water falling.

Fountains also address an issue called whisker stress. When cats drink from a regular bowl, they can feel the edges of the bowl against their whiskers, which triggers a natural defense mechanism that warns them of potential danger. Most fountains are designed to be more open, reducing this stress and making drinking more comfortable.

Fountains are especially beneficial for senior cats. They encourage fluid intake, which is crucial for flushing out your cat's system and helping to manage conditions like kidney disease or lower urinary tract disorders.

## Choosing the Right Bowl or Fountain

The material you choose matters. Stainless steel, glass, and ceramic are all excellent choices because they're non-porous and don't have small nooks and crannies where bacteria can hide. This reduces the risk of bacterial growth.

Avoid plastic bowls and fountains. Plastic is porous, which means bacteria can grow in its tiny crevices. This can lead to problems like chin acne in cats. Stick with the non-porous options for the cleanest, safest water source.

## Getting Your Cat to Drink More

If you're feeding your cat dry food, consider adding a small amount of warm water to wet food to increase their overall hydration. You can also offer a raw or wet diet, which naturally provides more water as part of the meal.

The best approach is to give your cat multiple water options. Have one or two fountains in your home to take advantage of their natural attraction to running water, but also keep at least one still water source available. Some cats prefer different options at different times, so variety can help ensure they're staying hydrated.

Ultimately, the goal is to provide your cat with constant access to water—whether through a fountain, a still bowl, or even a glass—to keep them healthy and well-hydrated throughout their life.
