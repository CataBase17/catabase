## Before the Move

Moving is stressful for both you and your cat. The key to a smooth transition is preparation, and getting this phase right will prevent long-term stress and confidence issues for your cat in the new space.

### Setting Up Base Camp

The best preparation you can make is to establish a great base camp by gathering all the items your cat will need. Focus on what's called "scent soakers"—things like cat trees, cat beds, blankets, and litter boxes that absorb and hold your cat's scent.

Before the move, start using these items so they become saturated with your cat's familiar smell. Old sweatshirts that your cat likes to lay on are perfect for this; place them inside one of their favorite cat beds. The goal is to create a space that feels like home because when your cat smells something, it feels like they own it and it signifies home.

For litter boxes specifically, follow the general rule of one litter box per cat, plus one extra. Introduce these new boxes to your cat's environment well before moving day so they become familiar with them and develop a sense of ownership and comfort.

### Expanding Your Cat's Food Palate

Cats under stress often shut down and lose their appetite, which can be dangerous. If a cat doesn't eat for 24 hours, they can develop fatty liver disease, a potentially fatal condition. To prevent this, start expanding your cat's palate before the move by rotating different types of canned food. This way, your cat will be accustomed to various flavors by the time moving day arrives.

Identify some "jackpot treats"—foods that are irresistible to your cat—and withhold them for about a week before the move. This makes them extra special incentives that can encourage eating in the new location. Having a variety of flavors and treats on hand will help your cat feel more at home and reduce the stress of the move.

### Making the Carrier a Positive Space

Most cats associate the carrier with stressful trips to the vet. To create a positive association before moving day, make the carrier a comfortable, inviting place your cat actually wants to spend time.

Start by removing the top and door of the carrier, then add a comfy bed inside—ideally one that already smells like your cat. Use jackpot treats to encourage your cat to enter willingly. Once your cat is comfortable spending time in this semi-covered bed space, you can gradually sneak the top back on and eventually close the door. The goal is for the carrier to feel like a secure, calm space surrounded by familiar smells, so when it's time to move, your cat will be less stressed even when the door is closed.

### Getting Your Cat Microchipped

If your cat isn't already microchipped, get it done before the move. If your cat is already microchipped, contact the microchip provider to update the information with your new address. Do this before moving day in case something happens during the relocation process.

### Making Boxes Fun

Cats love boxes, so turn this to your advantage. Make boxes fun zones by filling them with crinkled packing paper, ball toys, and other entertaining items. By introducing boxes in a playful way before moving day, your cat will already be familiar with them and associate them with positive experiences, which reduces the stress associated with moving.

## During the Move

The "during" phase begins about two days before your move.

### Setting Up a Temporary Base Camp

On moving day, designate a room in your current home as a temporary base camp for your cat—ideally a spare room that can be easily packed out, not your master bedroom. This room should contain all of your cat's essential items, including scent soakers, and should be packed up with boxes and items moved out to make space.

Once the movers arrive, keep your cat in this base camp room with the door closed. Put a clear warning sign on the door to remind others not to open it. With the front door open during the move, there's a high risk of your cat running out, so this secure space is critical.

### Transporting the Litter Box

Consider that your cat has a strong connection to their territory—marked by chairs, beds, couches, and especially the litter box. Take the litter boxes with you when moving, and don't sanitize or clean them with detergent. Preserving your cat's scent in the litter box is vital for their comfort.

If you're driving, you can triple-bag the litter or place it in multiple garbage bags to minimize smell during transport. Setting up the litter box with the cat's litter in it at the new location will help your cat adjust energetically and mentally, and get over the stress of moving.

### Managing Stress During Transport

Sedatives may help for the first couple of hours of travel, but they may need to be re-administered, especially on long trips, which can be complicated. A better alternative is flower essence remedies like Easy Traveler and Stress Stopper, which can be used together to help your cat feel more at ease. These are preferable to medication if your cat isn't severely stressed.

Before moving day, test any pheromone sprays, plug-ins, or other remedies you plan to use to ensure your cat doesn't have an adverse reaction. Don't introduce new products for the first time on moving day.

## After You Arrive

### Securing the New Space

Before letting your cat out of the carrier, ensure the screens are secure and there are no escape routes. Losing your cat in the first couple of days of a move is a serious risk.

If your new place has carpets and the previous owners had animals, use a black light to detect any remnants of pet urine on the carpets. Urine will fluoresce under a black light in various colors, indicating problem areas. Use an enzymatic odor remover to eliminate any lingering smells that might bother your cat and create behavioral issues.

### Setting Up the New Base Camp

Upon arrival, set up a new base camp for your cat in a quiet room, ideally the bedroom. Include their carriers and familiar items, food and water, and everything they need to decompress. You don't need to quarantine your cat away from you, but providing a quiet, comfortable space to adjust reduces stress and helps them settle in faster.

## After the First 24 Hours

Within the first 24 hours, your cat should start to feel more comfortable. Keep their feeding schedule the same to provide a sense of stability.

### Gradually Expand the Base Camp

Once your cat appears settled, begin moving some of the familiar objects and belongings to other areas of the house. These serve as signposts that mark your cat's territory and provide familiarity. A cat tree placed in a window allows your cat to explore the new neighborhood from a safe location. Carriers can be used as instant beds and placed around the house for additional comfort.

Rotate objects and belongings around the house so everything gradually becomes saturated with your cat's scent and feels like their territory. Prioritize your cat's adjustment over aesthetic considerations—delay getting rid of old scratchers or trees until your cat feels fully comfortable and secure.

### Restore Your Rituals

Play with your cat regularly. These rituals are crucial for their adjustment to the new environment and will help both of you feel better. Committing to these activities helps your cat feel like they own the space and makes the new place feel like home.

This is also a good opportunity to design the space to meet both your needs and your cat's. Provide environmental enrichment through cat trees, shelves, and other climbing opportunities. Rather than just helping your cat survive the move, focus on helping them thrive in the new environment. Use this fresh start to implement projects and ideas you may have been putting off, creating a better life for both of you.
