## Why Your Cat Might Have a Favorite Person

It's completely normal to feel a little hurt if your cat seems to prefer spending time with your partner over you. Cats do form attachments to their humans, and sometimes it looks like they're playing favorites—especially in households with multiple people.

## Understanding What's Really Happening

Your cat's preference for one person doesn't necessarily mean they don't love you. Waffle, a five-month-old cat, was showing a strong preference for his owner Jeremy's lap despite spending more time overall with Daniela, who works from home. Even though they shared feeding duties and playtime together, Waffle kept choosing Jeremy's lap.

The key insight is that your cat's behavior might not be about love at all—it could be about territory and habit. Cats are creatures of routine and ownership. They settle into certain spots and patterns, and these can shift over time. If your cat consistently gravitates toward one person, pay attention to where that person is sitting. Your cat might be claiming a particular spot on the couch rather than showing a preference for the person themselves.

## What to Look For in the Preferred Person

Watch how your partner interacts with your cat without judgment. The answer often lies in the details. Your partner might be:

- Petting your cat less frequently, which prevents overstimulation
- Using a gentle petting technique that calms your cat
- Sitting in a specific, comfortable location your cat loves
- Moving around less, providing a calmer, more settled environment

The goal is to be a detective rather than a participant. Observe objectively what your partner is doing differently, then try incorporating those same techniques into your interactions.

## Mixing Things Up

One practical solution is to change your seating arrangements. Cats are territorial, and they often bond with specific spots before they bond with people. By rotating where you and your partner sit, you can help your cat form new associations and spend time in different areas with different people. This doesn't require treats or bribery—it's simply about breaking the established pattern.

## Your Cat's Confidence Matters

A confident, well-adjusted cat will be secure enough to eventually spend time with multiple people. If your cat is showing the kind of confidence that comes from being settled in their home (like being harness-trained and comfortable exploring), that's actually a good sign. Your cat isn't avoiding anyone—they're just following their natural preferences right now.

## What Changes Over Time

Remember that your cat's preferences aren't permanent. Cats shift their allegiances and favorite spots as they grow and become more comfortable. Two months is still early in your relationship with a new cat. As they settle in more deeply, their behavior will naturally evolve. Keep being consistent, patient, and positive, and your bond with your cat will deepen.
