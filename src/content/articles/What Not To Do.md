## Don't Feed Your Cat Milk

You've probably seen the classic image of a cat lapping milk from a saucer, but this is actually a harmful misconception. Most cats are lactose intolerant, meaning they can't properly digest lactose, the sugar found in milk. This can lead to serious digestive problems, including bad stools and other gastrointestinal issues.

Cats may be attracted to dairy products like milk, ice cream, or whipped cream because of their high fat content, but consuming these foods can negatively affect their health. To keep your cat healthy, avoid giving her any milk or dairy products.

## Don't Skip Annual Vet Visits

Many cat owners skip annual veterinary check-ups, unlike dog owners who are more diligent about this. While your cat may hate the stress of being placed in her carrier, this shouldn't be a reason to avoid these important visits.

As cats age, especially past seven or eight years old, they can experience rapid physical changes that require professional monitoring. Annual vet visits help identify important health issues early, such as dental problems including tartar buildup and tooth decay that may require removal. Regular check-ups are just as important for cats as they are for children—they're essential to catching health problems before they become serious.

## Don't Dress Your Cat Up

It might seem adorable to put your cat in a taco costume or a melon hat, but most cats do not enjoy wearing clothes or costumes. Cats have highly sensitive fur and whiskers all over their body, so the pressure of wearing clothing is uncomfortable and physically unpleasant for them. If given the choice, your cat would almost certainly prefer to go without. While some cats may appear calm in costumes like bow ties or top hats, this is rarely because they actually enjoy it.

## Don't Force Interactions

Cats have a personal bubble, and forcing interactions with them can damage your relationship. Your cat may not always want to be touched or petted, and when you ignore her boundaries, she may become overstimulated, run away, or even bite.

Some cats tolerate petting for only a few strokes before they've had enough. It's essential to respect these individual preferences and stop when your cat signals she's done. Instead of forcing affection, let your cat initiate interactions on her own terms. You can use treats to encourage her to come to you, but wait for her to make the first move.

When you allow your cat to approach you without pressure, she'll associate you with positive experiences like receiving treats. This strengthens your bond, and she'll be more likely to rub against you and show affection naturally. Mutual decision-making in interactions builds trust and creates a healthier, more positive relationship.

## Don't Punish

Punishing your cat is destructive to your relationship and doesn't actually change her behavior. Methods like using a squirt bottle, yelling, or physical contact such as patting, spanking, or flicking are all ineffective and create fear instead of understanding.

When you use a squirt bottle, your cat may associate the bottle—or even you—with fear rather than learning not to exhibit the unwanted behavior. Over time, she may also simply stop reacting to it. Similarly, yelling confuses your cat because it's the same voice you use to express love and affection, so she doesn't understand what you're correcting.

If you need to discourage a behavior, any correction must be consistent, happen immediately after the unwanted behavior, and be applied with the same intensity every time. For example, an air canister can be used to deter jumping on certain surfaces. However, the most effective approach is positive reinforcement, which builds trust and strengthens your relationship rather than damaging it.

## Don't Do Things Your Cat Wouldn't Want

Never create content or situations involving doing things to your cat that she wouldn't choose for herself. This includes dressing her up, surprising her with cucumbers, or bathing her for entertainment purposes. These actions abuse the trust in your relationship.

While it's fine to capture your cat in naturally fun, sleepy, or playful positions she's already in, you should never put her in situations that cause distress or discomfort just for entertainment or social media content.

## Don't Declaw Your Cat

Declawing is mutilation—it's not just trimming back the claws, but actually de-knuckling, removing the last bone in each toe. The severity of this procedure is why over 27 countries have outlawed it.

Declawing often causes long-term behavioral problems, including refusal to use the litter box and increased biting. What seems like a quick solution frequently leads to cats being surrendered to shelters because of these serious side effects.

Fortunately, there are many humane alternatives. Nail caps can be placed over your cat's claws, and trimming her nails every couple of weeks reduces damage from scratching. Providing appropriate scratching posts and pads redirects her natural scratching behavior away from furniture. Behavioral techniques like using sticky tape on furniture surfaces can also be effective deterrents.

If your cat is scratching furniture or endangering babies, work with resources and behavioral guidance to find solutions that work for both you and your cat—without resorting to this harmful practice.
