## Why Your Kitten Bites and Attacks Your Hands

Your four-month-old kitten is biting because of their natural hunting instinct, not because something is wrong. At this age, kittens are driven to seek out toys and have fun, but they often target the wrong things—like your hands and feet—even when you're just sitting quietly. This is completely normal kitten behavior. The challenge is teaching your kitten what is and isn't a suitable target for play.

## Create a Feeding Schedule

Kittens don't follow a predictable body clock until around seven months old. Before that, their sleep patterns are unpredictable, with frequent waking and sleeping throughout the day. To help establish a rhythm, feed your kitten meals at set times rather than leaving food out for free feeding. Feeding three to four times a day at specific times helps regulate their energy levels and sleep patterns. Once your kitten settles into a feeding schedule, their sleeping and energy bursts will start to align with your daily routine, making them much easier to manage.

## Establish a Play Schedule

Use interactive toys like wand toys with feathers to get your kitten moving and tired. A technique called "boil and simmer" involves building up playtime intensity and then winding it down. Follow active play sessions with a meal—this creates a natural rhythm where your kitten plays, eats, and then rests. Start your day by playing together when you wake up, then offer a meal. This predictable pattern of activity, food, and rest helps calm hyperactive behavior and prevents constant attacking.

## Recognize the Warning Signs

Learn to read your kitten's body language before they pounce. When your kitten is getting ready to attack, you'll notice them getting low to the ground, wiggling their hindquarters, squishing their tail, and staring intently at their target. These are signals that they're about to strike. When you see these signs, have toys ready to redirect their attention instead.

## Keep Toys Handy and Varied

Keep a variety of toys available, including wand toys, remote toys like balls or fuzzy mice, and crinkle paper. Putting toys away after playtime makes them feel special and exciting when you bring them out again. When your kitten starts zeroing in on your hand or foot, immediately offer a toy to redirect them. You can also use a laser pointer as a last resort to guide your kitten away from something they shouldn't be playing with, but interactive toys are better because they let you actively play and help your kitten expend energy.

## Consider Getting a Second Kitten

One of the most effective ways to manage a hyper kitten is to get another kitten around the same age. Kittens naturally teach each other boundaries and appropriate play behavior. They wear each other out and keep each other company throughout the day, which is more natural for them than being alone waiting for you to play. Introducing a second kitten when your kitten is around 16 weeks old is ideal—at this age, your kitten will be very accepting of a companion. Two kittens together lead to a happier, more balanced home, and they'll have a lifelong bond that gives them comfort, play, and companionship when you're busy or away.
