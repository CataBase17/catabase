## Why Your New Cat Is Hiding

When you bring home a newly adopted cat, especially one around eight months old, it's completely normal for them not to immediately feel confident in their new space. Your cat won't necessarily "hit the ground running"—they need time to adjust, and that's okay.

Hiding is a common response to the stress of relocation. A new home means unfamiliar territory, strange smells, sights, and sounds. Your cat's personality matters too. If your cat is naturally shy or has had a difficult past—perhaps time in a shelter or on the street—they may be extra cautious during the adjustment period. Hiding under the bed or furniture is actually their way of coping and feeling safe while they process everything that's changed.

The good news: even if your cat is hiding a lot, positive signs tell you things are moving in the right direction. If your cat is eating and drinking normally, playing occasionally, exploring at night when you're not around, and watching the world from a high spot, these are all victories worth celebrating.

## Setting Up a Base Camp

Creating a dedicated "base camp" for your cat is one of the best things you can do. This should be a contained area—ideally one room—with everything your cat needs: food and water bowls, toys, scratching posts, a litter box, hiding spots, and at least one elevated perch or bed where they can watch the world.

Don't worry about opening the door to the rest of your home right away. Keeping your cat in this base camp for a while helps them bond with you as their guide, rather than becoming too attached to the territory itself. Your cat may need weeks in this space before they're brave enough to explore confidently. Let them move at their own pace.

High perches are especially valuable. Even if your cat doesn't use a tall cat tree immediately, keep it there. As your cat becomes more comfortable, they often claim these spaces as their own observation posts.

## Building Trust: When Your Cat Runs from You

If your cat tries to escape when you approach or walks into the room, understand that this is about trust, not about the space itself. Your cat is fearful of humans right now, but that can change.

Start by getting down to your cat's level. Standing over a scared cat is intimidating. Sit on the floor or bring a chair to their area so you're closer to eye level. This simple change makes a huge difference in how threatening you seem.

Next, use food as an incentive. The way to a cat's heart often goes through their stomach. When your cat's nose starts working, everything else follows. Hold something tasty and smelly—wet food, treats, or a small piece of chicken—and let your cat's natural curiosity take over.

If your cat won't take treats directly from your hand, don't push it. Instead, drop treats near their hiding spot or in their bed. This creates a positive association: when your hand comes near, good things happen. Repeat this daily.

If treats alone don't work, try mealtime. Place their regular food near their hiding place, then sit on the opposite side of the room. Use a calm, soothing voice. Don't stare at your cat—that can feel threatening. Let them eat in peace while you're simply present.

You can also provide portable hiding spots your cat can claim as their own, like their carrier with familiar bedding inside, or a covered bed. Knowing they have a secure retreat available makes it easier for them to venture out a little.

## Small Steps Forward

Progress with a scared cat happens in what might feel like millimeter increments. A five-minute play session is a huge win. Your cat exploring at night and using a window bed to watch outside—these are significant victories. Celebrate them.

The goal is to gently encourage your cat to take small steps beyond their comfort zone. If they're hiding under the bed, maybe the next win is getting them to rest on the bed itself. Then perhaps a spot near the bed. Then a few steps toward the middle of the room to reach a safe spot. Each tiny movement forward is progress.

This isn't a linear process. Some days your cat will seem braver; other days they'll retreat again. That's normal. Think of it like a roller coaster where small victories gradually build until a real breakthrough happens.

Patience, love, and a little bribery work together. Keep challenging your cat gently and gradually, always respecting what their body language tells you. If your cat seems shut down, that's a signal to slow down and give them more time.

The commitment and care you show now—prioritizing your cat's well-being and feelings—will build the strong bond that leads to a wonderful life together. Your cat will get there.
