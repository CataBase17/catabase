## Dangerous Foods

Cats are sensitive to many common household foods and substances. Bubble gum, beer, and coffee can all be harmful if your cat accidentally gets into them. Caffeine in particular is toxic to cats—even if it seems cute when your cat dunks their head into your coffee cup, it's actually dangerous. Make sure to cat-proof your home by keeping these items securely stored and out of reach.

## Cords

Electrical cords pose a serious hazard because cats can bite into them and get electrocuted, or ingest the rubberized coating, which can cause internal damage. Protect electrical cords—especially computer cords—by running them through PVC tubing. You can buy cord proofing kits or find PVC tubing at hardware stores.

Blind cords are equally dangerous. The strings that hang from blinds can strangle a cat if they get wrapped around the neck. Gather and knot the cords tightly at the top, out of your cat's reach. Alternatively, you can switch to cordless blinds to eliminate this hazard entirely.

## Bags

Plastic bags are a suffocation hazard, especially if your cat likes to crawl inside them or play with them. Store plastic bags securely where your cat can't access them. This is especially important if your cat has pica, an obsessive-compulsive disorder where cats are drawn to eating foreign materials like plastic.

Paper bags with strong handles also pose a risk, particularly for younger cats. A cat can get their head stuck in the handle, which can cause panic and injury. Remove the handles from paper bags before storing or discarding them.

## Household Chemicals

Cabinets containing household cleaners—window cleaners, bleach, laundry detergent—should be secured with child safety locks since cats are curious and may ingest these toxic substances. Don't rely on a cat simply avoiding something; use locks to prevent access entirely.

Antifreeze is especially dangerous because it has a sweet taste that attracts cats and dogs, but it's fatal if ingested. Check your garage and driveway carefully to ensure there's no antifreeze on the ground where your cat could come into contact with it.

Keep fertilizers, insecticides, and other lawn care products in airtight containers stored far away from your cat. Rat and mouse poison is also hazardous because cats may eat poisoned rodents. Consider using humane alternatives like relocation traps instead.

Prescription and over-the-counter medications, including antihistamines, vitamins, cold remedies, THC, and CBD, should all be kept in locked cabinets. Use child safety locks under the sink and on medicine cabinets. Remember that cats have a unique digestive system designed only for their natural diet, so many human substances are toxic to them. Vitamins formulated for adults and gummy vitamins in particular can be harmful. Anything potent enough to affect a human can have an even stronger effect on a cat.

## Ingestion Hazards

Ribbons, strings, and yarn are especially dangerous during the holidays. These can wrap around your cat's intestines, causing serious internal damage. Keep these items securely put away. The same applies to rubber bands, hair ties, and scrunchies—cats find these tempting to chew and swallow.

String and feather toys can be hazardous if left unattended. Always put these toys away after playtime rather than leaving them out. Be cautious about cheap cat toys with small parts like button eyes or long yarn tails—these can be easily ingested. Choose toys made from biodegradable materials instead. Avoid toys with mylar, which is not safe for cats to eat.

Sharp or small objects pose choking hazards or can puncture your cat's digestive system. Keep razors, needles, and pine needles (especially from Christmas trees) out of reach. Like toddlers, cats are prone to putting anything in their mouths.

## Furniture

Unsecured furniture can fall and cause serious injury, especially in earthquake-prone areas. Use earthquake straps to secure tall furniture to the wall. For items on shelves that could fall and break, use museum putty on the bottom to keep them in place.

Furniture with moving parts is particularly hazardous for cats. Recliners and automatic beds can trap or injure a cat, especially smaller ones, if the cat climbs inside. Regular couches and chairs with fabric openings in the back can also trap or strangle a cat—seal these openings to prevent accidents.

The refrigerator is another furniture hazard. Younger cats are attracted to the food smell and may jump inside. Always check that your cat isn't in the fridge before closing the door.

## Heat Sources

Cats are naturally drawn to heat sources because they're not great at regulating their own body temperature. You'll find them seeking warmth from the sun, dryers, washing machines, and dishwashers.

Fireplaces are hazardous because flames have a hypnotic effect on cats. Make sure the grill in front of the fireplace is secure and supervise your cat when the fireplace is in use.

Candles are also dangerous heat sources. Your cat may try to play with the flames, knock over the candle, and potentially start a house fire. It's best to keep lit candles out of reach entirely.

Car engines generate heat that attracts cats, but the fan belt can severely injure a cat if the engine starts while your cat is seeking warmth. Before starting your car, bang on the hood to scare away any cats hiding underneath.

Ovens and stoves pose burn and fire hazards. Cats may try to jump onto the stove or climb into the oven, potentially burning themselves or turning on burners. Use burner covers and stove locks to prevent these accidents.

## Toxic Plants

Many common houseplants are toxic to cats if ingested. These include spiky plants like cacti and aloe, as well as daisies, chrysanthemums, lilies, holly, and mistletoe. The ASPCA maintains a comprehensive list of toxic plants you can consult.

Keep plants out of your cat's reach by hanging them or placing them in inaccessible areas. Provide your cat with an alternative to chew on, such as cat grass, to distract them from toxic plants and reduce the risk of poisoning.

## Creating a Safe Home

Your home should be designed so you can easily locate your cat in case of an emergency. Know where your cat likes to hide—both in high places and low spaces. Take a critical look at your entire home to identify potential hazards and take steps to eliminate them. By being aware of these dangers and taking precautions, you can help ensure your cat lives a long, healthy life.
