## Food Variety

Feeding your cat the same food every day can lead to food intolerances and allergies. Offering a variety of foods instead can instantly make your cat happier—most cats would naturally choose a diverse diet over eating the same thing repeatedly.

A lack of food variety can be a breeding ground for these problems. This becomes especially important if your cat ever ends up in a shelter, rescue, or foster situation. The stress of a new environment combined with an unfamiliar diet can cause your cat to stop eating entirely. By introducing variety now, you're building their resilience.

It's also recommended to feed your cat a wet or raw diet rather than dry food, as this can lead to a longer and healthier life. You don't need to stick with one type of food every day—mixing things up is genuinely beneficial for your cat's health and well-being.

## Spreading Out Resources

Your cat can be instantly happier when you spread out their resources throughout your home. This includes food, water, scent soakers, beds, cat trees, and the litter box. These items act as territorial signposts for your cat, similar to how you might display family photos on a mantle. They validate your cat's existence and provide a sense of ownership and security.

Cat trees should be placed in socially significant areas and spread around the house rather than confined to a single "cat room." This helps your cat feel like they own multiple parts of their territory. Food and litter boxes should never be placed near each other—cats have a natural need for a personal bubble around their eating area and don't want to eliminate where they eat. By spacing out these resources, you increase your cat's confidence and satisfy their wild instincts.

## Litter Boxes

The ideal number of litter boxes follows a simple formula: have one more litter box than the number of cats. So for two cats, provide three litter boxes.

Avoid clustering all the litter boxes in one area like the garage or mud room. Instead, place them in socially significant areas throughout your home, such as the living room. This helps your cat feel more secure and confident by allowing them to complement their scent with yours, creating a sense of communal scent and territorial security. Cats value this territorial security—they often sit in your favorite spots and leave their scent behind because it makes them feel like companions in a shared space.

## Whisker Stress

Whisker stress is a real issue that affects many cats. It occurs when their whiskers are bent or disturbed, which commonly happens when they eat from deep or narrow bowls that fold their whiskers in half.

Your cat's whiskers are highly sensitive, with their own blood supply and nerve endings. They help your cat detect air movements, sense temperature, and navigate their surroundings. The width of your cat's whiskers is roughly the same as the width of their body, helping them determine if they can fit into tight spaces.

Watch for signs of whisker stress: your cat avoiding their food dish, taking a few bites and walking away, or pushing food onto the ground to eat from there. To fix this, use wide, shallow bowls that keep your cat's whiskers untouched while eating. Alternatively, feed your cat on a flat plate, which can reduce the stress caused by bent whiskers. A plate may require some adjustment to keep food from being pushed off, but it's worth trying.

## Trimming Claws and Giving Medicine

Two difficult but essential tasks are trimming your cat's claws and giving them medicine. Failing to trim claws properly can lead to health issues—overgrown claws curve back into the paw or get caught in fibers.

Cats can become aggressive when their claws aren't trimmed, but this is often due to the owner's fear and stress about the process. Starting when your cat is young and using positive associations with rewards helps tremendously. The energy and stress you bring to these tasks can be sensed by your cat, making the experience harder. Approach these tasks calmly and confidently, without apologizing or showing hesitation.

When trimming claws, touch your cat's paw and wait for the claw to come out. Then trim just the tip. Cat claws are transparent, so you can see the blood supply and avoid cutting too far. For giving medicine, you can open your cat's mouth and offer a treat—save special treats just for when your cat needs medicine. A methodical, slow approach with calm, positive energy makes all the difference.

## Cat-Proofing Your Home

Cat-proofing your house can immediately make your cat's life longer. Think of your cat like a toddler—but one that can reach the ceiling. Everything in your home needs careful examination to ensure their safety.

Pay special attention to storing cleaners and toxic foods safely out of reach, removing toxic plants, securing banisters that your cat could fall from, and checking window screens that can be easily pushed out. Candles are also a hazard. Your house contains many potential threats, and identifying and removing these hazards is crucial to helping your cat live longer.

## Playing With Your Cat

Playing with your cat can instantly improve their life. Using an interactive toy like a wand or string with a feather gives you control over the experience and connects you with your cat's natural hunting instincts. You can mimic both air prey and ground prey with these toys.

Even a simple toy—like a stick and a sock—used daily can make a significant difference. Regular play raises confidence in shy cats and can calm aggressive ones. Engaging your cat in hunting, pouncing, grabbing, and wrestling with a toy for just a few minutes each day has a real positive impact on their behavior and confidence. Play helps bring out your cat's natural personality and can be a fun and rewarding experience for both of you.
