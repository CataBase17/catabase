## What Is a Head Bonk?

A head bonk—also called a head bunt, head butt, head bop, bang, or bash—is when your cat pushes its head against your head, face, or mouth with a bit of force. Despite the aggressive-sounding name, it's actually a loving gesture. Your cat might crawl onto your chest and give you a head bonk, leaving behind some cat hair and showing you affection in a very physical way.

## Why Your Cat Head Bonks: Scent Marking

Your cat has scent glands in its forehead and all over its body. Scent is crucial to how cats understand and navigate their world. When your cat head bonks you, it's depositing its scent on you—a way of marking you as part of its territory and community.

Cats mark their scent in several ways. Beyond head bonking, they use flank rubs (rubbing their sides against you), face rubbing, and clawing. They often target areas with strong human scent, like couches and beds, for this scent soaking behavior. It's driven by their instinct to claim ownership and create a sense of belonging.

You can help your cat feel secure by providing items that carry your scent. Try placing a worn t-shirt in your cat's carrier before a vet visit—it makes the space feel safer and more comforting.

Scent is also how cats greet each other. They use nose-to-nose interactions and shared scent to create community and belonging, which is essential to their social behavior.

## Allorubbing: Scent Sharing Between Cats

Allorubbing is when cats rub their bodies against each other to share scent. Head bonking, flank marking, and scratching are all forms of allorubbing between cats. While head bonking is typically a behavior cats reserve for other cats, they can also do it with other species—including you. Your cat might even head bonk your dog, which can be confusing for the dog but is a genuine sign of affection from your cat.

## Head Bonking as the Ultimate Show of Affection

When your cat head bonks you, it's showing tremendous trust. Think about what that gesture requires: your cat has to be completely vulnerable, close its eyes, lower its defenses, and bump its head against your face—the face of a predator from your cat's instinctive perspective. That's genuine affection without reservations.

Another example of this ultimate trust is when your cat lays on the floor, rolls over on its back, and exposes its belly—the most vulnerable part of its body. This is similar to what's sometimes called the "sea otter position." Just as people show affection differently depending on how well they know someone (a side hug for an acquaintance versus a full embrace for someone close), a head bonk represents affection without boundaries or holding back.

## Not All Cats Head Bonk

Every cat is different. Not all cats exhibit head bonking behavior, and that's completely normal. Some cats may have had limited exposure to other cats during kittenhood, which can result in fewer cat communication skills and different ways of expressing themselves. Your cat might show affection through other means entirely, and that's okay.

The circumstances matter too. Your cat might head bonk when it needs soothing or when expressing affection. The behavior depends on your individual cat's personality and your relationship with them.

## Head Pressing: When to Worry

Be aware of the difference between a friendly head bonk and head pressing. If your cat is walking up to a wall and pressing its head into it repeatedly, this could signal a health problem—possibly a headache, dental pain, or another issue. If you notice your cat doing this, take it to the vet for a check-up to rule out any medical concerns.
