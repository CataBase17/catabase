## Understanding Your Cat's Needs

Before you start cat-proofing, think about your cat as a curious toddler who can reach the ceiling and is naturally inclined to explore, climb, and investigate. The specifics of what you need to do depend entirely on your individual cat.

Kittens are naturally driven to explore and discover new territory. As they grow, their hunting instincts and investigative abilities increase. Some cats are explorers who are always finding new places to investigate. Others are the type who knock things off shelves just to watch them fall—what you might call a "Dennis the Menace" personality.

Senior cats face different challenges. Their physical function, sensory abilities, and cognitive abilities become compromised, making them more prone to slipping, sliding, and falling. This requires special consideration when planning their environment.

Special needs cats—whether they have physical disabilities, hearing impairments, or blindness—don't perceive themselves as limited. They move through the world in their own way and need accommodations to prevent accidents. Cats with pica, a condition where they eat things they shouldn't, pose a significant danger to themselves and require careful oversight to prevent them from ingesting harmful substances.

Consider which categories your cats fall into, as multiple cats in your home may have different needs. Understanding these factors is your starting point for effective cat-proofing.

## Large-Scale Design Considerations

Cat-proofing is about designing your entire house with your cats in mind, starting with major structural elements.

**Screens and doors** are your first line of defense. Front and back doors should have screens that can't be pushed out. Use heavy-duty pet screening to prevent cats from escaping or getting injured while trying to push through.

**Balconies** present a real temptation for cats. If your balcony can't be fully screened in, don't let your cats out on it. It's not worth the risk.

**Cords** around your home should be covered with hard plastic, bendable cord protectors. These can also be sprayed with bitter apple to deter your cat from chewing on them.

**In multi-level homes**, banisters and landings require attention. Cats may try to do a "tight wire walk" on railings. Install soft landings or netting underneath to break their fall if they slip.

**Stairs** should have carpet or non-slip surfaces to prevent slipping and falling. Blind cords should be tied up to prevent your cat from getting tangled or strangled.

**Lit candles** should be kept out of reach. Beyond the risk of singed whiskers and fur, a knocked-over candle could start a house fire. As you create elevated spaces, also make sure you can physically reach your cats in case of an emergency.

## The Bathroom

The bathroom is an obvious starting point because it contains several toxins. Medicines, toothpaste, and soap can all poison your cat. Keep the toilet lid down—cats can fall in and ingest harmful substances.

Other bathroom hazards include tissue paper treated with lanolin or perfumes, deodorizers, and aromatherapy sticks, which can be toxic if your cat ingests them.

## The Kitchen

The under-sink area is a major hazard since it typically contains toxic cleaners. The easiest solution is to install child locks on the cabinet.

The stove and oven also pose risks. Cats may jump onto the stove or try to get into the oven. Stove handle guards and stove top covers can prevent accidents.

The dishwasher, refrigerator, and garbage cans are additional concerns. They can contain food, toxic materials, and choking hazards. Secure these areas and always check them before use, especially the dishwasher and oven, since cats may hide inside.

## The Dining Room

Dining rooms often contain food, flowers, and plants that can be toxic to cats. Tablecloths with dangling items can tempt curious kittens to pounce and pull, potentially bringing hot dishes down on themselves.

## The Living Room

Decorative items on mantles and shelves are at risk of being knocked down by curious or playful cats. Use museum putty to secure decorative items in place. For heavier furniture like armoires, attach earthquake straps to the wall to prevent them from falling.

**Recliners** present a serious hazard. Cats like to explore under and inside furniture, and they can get trapped or killed when a recliner is closed. Seal off any gaps or openings.

**Couches and chairs** with gaps or openings can trap your cat. They may become hung up on threads or get stuck in ways that require furniture to be torn apart to rescue them.

**Underbeds** are another hiding spot that can become a problem, especially in emergencies when you need to quickly access your cat. If your cat likes to hide under the bed, consider switching to a platform bed as a long-term solution.

## The Laundry Room

Cats may try to climb into the washer or dryer to make nests or escape danger. Always keep these appliances closed and check them before use. Dryer sheets, fabric softener, detergent residue, and the smell of soiled items can all be problematic for cats.

## Small Items and Crafting Materials

Small items pose surprising dangers. Rubber bands, thumb tacks, paper clips, and staples can all harm your cat. In homes with children, deflated balloons with strings and ribbons add to the hazard.

During holidays and after birthday parties, clean up ribbons and mylar decorations. Christmas trees and their decorations require similar caution.

Crafting materials are particularly dangerous. Needles and thread can wrap around your cat's intestinal tract and cause serious internal damage. Store these items safely and develop a habit of putting them away immediately after use, even if it means breaking old habits.

## Creating a Safe Environment

Cat-proofing your home requires attention to detail and consideration of every potential hazard throughout every room and level. The goal is to create an environment where you can relax knowing your curious, climbing, exploring cat is safe.
