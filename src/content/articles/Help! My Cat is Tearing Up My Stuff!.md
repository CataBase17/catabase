## Why Your Cat Is Attacking Your Shoes

If your cat has developed a strong preference for playing with and chewing your shoes instead of toys you've bought, you're not alone—and there's a good reason it's happening.

Your cat is drawn to your shoes because of their scent. Cats experience the world primarily through smell, which is far more important to them than sight or hearing. When your cat smells your shoes, they're recognizing your scent and using the shoes to claim ownership and mark territory. This is similar to how cats rub against beds, couches, and other items in your home—they're creating a scent map and feeling connected to you through that smell.

Your cat isn't just playing with the shoe as a toy; they're engaging in natural hunting and kicking behaviors while also cementing that bond through scent. The biting and kicking motions are instinctive play behaviors that need an outlet, but your shoes provide the added bonus of carrying your scent, which makes them irresistible compared to regular toys.

## Redirecting the Behavior

The key is to give your cat an appropriate outlet that provides both the satisfaction of those hunting and kicking instincts *and* your scent.

Start by using a kicker toy—the "ultimate bunny kicker" or similar elongated toy designed for cats to wrestle and bite. Fold this kicker and place it inside one of your shoes overnight so it absorbs your scent. The next day, present the scent-soaked kicker to your cat instead of letting them access your actual shoes. Since roughly half of all cats don't respond to catnip, using your scent as the lure is often more effective than traditional cat attractants.

Timing matters too. If your cat tends to get destructive when you come home and take off your shoes, use that moment to your advantage. Engage your cat with an interactive toy—something you can move around and play with actively—to burn off that burst of energy. Follow playtime with dinner, which will help tire them out further. Once they're settled, offer the scent-soaked kicker as an alternative outlet for their instincts.

The goal is to give your cat a sense of ownership and connection through the kicker, what you might think of as building their "cat mojo"—that feeling of security and belonging that comes from interacting with something that smells like you.

## Additional Strategies

If your cat is particularly attracted to the rubbery texture of shoes, try soaking rubber dog toys in your scent and offering those instead. Place the scent-soaked toy inside a shoe if needed to make it even more appealing.

You can also try a decoy approach: take an old shoe you don't care about, wear it for a day so it absorbs your scent, place a kicker inside it, and offer it to your cat as their designated "shoe." The downside is that your cat may not distinguish between this decoy and your good shoes, so this works best as a backup strategy if other methods aren't working.

The simplest and most effective approach, however, is prevention. Put your shoes away in a closet or other storage so your cat isn't tempted by them in the first place. Leaving shoes out sets your cat up for failure and creates frustration for both of you. By removing the temptation and providing appropriate alternatives that carry your scent, you're guiding your cat toward behaviors you both can live with.
