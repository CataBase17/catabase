## Why Does My Cat Purr?

Your cat purrs for several important reasons. The most obvious is that purring signals contentment and happiness—it's a leftover behavior from when your cat was a kitten. Mother cats purr to their kittens to show them where to nurse, and kittens purr back as a self-soothing mechanism when they're stressed or anxious.

But purring isn't only about happiness. Your cat may also purr when injured, at the vet's office, or even when encountering someone they dislike. This happens because of the healing properties of purring itself.

A cat's purr operates on both inhalation and exhalation, vibrating at a frequency between 25 and 150 hertz. This vibration has been shown to promote bone density and healing. So when your cat purrs while injured—even with something minor like a splinter in their paw—they're actually helping their body repair itself.

Understanding that your cat purrs for multiple reasons helps you recognize what your cat is communicating. Whether your cat is relaxed on your lap or recovering from an injury, that rumbling sound is often a sign that your cat is managing their emotional or physical state.
