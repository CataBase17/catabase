Cats communicate with you through a surprisingly diverse range of sounds. In fact, cats have over a hundred known vocalizations—more than any other domestic animal, including dogs, which have only around ten. Understanding what your cat is trying to tell you through these sounds will deepen your relationship and help you respond to their needs more effectively.

## Why Your Cat Meows

Your kitten actually starts meowing from birth as a way to find their mother's nipple when their eyes are still closed. In those early days, meowing is how kittens get a response from their mother. This behavior typically continues until around ten days of age.

Something interesting happens as cats grow up: they stop meowing to other cats. Instead, adult cats reserve meowing almost exclusively for communicating with humans. This suggests your cat has learned to use meowing to get what they want from you—whether that's food, attention, or access to a room. The meow is essentially your cat saying "hey, I need something," much like they once called to their mother as a kitten. Your response with treats and attention reinforces this behavior, which is why your individual cat may develop their own unique meowing patterns and frequency.

## Why Your Cat Purrs

Purring is one of the most mysterious cat vocalizations. Despite numerous studies, scientists still don't fully understand the exact mechanism behind purring. Theories include neural oscillators in your cat's brain and changes to the hyoid bone and vocal folds, but the complete picture remains unclear.

What's well-documented is that cats purr in various situations. The most obvious is when they're happy and content—curled up next to you, feeling relaxed and comfortable. But purring isn't always a sign of happiness. Cats may also purr when they're injured or even mortally wounded. This suggests purring serves a self-soothing and healing function. Research indicates that the vibrations from purring can actually facilitate wound healing by occurring at frequencies that help knit bones together.

It's worth noting that small domestic cats purr, but large cats generally don't—with the single exception of the snow leopard, which is the only big cat capable of purring.

## Trilling, Chirping, and Cheeping

These terms describe the same vocalization: a rolling sound similar to the Spanish rolled "r," made by pushing sound through the vocal cords while keeping the mouth mostly closed. Your cat produces this refined, delicate sound as a positive form of communication.

Female cats especially use trills when communicating with their kittens, using the sound to say "follow me" or "come here." A mother cat might trill to indicate feeding time or to lead her kittens to a specific location. You might also hear your cat trill when greeting you or other cats. Trilling is generally a positive sign—think of it as a more excitable version of a purr, indicating your cat is in a good mood and feeling affectionate.

## Chattering

If your cat is sitting at the window watching birds or squirrels and making rapid chattering sounds with their teeth, they're experiencing predatory excitement. This vocalization is an expression of your cat's instinctual hunting drive—they're essentially saying "I really like what I see." 

Some people theorize that cats chatter to imitate their prey and lure it closer, but this is unlikely. Cats are naturally stealthy and quiet hunters; making loud, attention-grabbing sounds would be a poor hunting strategy and wouldn't fool birds into approaching. The fact that cats often chatter at windows when they know they cannot reach their prey confirms this is an instinctual response rather than a deliberate hunting tactic.

## Caterwauls: Sounds of a Cat in Heat

Intact female cats that haven't been spayed make a distinctive sound called a caterwaul before and during heat. This loud vocalization, which can be described as something between a cat and a rooster, is meant to announce the cat's availability for mating to potential suitors. The sound can carry far and wide, attracting intact male cats from a wide area. This often leads to fights and disruptive behavior as males compete for the female's attention.

Spaying your female cat will prevent this behavior and the associated noise, while also preventing unwanted breeding and reducing the risk of certain health problems.

## Hissing

When your cat hisses, they're trying to push you away—typically because they're scared. The hiss is meant to be scary and abrasive, serving as a warning to whatever your cat perceives as a threat. This is a frontline defensive behavior that communicates "I'm frightened and I want you to be frightened too."

If your cat is hissing, it's a clear signal that they need space and should be approached with caution. Respect this boundary by stepping away and giving your cat room to feel safe. Ignoring a hiss or failing to recognize its significance can lead to undesirable consequences, including a scratch or bite.

## Growling

Growling is your cat's way of saying "back off." It expresses discomfort or displeasure with a situation and often precedes a hiss. Growling is part of a graduated communication system cats use to convey their displeasure and warn others to leave them alone.

Cats use a range of escalating vocalizations—from growls to hisses, spits, and screams—to make their boundaries clear. The primary purpose of all these sounds is to signal to others to get away and to emphasize the cat's desire to be left alone. When your cat growls, they're saying "I mean it" and really want you to respect their space.

## Yowling

A yowl is a more prolonged vocalization that can indicate distress, feeling cornered, or being threatened. It often precedes other defensive sounds like hissing, spitting, and growling. Your cat may yowl when they feel pain or fear, using the sound to drive away potential threats—in the wild, sounding vulnerable makes a cat an easier target for predators.

Yowling can be situational. You might hear your cat yowl if they're trapped (accidentally locked in a closet, for example), lost, or scared. In older cats, yowling—especially at night when the lights are out—can be a sign of feline dementia, often accompanied by disorientation and confusion.

## Individual Differences in Cat Communication

Here's something important to understand: how your cat uses vocalizations is highly individual. Two cats meeting for the first time may have difficulty communicating because they've each developed their own "language" based on their unique experiences. It's similar to two humans speaking different languages—the sounds they make may not accurately reflect their emotions.

A sensitive developmental phase occurs in kittens between two and nine weeks of age, during which they learn and develop most of the vocalizations they'll use throughout their lives. Kittens raised as bottle babies, relying entirely on humans for development, may develop inappropriate use of sounds compared to kittens raised with their mothers and littermates.

Just because your cat is hissing doesn't necessarily mean they're truly upset. They may be using a sound that worked for them in a previous situation. To build a better relationship with your cat, take time to learn their individual vocalizations and behaviors, and understand that the same sound can mean different things depending on context and your cat's emotional state.
