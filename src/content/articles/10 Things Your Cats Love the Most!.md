## What Cats Love

Every cat has a unique personality, just like people do. While some preferences vary from cat to cat—like whether your cat enjoys the smell of citrus—there are certain things that all cats genuinely love, and other things people assume they love that they actually don't. Understanding what truly makes your cat happy starts with knowing their natural instincts and needs.

## Hunting and Play

Hunting is wired into your cat's DNA. If your cat were ever in nature, they'd instinctively figure out how to hunt to survive and gain confidence. This natural behavior helps them secure their territory, feel safe, and provide for themselves, which builds their overall sense of well-being and confidence.

Play and hunting are the same thing for your cat: the equation is simple—play equals prey. When your cat engages in hunting or playing activities, they're tapping into their raw, primal nature and connecting with who they are at a fundamental level. This is essential for them to feel whole.

## Creating a Catified Environment

Your cat loves having spaces to explore and claim as their own. They'll find their preferred spots on the vertical axis—whether high up on a shelf, low on the ground, or at medium height like the arm of a couch. Creating what's called a "cat superhighway" by installing shelves around your room lets your cat get off the ground and effectively double the volume of their territory.

Catification—enriching your environment for your cat—taps into their natural ability to use agility and flexibility to their advantage. In nature, cats explore bushes and trees, fitting into small spaces. Your home should give them those same opportunities to interact with their environment in ways that feel natural.

## Sleep

Your cat loves to sleep—a lot. Studies suggest cats sleep between 15 to 20 hours a day. In nature, they rest constantly to build up the strength they need to hunt. Unlike humans, your cat doesn't fall into deep REM sleep. Instead, they take catnaps with what seems like one eye open, staying alert for potential threats or prey even while resting. This unique sleep pattern is an adaptation to their hunting lifestyle.

## Sunlight

Your cat has a strong affection for sun and will seek out sunny spots throughout the day like a living sundial. Cats aren't very efficient at regulating their own body temperature, so they rely on external heat sources. Sunlight is perfect for this.

Make every sunny spot in your house comfortable for your cat. Place a bed on a windowsill, or create a cozy napping area near windows that get good light. Windows are ideal locations because your cat can nap in the warmth and also enjoy scratching nearby.

## Scratching

Scratching is integral to your cat's well-being. When your cat scratches, they're removing dead nail sheaths, exercising their upper chest, and stretching—all things that make their body feel better. Scratching also allows them to mark territory and claim ownership. Your cat has scent glands between their paw pads that leave both a scent mark and a visual one, communicating to other animals that an area belongs to them.

Provide your cat with sturdy scratching surfaces in different orientations—horizontal, vertical, or triangular. This satisfies their scratching need and reduces the likelihood they'll scratch unwanted areas like your couch.

## Petting: Not Always

Not all cats love being pet, even though all cats do love touch to some degree. It's an overreach to assume every cat enjoys being pet from head to tail. Individual preferences vary widely, and some cats may initially enjoy petting but then get sick of it because it overstimulates them. 

All cats do love touch and companionship, but the way they prefer to receive it differs from one cat to another. Pay attention to your cat's individual preferences rather than assuming they'll enjoy what other cats do.

## Companionship

Cats are often misunderstood as being independent, aloof, and solitary, but this isn't entirely accurate. While cats are typically solitary hunters, they do engage in communal eating and grooming, showing they value social interaction and companionship. Female cats have even been observed raising each other's kittens, especially if one female can't care for her own. Female cats are born with more nipples than they need, which supports this cooperative behavior.

## Routine

Cats are driven by routine, which is connected to their natural instincts and daylight hours. As crepuscular animals, cats are naturally inclined to hunt at sunrise and sunset—this is programmed into their DNA. Over thousands of years of evolution, their eyes, ears, and nose have adapted to function optimally during these low-light hours, making them effective hunters when the light is dim.

In your home, your cat appreciates a predictable routine, especially when it comes to feeding times. Your cat can learn to associate certain times of day with food and may even develop a routine of waking you up at specific hours if they've learned when to expect meals. Cats thrive in environments with structure and predictability, not chaos or unpredictability.

## Running Water and Cat TV

Cats are desert animals that have adapted to get hydration from their food rather than drinking water, so it's not a completely natural behavior for them. The idea that all cats love running water isn't accurate. Some cats may prefer it, but many don't.

That said, most cats are fascinated by running water—not necessarily to drink, but because of how light plays and refracts through it, creating a prism-like effect that captivates them. Cats are drawn to the way running water interacts with light, similar to how they're attracted to bugs or other objects that reflect light. You might find your cat staring at or playing with a dripping faucet without actually drinking from it.

Cat TV refers to the activity of your cat looking out the window to observe their surroundings—birds, bugs, other cats, people, or traffic. Studies show cats spend a significant amount of time watching out windows, comparable to how much time they sleep. This allows them to engage their natural hunting instincts passively.

Hunting involves a lot of observation and waiting. Your cat tries to position themselves where they feel unseen, then waits for the perfect moment to pounce. This can take hours before they make their first attempt. The act of watching and observing is crucial—your cat is assessing their surroundings, looking for the right angle, the right target, and the best chance of success.

## What Makes Your Cat Happy

Your cat loves various activities: playing, hunting, climbing up high, burrowing down low, experiencing environmental enrichment, and following a predictable routine. They also love their human family and form an unconditional bond with the people in their lives. That bond is a two-way street that requires love and affection from both of you.

Every cat is an individual, and what one cat loves may not be the same for another. Spend time with your cat and discover what makes them happy and fulfilled. By observing and interacting with your cat, you'll learn about their unique personality and preferences, making your relationship even more special and rewarding.
