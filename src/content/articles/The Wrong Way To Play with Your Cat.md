## Playing with Your Cat Without Encouraging Aggression

If your kitten loves to roughhouse with you, it might seem harmless and cute now—but it can become a real problem as your cat grows. The key issue is teaching your cat that your hands are not toys.

When you use your hands to play fight with your cat, you're teaching him that hands are appropriate targets for his hunting instincts. This might be fine when he's small, but as he grows, his teeth and claws become sharper and more powerful. What looks like playful bunny kicks—where he rears back and strikes with his back legs—is actually your cat practicing his natural predatory behavior. His back legs are his strongest weapon in the wild, and they can cause serious damage.

The problem is that cats can't gauge the severity of their own actions. Your cat doesn't understand that he's hurting you until someone actually gets injured. And once he's learned to associate hands with play, it becomes difficult to undo that behavior. This is especially concerning if your cat ever interacts with children or visitors who might not expect a cat to attack their hands, or who might not know how to respond safely.

## Teaching Appropriate Play Targets

Instead of using your hands, redirect your cat's hunting instincts toward toys designed to be attacked. A kicker toy—a long stuffed toy that your cat can grab and bunny kick—gives him an appropriate outlet for this natural behavior. Interactive wand toys with feathers or other attachments on the end let him engage in hunting while keeping your hands completely safe. You can also use remote toys like small squeaky mice or fuzzy objects to redirect his attention away from you and toward something designed to be pounced on.

Laser pointers can be fun, but remember to always transition from the laser to a physical toy like a kicker so your cat can complete the hunting process and feel genuinely satisfied. Without that final catch, he may end up more frustrated and more likely to redirect his energy toward your hands.

The goal is simple: teach your kitten now that hands are not toys, and find alternative ways to play together. This prevents potential injuries down the road and ensures a safe, healthy relationship with your cat as he grows.
