## Talk to Your Vet First

Before making any changes to your cat's diet, consult with your vet. Each cat is an individual, and your vet can guide you whether you're switching from dry to wet food, wet to raw food, or any other dietary change. This conversation should happen both before and after you make transitions.

## Set Up Meal Times

Cats don't respond well to sudden food changes, especially if they're not hungry. To prepare your cat for a diet transition, stop free feeding and establish a structured meal schedule. The ideal frequency is three meals a day, which helps your cat develop a rhythm and builds hunger at specific times—making them more willing to try new foods.

## Provide Variety in Your Cat's Diet

Feeding your cat the same food for its entire life isn't ideal. In the wild, cats would eat a variety of prey, and your cat's digestive system benefits from being challenged with different foods. Rotating your cat's diet keeps their gut healthy and provides the variety they need.

There's another practical benefit: if your cat ever needs to be cared for by someone else, or if you're unable to access their usual food, a cat accustomed to eating different foods will adapt more easily. This prevents stress and eating problems if your cat ever ends up in a rescue or shelter where the same food may not be available.

## Transition Gradually From Dry to Wet Food

A good approach is to transition from dry food to wet food first, then later to raw if desired. This gives your cat time to adjust to new textures gradually.

Start by reducing the amount of dry food in each meal by half. Your cat should finish the reduced portion quickly. Then introduce wet food alongside it. Your cat may be curious or hesitant at first, but persistence and patience pay off.

Pick up meals after 30 to 45 minutes to discourage grazing, and serve the next meal the same way. Your cat's hunger will motivate them to try the new food. You can use "bribery" strategies: sprinkle ground-up dry food or treats over the wet food to make it more appealing.

For cats that are particularly resistant to change, try tube treats (the kind you squeeze out of a tube and serve in a dish). These introduce a new texture and flavor in a less intimidating way.

Experiment with different brands, textures, and flavors of wet food—pate, shreds, mints, and different flavor profiles. You can also add warm water to create a gravy, which increases hydration and appeal. Be patient with trial and error, and don't be frustrated by food waste during this process. Every cat is different, and what works for one may not work for another.

## Use the Hunt-Catch-Kill-Eat Approach

Cats are built to hunt, catch, kill, and eat. Incorporate this natural instinct into your feeding routine by engaging your cat in interactive play with toys before meal times. This excites them, allows them to exhibit their natural hunting behavior, provides exercise, burns calories, and prepares them to consume a meal.

Incorporating this approach into at least one meal per day makes a real difference in your cat's willingness to eat new foods and their overall vitality and energy.

## Transitioning to Raw Food

If you decide to move to raw food after your cat is comfortable with wet food, remember that cats are naturally drawn to food around body temperature—approximately 98 to 102 degrees Fahrenheit.

Defrost raw food and heat it to body temperature. You can do this by adding warm or hot water to create moisture, or by browning the raw food briefly in a pan. This adds hydration and makes the food more appealing.

If your cat isn't immediately interested, try presenting raw food in small meatball-like shapes that resemble treats. This familiar form can increase the likelihood your cat will eat it.

Use flat, wider plates for raw food rather than deep bowls. Flat plates help avoid whisker stress and have proven more successful with raw diets.

## Monitor Weight and Eating Patterns

When your cat transitions to a raw diet, you may see fat loss while muscle mass is retained. Some weight loss is normal and healthy. However, a loss of more than one percent of body weight per week is excessive.

Watch how much your cat is eating and ensure it's adequate. If you're concerned, add another meal—even a smaller snack meal helps. Your cat can comfortably have multiple meals a day—even four or five if your schedule allows, and making meals a ritualized activity benefits your cat.

It's normal for cats to occasionally skip a meal or eat a little less, but your cat should not go more than 12 hours without eating. Extended fasting puts your cat at risk for hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease), which is costly to treat and potentially fatal. If your cat hasn't eaten for 10, 12, 14, or more hours, address it immediately by trying a different approach to feeding.

## Have a Conversation With Your Vet

Nutrition is essential to your cat's health. Have an open dialogue with your vet about your cat's current nutritional status and your goals for their future. Your vet should welcome this conversation and help you make informed decisions. If you have disagreements or uncertainties, consult trusted online resources and keep communicating with your vet.

Being willing to try different foods, spend a little extra, and even donate rejected food to a rescue demonstrates real commitment to your cat's health. These efforts can lead to a better and longer life for your cat. Patience and persistence are key—nutrition is the foundation of your cat living their best life.
