## Cats Land on Their Feet

Your cat's remarkable ability to survive falls from great heights comes down to several interconnected physical features working in perfect coordination. Cats use their eyes and vestibular apparatus—the inner ear system that detects balance and position—to find the ground, no matter which way they're falling. Their flexible spine allows their back half to twist about 90 degrees while their front half rotates only 10 degrees in the opposite direction, helping them rotate to face downward. As your cat falls, they spread out their body to increase drag and slow their descent, similar to how a parachute works. They even have loose skin flaps that help increase drag further, much like the skin flaps found on flying squirrels. When landing, cats come down on their tiptoes rather than their whole foot, which spreads out the shock of impact and reduces the risk of serious injury.

The righting reflex begins developing in kittens around three to four weeks old and is fully perfected by nine weeks. While this ability is remarkably effective, your cat may still suffer minor injuries like broken toes from falls. This ability evolved because cats naturally navigate vertical spaces—climbing and perching at heights are important for their safety, social structure, and hunting success. Being able to fall without serious injury is essential for your cat's confidence and well-being as they move through their environment.

## Paw Preference and Walking Style

Just like humans, your cat may be left-pawed or right-pawed, showing a preference for one paw when playing, grabbing, or performing other actions. Male cats tend to be more left-pawed, while females are more likely to be right-pawed. Interestingly, about 40% of cats are ambidextrous in their walking pattern.

Your cat is digitigrade, meaning they walk on their tiptoes. Their unique walking style moves the front and back paws on one side of their body first, then the front and back paws on the other side. This pattern is so unusual that among land mammals, only camels and giraffes share it with cats.

## How Your Cat's Tongue Works

Your cat's tongue is covered with tiny ridges called papillae, made from keratin—the same tough material as human fingernails. These papillae serve multiple important functions. When grooming, they help remove oils and dirt from your cat's fur while drawing saliva down past the fur to the skin for deep cleaning. As the saliva evaporates, it helps lower your cat's body temperature, which is why cats spend up to a quarter of their waking hours grooming themselves.

The papillae also help your cat eat by ripping meat off bones, a function you can observe in big cats like lions and tigers. Since the papillae face backward, they're excellent at holding live prey in place. Their keratin composition makes them tough enough to effectively clean bones during feeding.

## Your Cat's Many Vocalizations

Your cat has an impressive vocal range, with over 100 different types of vocalizations identified—significantly more than dogs, which have around ten. This variety can make it challenging to understand exactly what your cat is trying to communicate at any given moment.

The meow is particularly interesting because it evolved specifically for communication with humans. While kittens may meow at each other, adult cats typically reserve meowing for interactions with people. Cats learn to meow because humans respond to it and often treat their cats like babies, and cats quickly figure out they can use this sound to get attention and affection. Beyond meowing, your cat also uses chirps, hisses, growls, and other sounds to convey different messages and emotions.

## Your Cat's Unique Nose

Every cat's nose print is completely unique, much like a human fingerprint. While the reason for this individual variation isn't fully understood, it's one of your cat's distinctive features.

Your cat's nose is also incredibly sensitive. They have 200 million odor sensors in their nose, compared to just 5 million in humans. Your cat relies on their sense of smell more than their eyes for survival, making their nose one of their most important tools for navigating the world and staying safe.

## The Healing Power of Your Cat's Purr

Your cat's purr does far more than show contentment—it plays a significant role in healing. When your cat purrs, they vibrate at between 25 and 150 hertz, a frequency range associated with bone healing, joint and tendon repair, and overall wound healing. This means your cat can recover from injuries and heal broken bones remarkably quickly, partly because of this therapeutic vibration.

But contentment isn't the only reason your cat purrs. Cats also purr when they're in pain or mortally wounded—including during childbirth—as a way to facilitate their own healing. Additionally, cats purr when they're fearful or scared. In these moments, purring acts as a self-soother, releasing endorphins that help calm them down. So while purring does signal happiness, it also serves as a pain reliever and anxiety reducer. The healing effects may extend to you as well. Many people report feeling better when a cat is purring on top of them while they're unwell, though research into whether cats can facilitate human healing is still ongoing.

## Why You Shouldn't Give Your Cat Dairy

Most cats are lactose intolerant and cannot digest milk properly. The common image of cats drinking milk is a misconception that can actually cause your cat digestive problems, including diarrhea. When kittens are weaned at around 10 weeks old and transition to solid food, they no longer need milk for nutrition. The milk people typically offer is cow milk, which isn't suitable for adult cats—or any non-calf animals.

Your cat is an obligate carnivore, meaning they're meant to eat meat. While your cat does need to stay hydrated, they can get most of their water from food, especially wet or raw food, which provides over 70% of their natural hydration. Since cats are desert animals by nature, they don't need to drink large amounts of water—about 30% of their diet can provide sufficient hydration. Giving your cat dairy products like milk or cheese can make them uncomfortable and lead to unpleasant digestive issues, so it's best to avoid these foods altogether.

## Cats and Dogs as Natural Enemies

The idea that cats and dogs are natural enemies is actually a myth—largely perpetuated by TV cartoons that portray them as adversaries. In reality, scientific research shows that cats and dogs can coexist peacefully when introduced properly.

The problem often stems from a fundamental misunderstanding. Many people view cats as "failed dogs" and don't recognize their unique motivations and needs, which are quite different from those of dogs. When you introduce a cat and dog the right way—by speaking to each animal's individual motivations and creating positive associations—they can develop a good relationship. That said, not all cats and dogs will get along. Individual personalities and temperaments matter significantly. Rather than assuming they're enemies by nature, focus on the specific characteristics of each animal and manage their interactions with common sense.

## Can Cats See in the Dark?

Cats are often thought to see perfectly in total darkness, but this isn't quite accurate. Your cat is crepuscular, meaning they're most effective during low light conditions like dusk and dawn—not necessarily in complete darkness. Their eyes are specially adapted for low light: they have additional photoreceptor rods and pupils that dilate to let in more light. However, this adaptation comes at a cost—they sacrifice some color vision to gain superior night vision.

The tapetum lucidum, a reflective layer in the back of your cat's eye, bounces light back out and amplifies it. This is why their eyes appear to glow in the dark. Even with these remarkable adaptations, cats still need at least a small amount of light to see. In total darkness, they're unable to see, so your cat relies on other senses—particularly their exceptional sense of smell—to navigate in complete darkness.
