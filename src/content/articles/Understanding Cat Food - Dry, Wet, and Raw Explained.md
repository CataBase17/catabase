## Understanding Your Cat's Nutritional Needs

Your cat is an obligate carnivore, meaning their body is built to eat one thing: meat. Unlike animals that graze or eat a varied diet, cats are designed to consume the bones, muscle, tissue, fat, and moisture of their prey. This biological reality is the foundation for understanding what your cat actually needs to thrive. Your cat's ancestors, like desert cats from over 10,000 years ago, ate whole prey animals and developed a digestive system perfectly suited to processing raw meat. That's what they should be eating today, and any food you choose should be measured against this standard.

## The Problems With Dry Food

Dry kibble dominates the pet food market largely due to convenience, but convenience has come at a significant cost to your cat's health. The process of creating kibble involves multiple rounds of intense heat exposure that fundamentally transforms meat. First, meat is ground into powder and cooked at high temperatures. Next, it's combined with other ingredients like pea protein or corn gluten—also broken down into powder form—along with supplements. This mixture is cooked again and turned into a dough, shaped, and baked under high heat a final time. By the end of this four-stage process, the original meat is unrecognizable. It no longer smells like meat, so manufacturers spray artificial meat flavoring on the kibble to make it appealing to your cat.

The carbohydrate content in dry food presents a serious problem. Your cat's natural diet consists of mostly protein, with only about 8–9% carbohydrates, which come from the plant matter inside their prey's stomach. Dry food, however, can contain wildly different carbohydrate levels depending on the brand. When your cat eats dry food 15–20 times a day through free feeding, these carbs cause their blood sugar to spike repeatedly. The problem worsens because heating carbohydrates increases their glycemic index even further, creating a double burden for an animal that isn't designed to process carbs. It's like giving a hyperactive child a constant supply of sugary snacks—your cat's system wasn't built for this.

The moisture issue is equally serious. Your cat is originally a desert-dwelling animal with highly efficient kidneys. Roughly 70–75% of their natural hydration comes directly from the meat they eat, not from drinking water. Dry food is, by definition, dehydrating. This lack of moisture forces your cat's kidneys and bladder to work much harder over time, which can lead to serious health problems: crystals, stones, and feline lower urinary tract disease. The absence of moisture essentially forces your cat's system to process food in a way it was never designed to handle.

Cats eating dry food are far more likely to become obese because of the high carbohydrate content—whether the food is grain-free or not. Since cats have low tolerance for carbohydrates, their bodies store excess carbs as fat. Free feeding, which is often paired with dry food, makes this worse by forcing your cat into a grazing pattern that isn't natural for them. Cats that hunt and eat fresh prey in the wild rarely develop obesity or diabetes because their bodies are doing what nature intended.

## Wet Food as a Better Alternative

Wet food—also called canned or prepared food—offers a much better alternative to dry kibble. It has higher water content and lower carbohydrate levels, which aligns much better with what cats actually need. Because of its water content and meat-based composition, wet food is what nutritionists call "bioappropriate" for cats. When your cat eats a diet that matches what they'd naturally consume, real health improvements can follow. Cats are attracted to the smell of meat, and switching to wet food can help them lose weight while actually keeping their muscle mass intact—something kibble often fails to do. Some cats eating wet food have even seen their diabetes reverse.

When evaluating wet food, pay close attention to ingredient lists and what's actually listed. Don't just trust the marketing on the front of the can. Manufacturers use specific terminology that affects what must actually be in the can. Terms like "chicken dinner" or "chicken and rice dinner" have different legal requirements, so identical-sounding names can mean very different things. Byproducts—parts of animals not normally eaten, like organs, feet, or beaks—are sometimes included in wet foods. While some argue this is acceptable if the whole animal is represented, it's not ideal when byproducts become the main ingredient.

Hidden ingredients are another common issue. Fish, for example, might appear in ingredient lists without being obvious, and it's a frequent allergen or sensitivity trigger for many cats. Chicken and beef are also common allergens, so if your cat has digestive issues or itching, check whether these appear in the food. Look for wet foods with these targets: 10 or fewer calories from carbohydrates, at least 40 percent protein, and under 50 percent fat. Even the worst wet food on the market is a better choice for your cat than the best dry food available when you weigh all the factors.

## Raw Food: The Most Biologically Appropriate Option

A raw diet is considered the most biologically appropriate food for cats because it closely mirrors what they would eat in nature. Unlike dry kibble, which is heavily processed with heat and often loaded with carbohydrates and added flavorings, raw food preserves the natural nutrients in meat. While wet food is better than dry food, it still has drawbacks. It's heat-processed, which denatures the meat, and the labels can be confusing—making it hard to know exactly what your cat is eating and in what proportions. With a raw diet, you have complete control over the ingredients and can eliminate mystery meat, byproducts, and unwanted additives.

When you switch your cat to a raw diet, you're likely to see several improvements. Your cat's digestion becomes more efficient because their digestive tract is designed to process raw meat. You'll notice better stools, less litter box waste, and a shorter digestive cycle overall. Raw food also leads to a healthier coat with less dandruff and dry spots. Many cats lose weight without losing muscle mass on a raw diet. Chewing on raw bones and connective tissue helps clean your cat's teeth naturally and provides a natural source of calcium, reducing the need for added supplements. Overall, cats on raw diets tend to have more predictable, higher energy levels—closer to what nature intended.

## Getting Started With Raw Feeding

You don't have to make raw food from scratch. Commercially available raw foods come pre-balanced with all necessary supplements and proper protein ratios, making them convenient and "idiot proof." You can choose your preferred protein source and let the manufacturer handle the nutrition calculations. If you prefer more control, homemade raw diets are also an option and are much easier to manage than they used to be. Resources are now available to help you create a balanced, healthy diet for your cat.

Cost is a legitimate concern, but making large batches of homemade raw food can be surprisingly affordable—potentially close to the cost of premium wet food in the long run, though you'll need to experiment to find the right balance for your budget. When preparing raw food for your cat, you must add supplements because some nutrients are naturally lost during preparation. Unlike a cat catching and immediately eating a mouse, prepared raw food lacks the blood supply and certain nutrients found in the head, particularly from the brain and eyes. Essential supplements include taurine, B vitamins, vitamin E, and essential fatty acids. Simply feeding your cat raw chicken from the supermarket won't provide these necessary nutrients. If you're buying commercially prepared raw food, these supplements are already included.

Bacterial contamination, especially salmonella, is often cited as a concern with raw diets. However, cats have been eating raw prey for thousands of years and aren't very susceptible to it. In fact, cooked meat has a higher rate of bacterial contamination than raw meat. Commercial dry and wet foods are frequently recalled due to salmonella, whereas raw foods have fewer contamination incidents. While the risk exists with any food source, it's a small part of the overall picture when it comes to raw feeding. To support your cat's digestive health during any dietary transition, consider using probiotics and digestive enzymes designed for cats, which can make a real difference in their gut health.

## Making the Transition

Before you switch to any new food, consult with your veterinarian, especially if your cat has pre-existing conditions like diabetes or a compromised immune system. Your vet should evaluate your cat and check their levels before you make the switch. When you're ready to move your cat to a new food, do it slowly. Sudden changes upset their digestive system and can lead to stomach problems. Your cat's system is sensitive to new nutrition, so patience during the transition is essential. Take your time introducing the new food to avoid digestive upset.

A raw diet is genuinely beneficial for most cats, but it's not right for every cat in every situation. The convenience of dry food has made it the dominant choice since the 1950s, but convenience shouldn't come at the cost of your cat's health. Your cat's long-term health and well-being are worth the extra effort. Consider reevaluating your current feeding choices and exploring alternatives that better match your cat's biological needs.
