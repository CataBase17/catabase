## A Cat's Tongue

Your cat's tongue has a unique structure that makes it feel rough against your skin. The surface is covered with backward-facing barbs called papillae, which are made of keratin—a tough, durable material. These papillae serve an important purpose: they help your cat remove meat from bones while eating. This is why when your cat licks you, it can feel almost like gentle exfoliation or microdermabrasion on your skin. This tongue structure is the same across all cats, from domestic pets to big cats in the wild, and evolved specifically to help them eat and process prey.

## Why Cats Lick

When your cat licks you, it's often a sign of confidence and territorial ownership. Cats have scent glands in their tongues and between their paw pads, as well as on their sides. By licking, scratching, rubbing, and head-bumping you, your cat is leaving their scent behind through pheromones. In your cat's mind, this marks you as part of their territory—a way of saying "you are a part of me" and establishing a sense of togetherness and ownership.

## Licking as Affection

Your cat may also lick you simply as a way to show affection. This behavior often traces back to kittenhood, when their mother would clean and lick them. Through this grooming, mother cats teach their kittens how to groom themselves while also showing them love and security. Kittens weaned too early—typically between three to four weeks and separated from their mothers—are especially likely to seek out this nurturing behavior with their owners.

Cats may also exhibit a behavior called smurging (kneading with their paws while purring) as a way of showing affection, a behavior they would have done while nursing. Both licking and smurging are direct connections to their earliest memories of protection, security, and maternal care.

## The Lick-to-Bite Pattern

Sometimes your cat's licking transitions into gentle biting. This can happen for a few reasons. First, kittens are naturally squirmy, so mother cats will bite onto their kittens' scruff—an area with very few nerve endings—to hold them in place. When your adult cat bites you, it's often not a full chomp but rather a gentle hold, sometimes called a "liquid bite," where they're trying to grab and secure you.

Second, excessive licking can lead to overstimulation. When your cat grooms intensely, that pattern of behavior can become overwhelming and trigger biting. The licking and biting together are rooted in your cat's earliest experiences with their mother, even though it may not always feel comfortable for you.

## You Taste Good

Your cat may also lick you because you simply taste good to them. The salt on human skin is particularly appealing to cats, especially in hot weather. The sugar your body naturally secretes can also be attractive to your cat. This straightforward explanation is just one of several reasons your cat might be drawn to licking you.

## Important Safety Warning

If your cat is a heavy licker, be cautious about what you wear on your skin. Never use lotions, perfumes, or essential oils before interacting with your cat. Many of these substances are toxic to cats if ingested, and a licking cat can ingest these products. If you're an over-groomer's owner, it's especially important to avoid these products to keep your cat safe.

## How to Reduce Licking and Biting

If the licking and biting become excessive, there are several strategies you can try. The most effective approach is to break the pattern without punishing your cat. When your cat is licking or biting, gently pick them up and put them down elsewhere. This interrupts the hypnotic cycle of grooming without making your cat afraid of you.

Consistency matters. Avoid inadvertently rewarding the behavior by talking to your cat or showing affection while they're actively licking or biting you. Even well-intentioned attention can encourage them to continue.

You can also physically cover the areas your cat likes to target. Wearing long sleeves or keeping a blanket nearby can discourage the behavior. Additionally, provide your cat with alternative ways to mark their scent. Set up scratching posts, cat trees, or beds in important areas like your bedroom or near the couch. These give your cat an approved outlet for scent-marking. You can also offer your cat a scent identification security blanket—a bed or toy with their scent on it—which can help them feel more secure and reduce the urge to mark you.
