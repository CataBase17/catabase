## The Structure and Function of a Cat's Tail

Your cat's tail is made up of vertebrae called caudal vertebrae, which are an extension of the spine but don't contain the spinal cord. These vertebrae end higher up in the lumbar spine, giving your cat a flexible and highly functional tail.

The primary job of your cat's tail is to act as a ballast, helping them balance while walking on narrow surfaces like fences or branches. When your cat jumps, the tail doesn't provide balance in the air, but it helps them land and instantly regain their footing, so they're ready for another jump.

Your cat's tail also helps them sense their surroundings. At the tip of the tail are vibrissae, or whiskers, which provide information about the environment, including barometric pressure and temperature. The tail also contains scent glands that allow your cat to mark their territory—a sign of affection in the feline world. By rubbing their tail on you or objects in their home, your cat is expressing ownership and trust.

Your cat's tail works alongside their eyes, ears, and whiskers to communicate with both you and other animals. Understanding what your cat's tail is telling you can help deepen your relationship with them.

## Tail Positions and What They Mean

**Straight Up**

When your cat holds their tail straight up, it's a sign of high confidence and trust in their surroundings. This position acts like a warning flag, signaling to others—including potential predators—that your cat is present. It's similar to the flags on the back of bikes that warn cars of danger.

A straight-up tail is a sign of trust and friendliness, much like other positive behaviors such as when your cat exposes their belly for a "cat hug" or gives you a slow blink. This posture conveys an alert, confident, and friendly mood—your cat is essentially saying "Hey, how you doing?"

**Backwards Question Mark**

A tail that curves into a shape resembling a backwards question mark is a relaxed and inviting posture. Often accompanied by a little crook at the end of the tail toward your cat's head, this position is a friendly and confident gesture.

Domestic cats are unique in their ability to walk with their tail held straight up in the air. This ability comes down to the complex calculus of their tail's length and weight—something that would be too energetically costly for wildcats to replicate.

**Straight Backwards**

When your cat's tail extends straight out from their body, parallel to the horizon, it indicates they're being explorative and cautious, but still confident. Your cat is walking into a new place and trying to make sense of it while remaining mindful of potential dangers. The vibrissae on the tail tip play a role here, helping your cat sense their surroundings and navigate with a mix of trust and caution.

As your cat's tail lowers gradually, it suggests they're becoming less confident and more uncertain. This doesn't necessarily mean they're fearful—rather, they're being cautious and verifying their trust in the environment. As the tail gets lower and may start to curve under the body, your cat is balancing their trust with the need to verify their safety.

**Curved Under**

When your cat's tail is curved under their body, it can signal that they're petrified and something is wrong in their world. This indicates a high level of fear or anxiety.

A tail tucked to the belly is a signal to you to address something in your cat's environment. If a toddler has just arrived, for example, your cat may be scared. You can help by providing a safe space where your cat can observe the toddler from a higher vantage point. If your cat's tail is glued to their belly, it may indicate that something traumatic has happened—such as a past incident with a dog—and your cat is now hiding from the perceived threat.

Your cat may also curl their tail under during panic responses, such as at the vet or when getting into a car, both common sources of stress and anxiety for cats. By observing your cat's tail position and behavior, you can gain valuable insights into their emotional state and take steps to address any underlying issues and create a more comfortable environment.

## Tail Movements

**Flicking Side to Side**

When your cat's tail flicks from side to side, it primarily signifies activation and a release of energy—like a pressure valve as your cat becomes full of energy. This can happen when they're watching something, such as birds outside, playing, or even when you're in the room.

Pay attention to the intensity of the flicking. As the movement progresses to a whipping motion, it can indicate that your cat is becoming overstimulated and annoyed. When the tail starts whipping from the base, it signals that your cat is highly overstimulated and unhappy—their whole body is on fire. At this point, they may bite or run away if touched.

Some cats may always have a flicking tail, but it's essential to look at the action itself and consider other signs, such as how hard they're swishing or tucking the tail, to determine your cat's true emotional state. Context matters: the same behavior can have different meanings depending on the situation.

If your cat's tail is vibrating and they're becoming overstimulated, remain calm and try to quiet their world. Lead them to a peaceful, darkened space where they can relax and receive treats or food. This can help them calm down.

**Straight Up and Vibrating**

When your cat's tail is straight up and vibrating, it often means they're spraying—especially if there's a vertical surface nearby, such as a wall. The vibration is tight and characteristic of this behavior. When cats spray, they're leaving a concentrated and potent form of urine that marks their territory and lasts longer than regular urination.

However, a straight-up vibrating tail can also be a sign of excitement and happiness rather than spraying. If your cat walks up to you with their tail in this position, they may be saying "Hi, I'm so happy to see you—you are my person." Pay attention to the context to distinguish between the two behaviors.

**Puffed Out Tail**

When your cat's tail puffs out, creating a "bottle brush" effect, it's a reflex that happens when they're scared. By puffing out their tail, curved back, and body, your cat is trying to make themselves appear larger and more menacing to scare off a perceived threat.

Just like humans, your cat can exhibit various behaviors that indicate something is bothering them. By paying attention to these signals, you can help provide a happy and trusting environment for your cat.
