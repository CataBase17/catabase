Cats throw up more often than you might expect. On average, most cats vomit at least once to three times a month, and this is considered normal. However, it's important to know the difference between normal vomiting and vomiting that signals a problem. If you're ever concerned about your cat's vomiting, consult your veterinarian for professional guidance.

## When Cats Throw Up Food

Your cat may vomit food either digested or undigested. Undigested food vomiting often happens when your cat eats too quickly—a phenomenon sometimes called "snarf and barf." This typically occurs within about 10 minutes of eating.

To address this issue, the easiest solution is to slow down your cat's eating process. A slow feeder bowl, which has ridges or grooves built in, forces your cat to eat slower. When cats eat at a slower pace, they're better able to register that they're full and stop eating too quickly, which reduces vomiting.

You can also help by elevating your cat's food bowl to a table or counter. This can reduce stress during mealtime, which is especially important in multi-cat households where cats may feel rushed to eat quickly due to competition with other cats or dogs.

If you have multiple cats, spacing out their food bowls and giving each cat a comfortable, stress-free eating environment is crucial. This ensures no cat feels the need to rush through their meal.

## When Cats Throw Up Grass

When your cat throws up grass surrounded by a little white foam, their system is trying to expel something that doesn't belong. This can happen if your cat eats a lot of cat grass.

It's important to understand that cats don't chew like humans do. Their teeth are designed for grabbing and yanking meat off a bone, not for chewing. This means cats often swallow things like blades of grass whole, and these can get stuck and come back up.

If your cat is throwing up non-food objects like toys, Christmas wrapping, strings, or tinsel, this is more serious. These items are indigestible and can be dangerous. Objects that appear in vomit signal that your cat has swallowed something harmful, and your vet should evaluate this right away.

## Hairballs

Hairballs are a natural part of cat ownership. They occur because cats are fastidious groomers—they use their tongues, which have tiny hooks, to pull off loose fur. It's not natural for cats to pass these hairs through their digestive system, so they accumulate in the stomach.

When your cat hacks up a hairball, you'll see them working hard to expel it. The hairball itself is a round, hard, condensed object that has been packed up from the stomach and worked through the esophagus. Long-haired cats tend to have more hairballs than short-haired cats.

If your cat is frequently throwing up hairballs, look for an underlying cause. Cats sometimes over-groom due to nervousness, allergies, or other issues. Addressing the root cause can help reduce hairballs.

One of the best preventive measures is regular brushing, especially for long-haired breeds. Brushing removes loose fur before it ends up in your cat's stomach, which decreases the likelihood of hairballs forming.

## Food Allergies and Sensitivities

Some cats have trouble digesting certain ingredients in their food. Common allergens include chicken, beef, and fish. If you suspect a food allergy, your vet may recommend a food trial.

A food trial involves feeding your cat a limited ingredient diet with just one protein and one carbohydrate—such as rabbit or venison—for about eight weeks. During this time, your cat should eat only the trial food. This helps rule out other potential allergens.

Beyond vomiting, pay attention to the overall quality of your cat's diet. A natural cat diet would consist of bone, flesh, fat, and muscle, along with small amounts of the kinds of plant material found in a mouse's stomach. Avoid foods with grains, hooves, tumors, or byproducts, and look for high-quality, human-grade, bioappropriate options.

One often-overlooked issue is lactose intolerance. Cats are lactose intolerant, so milk, cream, whipped cream, and ice cream can cause digestive upset including vomiting and diarrhea. If your cat has access to these products, eliminating them may help.

## Parasites

Vomiting combined with diarrhea or anemia can indicate parasites. Various parasites—including hookworms, roundworms, tapeworms, flatworms, coccidia, and giardia—infect a cat's digestive system and cause vomiting and other digestive problems.

In some cases, you may actually see parasites in your cat's stool as wiggling organisms, which can be unsettling. Your vet can test for parasites and provide appropriate treatment.

## Asthma

Cats with asthma display a distinctive hacking behavior during an episode. Your cat will get into a particular position with their butt up in the air and hack repeatedly, as if about to throw up—but nothing comes out. There's no white foam, bile, vomit, or other material.

These episodes typically last around 30 seconds and can be distressing to witness. If you notice this behavior in your cat, they need to be checked by a vet right away. Discuss with your vet how to manage asthma episodes if they occur in your cat.

## Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)

IBD is a condition in cats similar to the same condition in humans. While the exact cause is unknown, it produces identifiable symptoms and changes in the gastrointestinal tract visible through imaging.

IBD leads to chronic vomiting in cats. Treatment often requires trial and error, and you'll need to work closely with your vet to find the best approach for your individual cat.

## When Vomiting Is a Concern

Occasional vomiting—one to three times a month—is normal. However, chronic vomiting, defined as vomiting daily or vomiting that's new or unusual for your cat, requires veterinary attention.

If your cat is throwing up consistently, or if you're noticing vomiting that's different from their normal pattern, take them to a vet. This could signal an underlying issue that needs professional care.

Being aware of your cat's normal vomiting patterns and knowing when to seek help is key to keeping your cat healthy. When in doubt, consult your veterinarian.
