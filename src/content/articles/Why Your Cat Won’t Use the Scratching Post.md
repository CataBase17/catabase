## Why Your Cats Scratch the Couch

Your cats scratch the couch for a few important reasons. When cats scratch, they're exercising their claws and removing dead nail sheaths—but the *location* they choose matters. Your couch is especially attractive because it's sturdy (so it won't tip over when they scratch), and it carries your scent. Cats are drawn to surfaces that smell like their owners and use scratching to co-mark these spots, essentially claiming them as part of their territory.

The cushions are particularly appealing because that's where you sit and interact with your cats. This familiarity makes the couch feel like a meaningful place to scratch, even when you've provided other scratching options nearby.

## Understanding Your Cat's Scratching Style

Your cats are giving you an important clue: they're scratching the *cushions* of the couch, not the arms. This tells you they're **horizontal scratchers**—they prefer to scratch flat surfaces rather than vertical ones. This is a key insight, because it means the vertical cat tower you've set up, while wonderful for climbing and perching at the window, doesn't match their scratching preference.

Not all cats have the same scratching style. Some prefer vertical surfaces, some prefer horizontal, and some like both. Once you know your cats are horizontal scratchers, you can work with that preference instead of against it.

## Redirecting Horizontal Scratchers

The most direct solution is to provide a **horizontal scratcher positioned right where your cats want to scratch**. Place it at the foot of the couch or even on the couch itself when you're not home. You can experiment with placement—some cats respond better to a scratcher on the floor, while others prefer one elevated on the furniture itself. The goal is to make the alternative more appealing than the couch cushion.

Since your cats came from a foster home with their original scratching bed, and they've already been scratching that, you might also consider what material that bed is made from. Cats often gravitate toward textures they already know, so understanding what surface they naturally prefer can help you choose the right horizontal scratcher.

## Protective Options for Your Couch

If redirecting hasn't fully solved the problem, you have practical alternatives to double-sided tape. **Clear plastic runners with bumps** (the kind used as floor protectors) can be cut to size and laid across the couch. These make the surface uninviting to scratch while remaining transparent, so your couch stays visible underneath. When you leave the house, your cats are far more likely to avoid the couch entirely when it's covered with these bumpy runners—they simply won't want to scratch it.

Alternatively, you could change the couch's covering fabric altogether. If the current material is similar to the sisal or fabric on scratching posts, your cats may find it irresistible. Switching to a different, less inviting texture can reduce the appeal.

## Setting Up Your Space for Success

The key principle is providing a "yes" (an appealing alternative) whenever you restrict a "no" (the couch). You've already done this with the cat tower—it's in a great spot by the window, and your cats do use it. But since they're horizontal scratchers, you need to add a horizontal option to the equation.

Every piece of furniture should serve a purpose for your cats. Your cat tower gives them climbing and window viewing. A horizontal scratcher gives them the surface they actually want to use. By providing both, you're working with your cats' natural preferences rather than fighting against them.

You can also keep your cats engaged with play to channel their energy. Interactive toys like wand toys help satisfy their hunting instincts and keep them mentally stimulated, reducing the urge to scratch out of boredom or restlessness.
