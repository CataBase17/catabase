## Understanding the Laser Pointer

Laser pointers are a somewhat controversial cat toy. Some people love them and think their cats enjoy them, while others point out real frustrations and dangers. The key to using them well is understanding how your cat's hunting instincts work.

About 50 to 70 percent of cats become extremely interested in laser pointers—so interested that they'll drop everything, including food, to chase that little red dot. Some cats and dogs will go to great lengths to pursue it, which raises an important question about what the laser pointer actually is.

## Why a Laser Isn't Really a Toy

The laser pointer isn't a toy in the traditional sense. It's actually a frustrating thing for a predator. Your cat's hunting instinct is triggered, but the laser prevents them from completing the natural hunting cycle: hunt, catch, kill, and eat.

When your cat plays with a wand toy that has feathers, you'll see the full natural behavior unfold—running, catching, wrestling, and putting the feather in their mouth. A laser pointer stops short. It teases your cat without providing anything tangible to catch or finish. This can be frustrating, exhausting, and even potentially harmful (cats have been known to break teeth chasing something they can never catch).

## How to Use a Laser Pointer Responsibly

If you use a laser pointer, think of it as a way to kick-start play rather than the whole play session. Use it to get your cat moving and engaged—like turning a key to crank an engine to life. Some cats need that extra stimulation to get fully engaged and running.

Once your cat is active and moving, transition to a different type of play. Sneak a feather toy around corners, fly it through the air, keep it just out of reach, and then let your cat catch it. By allowing your cat to actually catch and "kill" the toy, you complete the play cycle, giving your cat the satisfaction they need.

This is crucial: never end a play session with just a laser pointer. If you do, your cat will be left frustrated, which often leads to destructive behavior—knocking things off shelves, bothering other pets, or climbing the curtains. Your cat's pent-up hunting energy has nowhere to go.

## Redirecting Your Cat

Laser pointers can be useful for redirecting your cat's attention when they become fixated on another cat or are heading toward trouble. Point the laser where your cat can see it and lead them away from the problem situation, then transition to something else—treats, play with a wand toy, or another engaging activity. This is a much gentler way to redirect behavior than yelling or trying to scare your cat away, and it won't damage your relationship with them.

The key is always to follow through. Lead your cat to a better activity and complete the play cycle so they don't end up frustrated and destructive.

## Are Laser Pointers Safe for Your Cat's Eyes?

The concern about eye damage from laser toys isn't conclusive. These devices emit incredibly low-intensity light, and brief exposure when your cat glances at the laser hasn't caused damage in most cases. If your cat were to stare at the laser for an extended period, there is theoretically a risk, but normal use—leading your cat from one place to another—isn't a concern.

The real issue with laser pointers isn't eye safety; it's the frustration and incomplete play cycle. Remember that your cat needs real satisfaction from playtime, not just a brief stimulation. Think of it this way: offering your cat just a laser pointer and nothing to catch is like giving them only a potato chip when they need a full meal. It's not enough.

Use laser pointers as a tool to initiate play or redirect unwanted behavior, but always finish the job with a real toy your cat can catch and "complete the hunt" with.
