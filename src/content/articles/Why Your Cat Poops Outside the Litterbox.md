## Why Your Cat Is Pooping Outside the Litter Box

Your youngest cat has stopped using the litter box to defecate, even though she's healthy and you have four litter boxes for three cats. She's pooping on the carpet or pads in front of the boxes, usually right next to them. This pattern tells you something important: she wants to use the box but something is stopping her.

## Understanding What Your Cat Is Telling You

When your cat poops outside the litter box but stays close to it, she's signaling discomfort or an aversion to using the box itself—not rejection of the location. Cats will avoid the litter box if it hurts to use it, even though they're conditioned to go there. Your cat won't tell you directly that something is wrong; instead, she shows you through her behavior.

Since your vet confirmed she's healthy, the issue is likely related to how the litter box experience feels to her physically or the conditions around it.

## Check for Physical Discomfort

Ask your vet to palpate (feel) her belly during an exam, as general physical exams may not reveal pain or discomfort unless the vet specifically checks. Also bring in a fecal sample for analysis to rule out parasites or other digestive issues. Hard, dry stools or runny stools can both make defecation painful or uncomfortable.

## Evaluate Your Cat's Diet

Diet is often the culprit. If your cat eats too much dry food, she may become dehydrated, leading to hard and painful stools. Conversely, sudden diet changes—switching too quickly from dry to wet or raw food—can cause diarrhea or loose stools, which are also uncomfortable.

Since your cat currently eats mostly dry food, try adding more moisture to her diet. You might also consider a supplement like slippery elm, which can help improve digestive health and stool quality.

## Remove Potential Deterrents

Litter mats placed in front of or around the box can deter some cats from using it. Try removing any mats you have and see if your cat's behavior changes.

## Experiment Systematically

Approach this like detective work. Make one change at a time so you can identify what actually helps:

- Try adding a new litter box with different litter in a different location to see if she'll use it
- If she does, gradually change the litter in your existing boxes to match
- If the boxes are near something loud (like a washing machine), move them away
- If she responds well to a change, you can eventually remove the extra boxes once the problem is resolved

Avoid making multiple changes at once, as cats dislike change and it can worsen the problem. When owners panic about litter box issues, they often change the litter, the boxes, and their placement simultaneously—which creates more stress for your cat.

## Rule Out Environmental Changes

Think through any recent changes in your home. Has the litter box location changed? Is the litter brand new? Have you added or moved any furniture? Reverse any recent changes to see if they're contributing to the problem.

With patience and systematic experimentation, you should be able to identify what's making your cat avoid the litter box and resolve the issue.
