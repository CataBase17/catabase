## Understanding Wallflower Cats

A "wallflower cat" is a shy or scared cat that often hides in small, enclosed spaces like closets or under beds. These cats may spend most of their time in hiding, demonstrating what's sometimes called "anti-mojo" behavior. Wallflower cats feel the need to become "uber small" and "uber insignificant" to cope with their environment. They live in what might be called the "unconfident zone," where they try to be invisible and blend into the furniture. You can identify this behavior by watching for extreme timidity and a preference for hiding in small spaces.

Well-meaning cat owners often accidentally make this behavior worse by providing comfortable hideaways—like building a bed in the closet or feeding the cat in enclosed areas. While these seem like kind gestures, they actually prevent your cat from building confidence and developing their natural mojo.

## Helping Your Wallflower Cat Build Confidence

Rather than catering to your cat's fear, you should encourage them to explore their surroundings and come out of their comfort zone. The key is identifying your cat's "confident zone"—the areas where they already feel safe—and using that as a starting point. From there, you can gradually help them assert their dominance and build their confidence.

Start by removing or blocking access to their favorite hiding spots, such as under the bed or coffee table. This creates a more open environment that naturally encourages exploration. For each cat, there's a "challenge line"—the boundary between where they're comfortable and where they're not. You want to gently pull them forward past this line, helping them overcome their fears step by step.

## Using a Cocoon for Safety and Growth

One of the most effective tools for helping a wallflower cat is creating what's called a "cocoon"—a small, enclosed space that feels safer than a traditional hiding spot like under the bed, but that you can control and move.

Before using a cocoon, it's important to block access under beds. You can do this with a platform bed or by attaching pieces of wood underneath. This matters especially in emergencies: in a crisis like a house fire, a cat hiding under the bed won't be able to escape.

A cocoon serves a different purpose. It gives your cat that small, enclosed feeling they crave while keeping them part of the household. A cocoon can be a simple, portable enclosure—like a bed with a hood, a blanket draped over a frame, or a "comfy clamshell"—that you can place on a couch, bed, or floor in various locations.

Here's how to use one: Start by letting your cat get comfortable with the cocoon in a quiet space, like a closet. Once they're relaxed there, gradually move it to more open areas—next to the couch, on the couch, or near the family. As you move it, slowly open up the enclosure, creating openings so your cat can peek out and feel confident about watching the world while still having their safe space. Over time, your cat becomes accustomed to being in the main living areas and can observe and interact with their surroundings from a position of security.

## Placing the Cocoon and Encouraging Exploration

You can create a bed that allows your cat to peek out and feel confident. Start with it a little more closed, then slowly open it up over time as your cat becomes accustomed to it. By placing this bed on or next to the couch rather than in an isolated closet, your cat can still feel part of the family without needing to retreat to hidden areas.

The goal is to address the underlying reason your cat is hiding—their need for control and security—rather than simply accepting the hiding behavior. When you provide that sense of control through a cocoon and gradually introduce your cat to new locations and experiences, they can become more confident and active participants in family life.
