## Understanding Why Your Cat Needs a Basecamp

Cats are intensely territorial animals. When you bring your cat into a new home or introduce major changes—like a move, a new pet, a guest, or a new baby—your cat may experience panic and anxiety. Without a sense of security and familiarity, cats often act out through behaviors like scratching or inappropriate urination as they try to claim the space and make it their own.

A basecamp gives your cat a secure, familiar zone where they can decompress. The more overwhelming the new situation, the more anxious your cat may become, and a solid basecamp helps prevent stress-driven behaviors. Think of it as a panic room your cat can retreat to when feeling scared or overwhelmed.

## What Goes Into a Basecamp

The foundation of a basecamp is scent soakers—porous items that your cat can lay against or rub their cheek on to deposit their scent. These items become territorial signposts, proof of your cat's existence in the space, much like family photos on a mantel serve that purpose for humans.

Scent soakers can include worn clothing, pillows, blankets, beds, toys, food and water bowls, and litter boxes. Two litter boxes in the basecamp are especially important, as they allow your cat to deposit scent and mark territory. You can also convert a cat carrier into a bed by adding a blanket and treats, turning it into both a comforting den and a scent soaker.

The goal is to fill the basecamp with items that carry your cat's scent or will soon carry it, creating an environment that feels wholly theirs.

## Where to Place Your Basecamp

Choose a socially significant spot in your home—a spare bedroom, office, or den works well. These spaces feel important to your cat and give them a sense of ownership. Avoid the master bedroom, as excluding other pets from your bedroom for extended periods can create rivalries within your household.

Don't use small, confined spaces like mud rooms, washer and dryer rooms, garages, or bathrooms. These areas may make your cat feel caged rather than secure. The basecamp should feel like a refuge, not a cage.

## Using Basecamp to Introduce Cats

When you're bringing a new cat into a multi-cat household, the basecamp becomes essential for safe introductions. Place the new cat in the basecamp, then rotate your resident cats into the room a couple of times a day. This allows all your cats to become familiar with each other's scent without the stress of a face-to-face meeting—it's like a long-distance handshake.

As the introduction progresses and you begin expanding the basecamp, the scent-soaking items will carry the combined scent of all your cats. This is a positive sign that they're becoming comfortable with each other's presence.

## Expanding Basecamp Into Your Home

As your cat grows confident and ready to explore the house, their basecamp items move with them. Bring pillows, scratching posts, litter boxes, and bowls out into other rooms—place a pillow on the couch in the living room, for example. These items feel like a red carpet to your cat, introducing their scent and marking territory throughout the home.

Use a rotation system: items soaked with your cat's scent are placed around the house, then fresh items are brought back to the basecamp for your cat to scent soak again. Then rotate those newly scent-soaked items out into the home. This keeps your cat's mojo strong throughout the house and enhances their sense of ownership and happiness in their space.
