If your cat has ever gagged or jerked in response to certain sounds—like the crinkling of tin foil, a spoon dropping into a ceramic bowl, or the tapping of a keyboard—you may be witnessing something called Feline Audiogenic Reflex Seizures, or FARS. This condition causes cats to experience seizure-like reactions when exposed to specific sound stimuli.

## What FARS Is

FARS refers to cats responding to different sounds by experiencing seizure-like behavior. The condition falls into three categories: generalized tonic clonic seizures (what most people think of as a typical seizure), myoclonic seizures (which involve jerking behavior), and absence seizures (where your cat appears to zone out). Myoclonic seizures are the most common type experienced by cats with FARS.

Your cat's sensitivity to sound is rooted in their biology. Cats are highly attuned to certain sound frequencies, particularly in the ultrasonic range around 40 kilohertz—the range that mice and rats use to communicate. This heightened sensitivity can contribute to their susceptibility to FARS.

## Sounds That Can Trigger FARS

A wide range of everyday sounds can trigger seizure-like reactions in cats with this condition. Common triggers include:

- Crinkling tin foil
- Metal spoon dropping into a ceramic feeding bowl
- Chinking or tapping of glass
- Paper or plastic bags crinkling
- Computer keyboard tapping
- Mouse clicking
- Clinking of coins or keys
- High-pitched sounds, including a young child screaming

The severity of the reaction can depend on the volume and duration of the sound. Louder and longer-lasting sounds may cause worse seizures, and individual cats vary in how sensitive they are to specific frequencies.

## What to Do If Your Cat Has FARS

The good news is that FARS is generally considered a very mild condition, especially when the primary manifestation is gagging or mild jerking. Most myoclonic seizures do not progress to full-on seizures.

However, the condition can be managed. As your cat ages, the reactions may become stronger, but there are medications that have shown some success in treating FARS. If you notice your cat experiencing these reactions and you're concerned, or if the symptoms worsen over time, consult with your veterinarian or seek out a veterinary behaviorist. A specialist can prescribe appropriate medication and provide detailed information about managing the condition.

For now, the best approach is to be aware of your cat's triggers and try to minimize their exposure to the sounds that cause reactions. Keep track of which sounds bother your cat most, and avoid using them unnecessarily around your pet.
