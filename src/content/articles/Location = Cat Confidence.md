## Understanding Cat Confidence Types

Cats demonstrate confidence in different ways depending on their personality type. Understanding which type your cat is will help you support them better.

**Tree dwellers** show confidence by spending time in elevated positions—anywhere above ground level, from a chair or table to the top of the couch or rafters. Being up high isn't necessarily about hiding; it's about claiming a confident position in their surroundings.

**Bush dwellers** prefer to hide under objects and stay low to the ground. They're confident in their own way, but they express it differently than tree dwellers do.

**Beach dwellers** are the most territorial. They prefer to keep all four paws on the floor and will make their presence known by claiming space and requiring others in the house to move around them. They're sending a clear message: this is my territory.

## When Confidence is Lacking

Some cats lack confidence and become "cavers"—they try to be small and invisible, hiding away rather than claiming territory. This is different from being a natural bush or tree dweller. Cavers are driven by fear and a desire to disappear, not by a confident choice about where to spend their time.

## Building Confidence Through Catification

The key to helping your cat thrive is creating an environment where they feel confident and in control. This process, called "catification," involves providing structures that let your cat move around with confidence and express their natural personality.

For tree dwellers, you might provide ramps and elevated pathways—what some people call "cat superhighways"—that let them navigate their world from above. For bush dwellers and cavers, you'd focus on safe spaces and structures that let them feel secure while gradually building their confidence.

The real goal is to help your cat own their territory, whatever that looks like for them. By watching and understanding your cat's individual personality and needs, you can support them in becoming confident and happy in your home.
