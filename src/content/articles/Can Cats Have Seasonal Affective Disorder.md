## How Seasonal Changes Affect Your Cat

As the days get shorter, your cat's behavior and mood can shift too. Cats are particularly susceptible to these changes because they're crepuscular animals, meaning they're naturally most active during sunup and sundown. This isn't random—their ancestors evolved to hunt when their prey was most active and when their eyes worked best, especially during the magic hour into sunset. When daylight becomes scarce in the fall and winter, your cat loses those peak activity windows and may become less active, sleep more, and withdraw socially.

Shorter days also affect your cat's mating behavior. In the wild, kitten season happens when there's more sun available, triggering increased activity and breeding in unspayed or unneutered cats. Even indoor cats respond to these light cycles.

## Signs Your Cat May Be Struggling

Watch for behavioral changes as the seasons shift. If your cat eats noticeably less over a prolonged period, sleeps constantly, or isolates itself from the family for an extended time, these are signs they need support. Some cats also overgroom due to anxiety during seasonal transitions. These aren't issues that will resolve on their own—they warrant attention.

If you notice these symptoms, talk to your vet or a holistic vet. They can suggest supplements or other options to help ease the transition and regulate your cat's circadian rhythms.

## Maintain Consistent Routines

One of the most helpful things you can do is keep your cat's schedule predictable. Cats are creatures of routine and don't adapt well to disruptions, so consistency reduces their anxiety. Start by feeding your cat at set times each day rather than leaving food out all day (free-feeding). Cats are opportunistic hunters by nature, and scheduled meals that mimic their natural hunting instinct—hunt, catch, kill—help regulate their behavior and mood.

If your cat is used to free-feeding, transition gradually to a meal schedule to avoid confusion. You can also try adjusting feeding times to coincide roughly with sunrise and sunset, which honors their crepuscular nature. Find a schedule that works for both you and your cat, since rigid routines that don't fit your life won't be sustainable.

## Boost Playtime and Enrichment

Active play is crucial during darker months. Playing with your cat stimulates their natural hunting behavior, provides mental stimulation, and helps regulate their circadian rhythm. Don't rely solely on passive enrichment like Cat TV—direct interaction with you matters most. Regular play sessions help combat the lethargy that can set in as daylight decreases.

## Maximize Natural Light

Windows are your cat's best friend during this time. Place their favorite items—treats, toys, or beds—near sunny windows to encourage them to spend time there and soak up available daylight. Bring favorite treats to window perches during the day so your cat associates that spot with positive experiences.

If natural light is limited where you live, consider using full spectrum lights for light therapy, similar to what people use. Having some light on in the house during seasonal transitions can genuinely help your cat adjust.

Remember, your cat is a creature of habit and doesn't cope well with sudden changes. Make adjustments thoughtfully, with compromise in mind for both you and your cat's wellbeing.
