## Understanding Your Cat as a Cat, Not a Dog

The number one mistake people make is not respecting who their cat actually is. This often happens because we view cats through "dog colored glasses"—but cats and dogs have completely different evolutionary histories and relationships with humans.

Dogs have been bred over 10,000 years to perform jobs and please their human companions, which is why they're highly motivated by human approval. Cats, on the other hand, developed a mutually beneficial relationship with humans: cats provided pest control, and humans offered food and shelter. This relationship only recently changed when cats began living indoors full-time.

Because cats don't have the same prime motivators as dogs, they aren't naturally driven to seek your approval the way a dog is. This difference is crucial to understand. When cat owners expect their cats to behave like dogs, they end up disappointed and frustrated. The key is to recognize and respect the unique characteristics and needs of your cat, rather than judging them by dog-human relationship standards.

## Litter Box Mistakes

Litter box problems are among the most common issues in cat care. There are several ways you can get this wrong.

**Placement matters enormously.** Don't put litter boxes in inconvenient or scary locations—behind a washing machine or in high-traffic areas where your cat might feel trapped or anxious are both problematic. Cats are territorial and don't naturally want to use the same spot every day, so placement near other pets or children can create problems. A safe, comfortable, accessible location is essential.

**Choose the right litter.** Heavily scented litter can be offensive to cats. Avoid litters with pointy particles that can hurt your cat's paws when they step on them. Look for litter that's comfortable and safe.

**Have enough boxes.** A general rule of thumb is one litter box per cat, plus one extra. This ensures each cat has a clean and accessible place to use the bathroom, especially in multi-cat households.

**Keep them clean.** Failing to clean the litter box regularly is a major mistake. Scoop the litter box daily and change the litter completely on a regular basis to keep the area clean and prevent unpleasant conditions.

**Avoid hiding litter boxes in furniture.** This can be problematic, especially in households with multiple pets or children, as it can lead to accidents when your cat is using the litter box. It's better to place them in a safe and accessible location.

## Cat-Proofing Your Home

Having a cat in the house is similar to having a toddler with unrestricted access to various areas. You need to cat-proof your home to prevent potential harm.

Start by checking for toxic plants. There is an extensive list of plants, flowers, and shrubs that can be poisonous to cats. Some plants like lilies can be potentially fatal if ingested, so verify whether the plants in your house are safe.

Also restrict access to dangerous areas, such as under reclining chairs where your cat could get caught, and keep cleaning supplies and medicine cabinets secured. Think about all the ways your curious cat could get into trouble and address those hazards.

## Kitten Socialization and Play Mistakes

Playing with kittens using your hands is a mistake with painful long-term consequences. When you do this—especially if you let them nurse on your fingers or bat at your hands—kittens learn to associate hands with toys. As they grow, they'll bite and scratch hands, even though it seemed cute when they were young.

Similarly, failing to socialize kittens with various types of humans is a significant mistake. Expose your kitten to men, women, kids, and teens so they become more comfortable and calm in different situations. This early socialization can help them handle potentially stressful experiences—like nail trimming—more gracefully and teach them to associate these experiences with positive outcomes.

Not getting kittens used to being in a carrier is another missed opportunity. Vet visits and travel are much more difficult if your cat hasn't learned to associate the carrier with a safe place. It's much easier to teach this when they're kittens than to deal with a stressed adult cat. The kitten stage is your best window to create positive associations with humans and new experiences that will benefit them for life.

## Regular Veterinary Care

Not taking your cat to the vet is a significant mistake, even if your cat seems perfectly fine. Regular vet visits are essential for your cat's health, and putting them off can be detrimental.

Getting cats into a carrier and to the vet can be traumatic, but avoiding it out of love can lead to neglecting your cat's health needs. To make this easier, expose your cat to the carrier and take short drives regularly. You can also take your cat out in a backpack or carrier on walks to help them become more comfortable with new experiences and reduce stress during vet visits.

Health changes can occur rapidly in cats, so being on top of their health is crucial. Regular health check-ups help you prevent or identify potential issues early on.

## Introducing Cats and Other Animals

Improper introductions between cats, or between dogs and cats, can backfire and have negative consequences.

Cats are territorial animals that can function well in groups with other cats, but introducing a new cat to an existing cat's territory can be problematic if not done properly. The same applies when introducing a dog to a cat. A slow, controlled introduction is essential to ensure both animals feel safe and secure and to prevent negative interactions like a dog chasing a cat.

When you introduce animals in a controlled manner, both animals can come to feel like they own the space, leading to a harmonious relationship. Take the extra steps needed to ensure a smooth introduction rather than rushing the process.

## Environmental Enrichment

Not providing environmental enrichment is a significant mistake that many cat owners make. Add features such as shelves, cat trees, and window space to increase your cat's perception of territory and keep them mentally stimulated.

Cats perceive volume from floor to ceiling, so adding vertical elements like cat trees and shelves creates a "cat's super highway" and reduces congestion, especially in multi-cat households. This gives each cat their own space and reduces territorial conflicts.

Allowing your cat to have ownership of territory is also important. Provide window space and "Cat TV" through a bird feeder outside the window so your cat can watch birds and feel engaged with the world.

Create a "base camp" or a safe space for your cat—such as a bedroom with familiar scents and belongings. This is especially helpful during stressful events like moving. Your base camp should include items that smell or signify ownership to your cat, such as litter boxes, beds, and blankets, to help them feel at home and secure.

## Proper Nutrition

Cats require proper nutrition, and many owners make critical mistakes in this area.

Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning they need to eat actual meat, not just representations of meat. They're also highly carb intolerant, so having lots of carbs in their diet is a big mistake. In the past, feeding cats high-quality wet or raw food was difficult and expensive, but now many quality brands are available, making it easier to provide your cat with the right food.

The key is to learn about what cats need to eat, what they thrive on, and what could hurt them in the long run. Take the time to educate yourself about cat nutrition so you can make informed decisions and provide the best possible care for your cat.

## Declawing

Declawing a cat is considered a significant mistake and is viewed as abusive and inhumane. The procedure involves amputating each digit at the first knuckle, which causes pain and changes the way your cat walks forever.

Declawing can lead to various negative consequences: increased chances of arthritis, erosion of your cat's confidence, litter box issues, and an increase in biting instead of scratching. It can also damage the relationship between you and your cat.

If you're concerned about your cat's claws, there are humane alternatives. You can trim their claws regularly or use nail caps, which are not inhumane and can be a viable solution for cats with claw problems. Mutilating your cat to save furniture or for any other reason is not acceptable. Educate yourself on the subject and make informed decisions about your cat's care.
