## Dirty Litter Boxes

Your cat's litter box is one of the most important things in your home—keep it clean. Cats have a natural instinct to bury their waste, but they've agreed to confine themselves to a small space for your sake. The least you can do is scoop the litter box every single day.

Make sure the box is large enough for your cat to turn around comfortably without having to dodge waste. A clean, spacious litter box is essential to your cat's health and happiness. If the box gets too dirty, your cat may decide to go somewhere else in your home instead.

## Loud Noises

Cats have one of the largest hearing ranges of any land mammal. While humans can hear up to about 20,000 hertz, cats can hear all the way up to 64,000 hertz. This means your cat is incredibly sensitive to high-pitched sounds like fire engine sirens, smoke alarms, and loud music that contains overtones you might not even notice.

Your cat's sensitive hearing evolved to help them hunt small prey like mice and crickets, which make high-pitched sounds. Be mindful of loud appliances and noises in your home, and try to protect your cat from unnecessary exposure to sounds that might distress them.

## Strong Smells

Your cat's sense of smell is extraordinarily powerful. They have between 20 to 200 million olfactory scent receptors in their nose, compared to your five million. This means scents that seem mild to you can be overwhelming to your cat.

Cats typically hate the smell of vinegar, onion, garlic, citrus, menthol, mint, and gasoline. Avoid using scented cat litter, as it can be aversive to most cats. If you want to keep your cat away from certain areas—like a Christmas tree—you can use small baggies with holes in them filled with lemon or grapefruit. The scent will discourage your cat from investigating without harming them.

## Changes to Routine

Cats are creatures of routine, and they thrive when their daily life has structure and consistency. Your cat's day is built around your patterns: when you wake up, when you go to work, when you come home, and when you have meals. Your cat pays close attention to these rhythms and the energy you bring to the day.

Even a short disruption—like being away for just two days—can cause real stress. Your cat might respond by wrecking the place, hiding, or acting out. This doesn't mean you need to live like a robot, but it does mean your cat prefers order and organization. They're essentially averse to chaos and disorder. When their routine gets disrupted, their whole sense of stability is shaken.

## Cold Temperatures

Your cat's body temperature runs around 101.5 degrees, which is higher than your 98.6 degrees. As descendants of desert animals, cats are accustomed to warmth and naturally seek out warm spots like sunny windows or heat-generating devices, especially in winter.

Cats conserve heat by curling into tight balls or finding small, enclosed spaces like boxes. Unlike you, they don't have sweat glands across their body—they can only regulate temperature through their paw pads. This makes them much more sensitive to cold. A rapid drop in body temperature can be problematic for your cat, so provide warm resting spots throughout your home.

## Dogs Without Boundaries

The idea that cats hate dogs is largely a myth. What cats actually dislike is certain dog behaviors—specifically, a lack of control and disrespect for personal space. Cats are control freaks who prefer order. Dogs, by contrast, can be chaotic and intrusive, invading a cat's space by suddenly approaching, smelling them, or trying to interact while they're using the litter box.

The real issue is territorial respect. Dogs often move fast and invade sleeping spots and other areas your cat considers theirs. If a dog can sit and stay while your cat walks around the room, it shows respect for the cat's territory. This respect is essential for the two animals to coexist harmoniously. When a dog respects your cat's boundaries, your cat is much more likely to tolerate the dog's presence.

## Car Rides

Your cat doesn't hate cars themselves—they hate the loss of control and the chaotic experience. A car ride combines everything cats dislike: being out of control, strange smells, unfamiliar visuals, and loud noises all at once.

The experience can feel even worse if your cat only associates car rides with trips to the vet. However, if you expose your cat to car rides when they're young and pair them with positive experiences, they can learn to tolerate or even enjoy the ride. Cats that are socialized to adventures—like walks or hikes—and regularly ride in cars to get there can become confident and calm passengers, and may even come to enjoy the experience.

## Costumes and Restrictive Items

Cats hate being dressed up in costumes because it takes away their sense of control. Something around their body that they can't remove feels confining and uncomfortable. Cats are fight-or-flight animals who value control over their own bodies and decisions. Anything that restricts their movement or autonomy goes against their nature and will cause stress.
