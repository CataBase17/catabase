## Cats Are Not Solitary Animals

Contrary to what many people believe, cats are not solitary creatures. They're actually social and community-minded animals that need the company of others. This is especially true for kittens, who require the presence of another kitten of the same age to learn and thrive. Kittens need to learn how to "speak cat" from other cats—skills that a human companion alone cannot fully provide.

Every kitten needs a buddy or best friend to learn proper social skills and understand what it means to be a cat. While you can certainly provide companionship and play, it's not enough to replace the need for a feline friend.

## The Critical Window for Social Development

Between two and nine weeks of age, kittens go through a critical phase of social development. During this time, they learn essential lessons by interacting with their littermates—playing, wrestling, and making up afterward. These experiences shape their behavior for the rest of their lives.

One of the most important things kittens learn is bite inhibition. Through play-fighting with littermates, they discover how hard they can bite and strike without causing real harm. This learned restraint is crucial because it teaches them to play nicely with you and other animals, rather than directing aggressive play at your ankles, wrists, or furniture.

When a kitten is raised alone without littermates, it can develop single kitten syndrome. These kittens may not learn proper bite inhibition and could direct their play aggression toward humans. They also miss out on the mental and physical stimulation that comes from having a playmate.

You can help by providing appropriate outlets like scratching posts and engaging in regular interactive play with toys. However, this can only partially make up for what a kitten companion would provide.

## Adopt Two Kittens Instead of One

When you're ready to bring home a kitten, consider adopting two instead of one. Having a buddy from the start leads to healthier and more stimulated cats. While it might seem like adopting two would be double the work, it's often actually half the work. The two kittens will exercise and stimulate each other mentally and physically, reducing how much entertainment and attention you need to provide.

Yes, there are additional upfront costs—food, vet bills, and supplies for two cats rather than one. But in the long run, two well-adjusted kittens with each other's company are less likely to develop behavioral problems caused by loneliness or boredom. They'll also have each other's company when you're away at work or running errands, which means less stress and a happier household overall.
